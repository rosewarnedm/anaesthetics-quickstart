# Chapter 12: Human Factors, Ethics and Consent

Most anaesthetic harm stems not from knowledge gaps but from system failures and human performance. Your non-technical skills, ethical judgement, and consent practice are as important as your pharmacology.

## Human Factors Essentials

### The Core Idea

Humans make errors. Good systems expect this and trap mistakes before they reach patients. Think of Reason's Swiss cheese model: hazards only cause harm when holes in multiple defences align. Your job is to add layers — checklists, culture, equipment design, communication.

### Error Types and Why They Matter

- **Slips and lapses** — attention or memory failures (wrong syringe, missed step). Mitigated by standardisation, labelling, checklists.
- **Mistakes** — wrong plan (e.g. treating laryngospasm as bronchospasm). Mitigated by cross-checks and cognitive aids.
- **Violations** — deliberate shortcuts. Mitigated by culture and making the safe route the easy route.

Recognise which type has occurred; the fix differs.

### Non-Technical Skills (ANTS)

These are formally assessed in the RCoA curriculum. Practise them deliberately.

- **Situational awareness**: gather information → understand it → anticipate what comes next. Scan monitors, the patient, and the environment continuously. If something feels wrong, say so.
- **Decision-making**: under pressure, use rehearsed rules and cognitive aids. Set explicit time-checks: "if no improvement in two minutes, I will change plan."
- **Communication**: closed-loop, brief, name the person. Confirm understanding. Avoid jargon with patients.
- **Teamwork**: allocate roles out loud, state the plan, invite concerns, acknowledge challenges.

### Cognitive Load and Fixation

Stress narrows attention. You will fixate. Protect against it:

- Use cognitive aids for emergencies (airway, anaphylaxis, LAST) — keep them visible, not buried in a drawer.
- Apply a "sterile cockpit" during induction and crises: stop non-essential conversation and tasks.
- Verbalise saturations and time elapsed; set a timer for apnoea.
- Call for help **early** — before you are overwhelmed, not after.

### Briefings and Debriefings

- Complete the **WHO Surgical Safety Checklist** every case, every time.
- Run a brief pre-list check: plan, kit, allergies, VTE, blood, special risks.
- **"STOP Before You Block"**: confirm patient, procedure, side, site, consent, anticoagulation, and monitoring before every regional technique.
- Debrief after significant events and at list end. Even two minutes asking "what went well, what would we change?" drives learning and supports wellbeing.

### Speaking Up

Use graded assertiveness if you have a safety concern — start with a question, escalate to a statement, be explicit if needed. If still unsafe, stop the process. Thank people who raise concerns; this makes it easier next time.

### Handover

Structure every handover (SBAR is a reliable framework). Minimise interruptions. Cover as a minimum:

- Procedure performed; airway management and grade
- Analgesia plan and last doses given
- Antiemetics; fluids and blood products
- Lines, drains, catheters
- Oxygen requirements and ventilation
- Regional blocks — technique and expected wear-off time
- Allergies; antibiotics
- Relevant comorbidities; escalation plan
- Any critical intraoperative events

A poor handover is a direct patient safety risk. Write it down.

### Environment and Equipment

- Standardise your workspace. Keep the drug preparation area uncluttered.
- **Monitoring**: follow Association of Anaesthetists standards. Capnography is mandatory whenever a breathing system is attached and for all intubated patients.
- **Alarms**: on, audible, set appropriately. Never silence an alarm without understanding it.
- **Syringe safety**: label immediately after drawing up using colour-coded labels; two-person check for high-risk drugs; separate look-alike ampoules physically.
- Use NRFit and other safety connectors correctly — they exist to prevent misconnections.

### Fatigue, Stress, and Performance

Fatigue impairs vigilance, calculation, and empathy. Recognise it in yourself. Plan breaks, eat, hydrate. On nights, anticipate microsleeps and avoid solo high-risk procedures when exhausted. If staffing is unsafe, escalate — this is a patient safety issue, not a personal complaint.

Stress signals: irritability, tunnel thinking, task-shedding. Pause, breathe, prioritise the most critical task, and ask for help.

### Incident Reporting

Report near-misses and safety events locally. The purpose is system improvement, not blame. Engage with governance processes. Apply **duty of candour** when harm occurs: be open with the patient and family, explain what happened, apologise, describe next steps, document, and involve seniors and governance.

## Ethics in Anaesthesia

### The Four Principles in Practice

- **Autonomy**: respect informed choices, including refusals you disagree with.
- **Beneficence**: act in the patient's interest.
- **Non-maleficence**: weigh risk against benefit; avoid disproportionate harm.
- **Justice**: fair access, avoid bias, steward resources responsibly.

These principles frequently conflict. Recognise the tension and discuss with seniors.

### Shared Decision-Making and Material Risk

Following *Montgomery*, you must disclose risks that a reasonable person in **this patient's position** would consider significant, plus reasonable alternatives including doing nothing. Tailor the discussion to their values, occupation, concerns, and prior experience. Avoid paternalism. Your role is to support a decision, not make it for them.

### Confidentiality

Share patient information on a strict need-to-know basis within the clinical team. No identifiable information in public spaces, messaging apps, or social media. Obtain explicit consent before using images for teaching.

### Teaching and Supervision

Patients are entitled to know if a trainee may perform their procedure under supervision. Your supervision level must match your competence. If asked to work beyond your competence, say so and seek appropriate support.

### Fairness and Resources

ICU beds, theatre time, and blood products are finite. Contribute to reasoned, documented, multidisciplinary decisions. Escalate resource constraints early — never silently accept an unsafe situation.

## Consent: Practical Basics

### Elements of Valid Consent

Valid consent requires:
1. **Capacity** — decision-specific and time-specific; assume it unless clearly absent.
2. **Voluntariness** — free from coercion.
3. **Information** — material risks, benefits, alternatives, and what recovery involves.

All three must be present.

### Timing and Setting

Do not consent patients on the trolley in the anaesthetic room unless it is a genuine emergency. Avoid consenting someone who is sedated, in uncontrolled pain, or distressed. Provide written information where possible and allow time for questions.

### Documentation

Record:
- Options discussed and material risks relevant to this individual
- Patient's priorities, questions, and understanding
- Leaflets given; interpreter or chaperone used
- Who obtained consent

Document specific individual risks (e.g. dental risk with loose teeth; nerve injury with regional). Use a written consent form per local policy for general anaesthesia, regional anaesthesia, sedation, and invasive procedures.

### Capacity and the Mental Capacity Act

**Support capacity first**: plain language, visual aids, hearing and vision support, interpreters, adequate time.

**Functional test** — can the person:
- Understand the information?
- Retain it long enough to decide?
- Weigh it to reach a decision?
- Communicate that decision?

If capacity is lacking, make a **best-interests decision**: involve those who know the patient, consider their known wishes and values, and choose the least restrictive option. Respect valid **advance decisions to refuse treatment** and **lasting power of attorney** for health decisions.

In a life-threatening emergency where consent cannot be obtained, treat to save life or prevent serious deterioration while seeking information. Document your reasoning.

### Children and Young People

- Age 16–17: presumed to have capacity.
- Under 16: may be Gillick competent — assess individually.
- Those with parental responsibility can consent for a non-competent child. The child's best interests always prevail.
- Competent minors who refuse treatment in life-threatening situations may, in rare circumstances, be overridden through legal routes. Seek senior and legal advice immediately.

### Sedation and Consent

Sedation impairs capacity — obtain consent beforehand. Discuss the goals of sedation, alternatives (including no sedation), monitoring, and the possibility of deepening to general anaesthesia. Ensure trained staff and equipment for airway rescue are immediately available.

### Regional Anaesthesia and Invasive Procedures

Regional consent is separate from surgical consent. Cover:
- Benefits (analgesia, reduced systemic side effects)
- Alternatives, including general anaesthesia
- Common and serious risks: failure, block spread, nerve injury, vascular injury, infection, LAST, potential conversion to general anaesthesia
- Mark the site; complete "STOP Before You Block"

Wrong-site block is a Never Event.

### Blood and Blood-Sparing Strategies

Discuss transfusion where clinically relevant: indications, risks, benefits, and alternatives (cell salvage, pharmacological agents, permissive strategies). For patients who decline transfusion (e.g. Jehovah's Witnesses), explore accepted alternatives clearly, document in detail, and involve the transfusion team early.

### Language and Access

Use a **professional interpreter**, not a family member, wherever possible. Allow extra time. Verify understanding with teach-back.

### DNACPR and Perioperative Resuscitation Decisions

DNACPR means do not attempt cardiopulmonary resuscitation — it does **not** mean do not treat. Discuss perioperative plans early. A time-limited modification of the DNACPR for the perioperative period may be appropriate; follow national guidance, communicate clearly with the patient, family, and all teams, and document thoroughly.

### When a Patient Refuses Recommended Care

Explore the reasons. Confirm capacity. Provide balanced information. Respect an informed refusal — this is a legal and ethical right. Offer a second opinion. Document everything carefully.

## Special Scenarios You'll Meet Early

- **Confused elderly patient for emergency laparotomy**: rapid capacity assessment; involve family; best-interests decision; prioritise life-saving care; document.
- **Intoxicated trauma patient**: likely lacks capacity acutely; treat in best interests; revisit consent when sober for subsequent procedures.
- **Aggressive or delirious patient**: use least-restrictive measures; if sedation or restraint is needed, establish the legal basis (MCA best interests or relevant mental health legislation) and document.
- **Perioperative DNACPR**: clarify which interventions are appropriate intra- and postoperatively; communicate to all teams.
- **Trainee-performed neuraxial procedure**: disclose supervision level to the patient; ensure the supervising consultant is immediately available; follow local policy.
- **Research or audit**: only recruit patients with appropriate approvals and informed consent; deferred consent may apply in emergencies — follow the approved protocol.

## Safe Defaults
