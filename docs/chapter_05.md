# Chapter 5: Airway Anatomy and the Difficult Airway

One principle overrides everything else in this chapter: **oxygenation is non-negotiable; intubation is not**. Every decision you make at the airway should be filtered through that lens.

## Core Airway Anatomy

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


### Surface Landmarks

You need to be able to identify these by palpation, not just on a diagram:

- Hyoid bone — sits above the thyroid cartilage
- Thyroid notch — the V-shaped notch at the top of the thyroid cartilage
- Cricoid cartilage — the complete ring below it
- **Cricothyroid membrane (CTM)** — the soft depression between them; your emergency front-of-neck access (eFONA) site

**Palpate the CTM on every patient where difficulty is anticipated, before induction.** In a crisis you will not find what you have never located.

### Upper Airway Regions

- **Nose:** warms and humidifies air; turbinates bleed readily if instrumented roughly
- **Oral cavity:** the tongue is the commonest cause of obstruction under anaesthesia
- **Pharynx:** a collapsible tube that depends on muscle tone; tone disappears with anaesthesia
- **Larynx:** epiglottis → glottis (vocal cords) → arytenoids; sits between thyroid and cricoid cartilages
- **Trachea:** supported by C-shaped rings; right main bronchus is more vertical — endobronchial intubation goes right by default

### Why the CTM Matters

The CTM is an avascular ligament. It is your **scalpel–bougie–tube** site in a cannot-intubate, cannot-oxygenate (CICO) emergency. Find it before you need it.

Cricoid pressure occludes the oesophagus to reduce regurgitation risk during RSI, but it can worsen the laryngoscopic view and impair ventilation. **Release it if it is hindering oxygenation or laryngoscopy.**

### Airway Innervation — What You Need to Know

| Territory | Sensory nerve | Clinical relevance |
|---|---|---|
| Oropharynx | Glossopharyngeal | Gag reflex |
| Supraglottis | Internal superior laryngeal (vagus) | Laryngospasm trigger |
| Infraglottis and trachea | Recurrent laryngeal (vagus) | Cough trigger |

Topical anaesthesia for awake intubation targets these territories in sequence. Stimulating the supraglottic larynx at a light plane of anaesthesia triggers laryngospasm; stimulating below the cords causes coughing.

### Paediatric Differences

Children are not small adults. Key differences:

- Large occiput flexes the neck in supine — use a shoulder roll to restore a neutral or slightly extended position
- Large tongue and floppy, angled epiglottis make visualisation harder
- High oxygen consumption and low functional residual capacity (FRC) — they desaturate **fast**
- Small airway lumen — a little oedema causes a lot of resistance

Experienced help early, meticulous preoxygenation, and gentle ventilation are essential in children.

## Physiology That Matters

### Why Oxygenation Fails

Anaesthesia abolishes pharyngeal dilator tone — the airway collapses at its softest point, the oropharynx. Muscle relaxants remove laryngospasm but also remove what little tone remains.

Induction reduces FRC and promotes atelectasis, compressing the oxygen reserve at exactly the moment you need it most.

### Preoxygenation

The goal is to replace nitrogen in the FRC with oxygen, maximising your apnoea time.

- Tight mask seal, high inspired oxygen, patient breathing normally, mouth closed with jaw support
- **Head-up or ramped position** increases FRC and reduces aspiration risk — use it routinely, especially in obesity and pregnancy
- Add PEEP/CPAP, and consider nasal high-flow oxygen for apnoeic oxygenation in higher-risk patients
- End-tidal oxygen concentration is a better measure of denitrogenation than a fixed time target — use it where available

Do not cut corners here. Good preoxygenation buys you the seconds that matter.

## Airway Assessment

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


### History First

- Any previous difficult airway or anaesthetic record? Ask specifically and chase the notes.
- Stridor, voice change, dysphagia — symptoms of compromise
- OSA, obesity, pregnancy, reflux — risk factors for difficulty and aspiration
- Head and neck surgery or radiotherapy, rheumatoid arthritis, ankylosing spondylitis — fixed or worsening restriction

### Examination

Look at the whole patient before opening the mouth:

- Mandibular size (retrognathia makes laryngoscopy harder)
- Neck habitus and mobility
- Beard (mask seal problem)

Then assess:

- Mouth opening
- Jaw protrusion (can upper incisors advance beyond lower? — a proxy for atlanto-occipital extension)
- Mallampati score — tongue size relative to the oropharyngeal space
- Thyromental distance — space for the tongue during laryngoscopy
- Dentition and nasal patency

**No single test reliably predicts difficulty.** Combine findings. If you think "this might be difficult", plan as if it will be.

### Predictive Mnemonics — Know These

Use these to assess difficulty across all four possible failure points:

- **MOANS** — difficult *mask ventilation*: Mask seal (beard), Obesity/OSA, Age, No teeth, Snoring/stiff lungs
- **RODS** — difficult *supraglottic airway*: Restricted mouth opening, Obstruction/Obesity, Disrupted airway, Stiff lungs
- **LEMON** — difficult *laryngoscopy/intubation*: Look externally, Evaluate 3-3-2 relationships, Mallampati, Obstruction/Obesity, Neck mobility
- **SHORT** — difficult *front-of-neck access*: Surgery/scarring, Haematoma/infection, Obesity, Radiation, Tumour/distortion

## Planning and Preparation

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


### The DAS Framework: Plans A–D

Before every general anaesthetic, make a stepwise plan and **say it out loud** to your team:

- **Plan A:** Optimised intubation attempt(s)
- **Plan B:** Supraglottic airway (SGA) for oxygenation and ventilation
- **Plan C:** Face mask ventilation, or wake the patient
- **Plan D:** Emergency front-of-neck access (eFONA)

The key discipline is limiting attempts. **Stop repeating what has failed.** Change something meaningful between each attempt — device, blade, operator, position, adjunct — or move to the next plan.

Call for help early. It is far easier to stand down help you do not need than to summon it in crisis.

### Equipment — Have It All Ready Before You Start

- Facemask with PEEP valve
- Oropharyngeal (OPA) and nasopharyngeal (NPA) airways in appropriate sizes
- 2nd-generation SGA (e.g. i-gel or LMA Supreme) — better seal and gastric channel than 1st-generation devices
- Direct laryngoscope *and* a video laryngoscope (VL) with appropriate stylet for the blade type
- Bougie
- Cuffed endotracheal tube with cuff pressure monitoring
- Working suction (Yankauer)
- eFONA kit — scalpel, bougie, cuffed tube — with CTM already identified

This is not optional. The time to find missing equipment is not during a difficult airway.

## Basic Techniques Done Well

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


### Mask Ventilation

- Position matters first: head-up, chin lift, jaw thrust
- Use a two-handed "VE grip" if one hand is insufficient — ask for help early
- OPA and/or NPA as adjuncts; PEEP to prevent pharyngeal collapse
- Gentle pressure to reduce gastric insufflation
- Beard causing poor seal? Gel or a transparent dressing over it before you start

### Supraglottic Airways

Prefer 2nd-generation devices for rescue ventilation. Insert with jaw lift, confirm with capnography and chest movement. An SGA can be a bridge to definitive airway management or a final destination depending on the clinical situation.

### Tracheal Intubation

- Position and preoxygenate well before the first attempt — this is your best opportunity
- VL improves glottic *view* but does not guarantee easy intubation; ensure you have the correct stylet for a hyperangulated blade
- External laryngeal manipulation (BURP or bimanual) improves the view — have an assistant maintain it
- A bougie is invaluable with a partial view; tracheal clicks and hold-up at the carina are supportive but not infallible signs
- **Confirm with sustained waveform capnography.** Six breaths of CO₂ waveform. Absent capnography after intubation means oesophageal placement until proved otherwise — act immediately
- Check depth and listen for bilateral air entry to exclude right mainstem intubation

### Rapid Sequence Induction (RSI)

RSI is indicated when aspiration risk is elevated. Key points:

- Plan the whole airway strategy *before* you give drugs
- Cricoid pressure if trained; release it if it impedes oxygenation or laryngoscopy
- Oxygenation takes priority over technique dogma — if saturations fall, ventilate gently
- 2nd-generation SGA immediately available for rescue

## The Difficult Airway: Strategies

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


### Predicted Difficult Intubation

If you anticipate difficulty, consider whether you should:

1. **Avoid general anaesthesia** — use regional or neuraxial where appropriate
2. **Perform awake tracheal intubation (ATI)** — indicated when both intubation and ventilation may be difficult, or when aspiration risk is high
3. **Plan elective tracheostomy** for critical upper airway obstruction — involve ENT early

### Awake Tracheal Intubation — Principles

The DAS ATI guidelines are the reference. Core principles:

- Patient cooperation and thorough topical anaesthesia are the foundation
- Oxygen throughout — nasal and/or facemask
- Sedation is optional and must be titrated cautiously with full monitoring; oversedation in a compromised airway is dangerous
- Suction ready; airway rescue plan in place before you start
- Know the technique you are using; limit attempts; stop and reassess if failing
- Have a clear plan including eFONA if the patient deteriorates

### Unanticipated Difficult Intubation

- After a failed first attempt: **pause, optimise, call for help**
- Announce the plan and maximum number of attempts to the team
- Escalate through your planned adjuncts (VL, bougie, different operator)
- If intubation fails but oxygenation is maintained: insert 2nd-generation SGA, then decide — proceed via SGA, attempt fibreoptic intubation through the SGA, or **wake the patient**
- If you cannot intubate **and** cannot oxygenate: **declare CICO, proceed to eFONA without delay**

### Emergency Front-of-Neck Access (eFONA)

The DAS-recommended technique for anaesthetists is **scalpel–bougie–tube**:

1. Announce CICO. Get help. Position the patient and apply oxygen as best you can.
2. Identify the CTM — vertical midline soft depression between thyroid and cricoid cartilages.
3. Make a bold horizontal stab incision through skin and membrane. Stay in the midline.
4. Insert the bougie caudally into the trachea.
5. Railroad a small cuffed tube over the bougie. Inflate cuff, ventilate, confirm with capnography.

This is a skill that degrades without practice. Train on simulators regularly. After eFONA: ventilate gently, secure the airway, and involve ENT and ICU for definitive management.

## Special Situations

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


### Obesity and OSA

Low FRC, high oxygen consumption, rapid desaturation — a combination that compresses your safety margin severely.

- Ramp the patient (ear level with the sternal notch)
- Aggressive preoxygenation with PEEP/CPAP and nasal oxygenation
- VL as default; low threshold to wake the patient if not progressing
- Extubate fully awake, sitting up, with adjuncts available

### Pregnancy

Oedematous airway, high aspiration risk, rapid desaturation, and aortocaval compression all combine to make obstetric airway management high stakes.

- Left uterine displacement and ramped position
- Maximise preoxygenation with PEEP/CPAP
- Have a senior colleague immediately available; follow your unit's failed intubation pathway
- 2nd-generation SGA as rescue if intubation fails — know the pathway before you start

### Cervical Spine Concern

- Manual in-line stabilisation throughout; avoid neck movement
- Remove the front of the collar with manual stabilisation maintained — collars restrict mouth opening
- VL is preferred; consider awake techniques

### Upper Airway Pathology (Tumour, Infection, Stridor)

- Do not destroy your safety margin. Awake techniques are usually correct.
- Involve ENT before you start — you may need an emergency tracheostomy
- Avoid sedation that risks tipping partial obstruction to complete obstruction

### Facial Trauma and Active Bleeding

Blood and vomit in the airway kill quickly. Suction early and often.

- Avoid nasal routes with suspected base of skull fracture
- Awake techniques if the patient is cooperative, but often not feasible in trauma
- SGA to maintain oxygenation if mask seal is impossible; be ready for eFONA

## Aspiration: Prevention and Management

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


- Risk-stratify every patient; fasting status does not guarantee an empty stomach
- RSI when aspiration risk is elevated
- Head-up position and suction within reach
- **If regurgitation occurs:** head-down and lateral position if possible, suction the upper airway, secure the airway promptly, oxygenate
- Inform the team, document clearly, consider critical care involvement, and postpone non-urgent surgery
- Complete an incident report

## Extubation Is an Airway Procedure

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


The DAS extubation guidelines make this explicit. Treat extubation as seriously as induction.

- Risk-stratify before you start; plan extubation as you planned intubation
- Extubate when fully awake, obeying commands, with neuromuscular block reversed and confirmed
- Oxygen and suction ready; consider an airway exchange catheter for high-risk cases
- Post-extubation CPAP or oxygen support for OSA/obesity
- Have a reintubation plan — do not walk away from a marginal airway

## Pitfalls to Know and Avoid

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


- **Chasing the tube while the saturations fall.** Stop. Oxygenate.
- **Repeating the same failed attempt.** Change something meaningful or change plan.
- **Poor positioning before laryngoscopy.** Fix it first — it costs seconds and gains minutes.
- **Inadequate preoxygenation.** Take the time. It buys you the time you will need.
- **Underusing simple adjuncts** — OPA, NPA, two-hand mask grip, PEEP.
- **Wrong equipment for the VL blade** — hyperangulated blades need a pre-shaped stylet.
- **Maintaining cricoid pressure despite harm** — release it if it is causing problems.
- **Oesophageal intubation** — absent capnography after intubation means remove the tube now.
- **Laryngospasm** — avoid airway stimulation at a light plane; treat promptly with jaw thrust, oxygen, deepening anaesthesia, or succinylcholine per local guidance.
- **Airway oedema after prolonged prone or head-down cases** — perform a cuff-leak test before extubation and be ready to delay if concerned.
