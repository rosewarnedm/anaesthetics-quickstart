## Pre-Use Setup

Complete the full pre-use machine check before every list. Verify pipeline pressures, confirm a full back-up oxygen cylinder, and check battery power is available.

Warm up and zero gas analysers. Fit a fresh water trap and sampling line. Trust the oxygen analyser over pipeline colour-coding.

Attach standard monitoring before induction:
- ECG
- Pulse oximetry
- NIBP (frequent cycling)
- Capnography — connected as soon as an airway device is placed
- FiO₂ and volatile agent monitoring

## ECG

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


The ECG detects rhythm, rate, and ischaemia trends. Prepare skin properly: dry it, clip hair if needed, avoid bony prominences.

**Common pitfalls:**
- **Diathermy artefact** — maximise the filter, ensure proper earthing, separate ECG cables from diathermy leads
- **Lead reversal** — unexpected inverted complexes; recheck placement
- **Poor trace** — replace electrodes and re-prep skin
- **Paced rhythms** — some monitors miscount; correlate with the pulse or arterial waveform

ST analysis is useful but artefact is common. Use it as a trend, not in isolation.

## Pulse Oximetry (SpO₂)

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


The probe uses red and infrared light to distinguish oxygenated from deoxygenated haemoglobin. It requires pulsatile flow. The plethysmography waveform gives you beat-to-beat perfusion information — a good-quality waveform means the reading is reliable.

**Common pitfalls:**
- **Poor perfusion** (cold, shocked, vasoconstrictors) — warm the limb, reposition the probe, consider a forehead or ear probe, or insert an arterial line
- **Motion artefact** — secure the probe, switch sites
- **Nail varnish or acrylics** — remove or rotate the probe 90°
- **Methaemoglobinaemia** — tends to push SpO₂ towards a fixed mid-range value regardless of true saturation
- **Carboxyhaemoglobinaemia** — overestimates SpO₂; if carbon monoxide poisoning is suspected, send a co-oximetry sample

If SpO₂ falls with a good-quality plethysmograph: treat hypoxia. Check the airway, ventilation, FiO₂, circuit, and lungs.

## Non-Invasive Blood Pressure (NIBP)

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


The cuff detects oscillations in the arterial wall during deflation. Use the correct cuff size — too small overestimates, too large underestimates. Position the cuff at heart level.

**Common pitfalls:**
- **Arrhythmias** — oscillometric devices are unreliable; consider an arterial line
- **Surgical site or IV in the same limb** — use a different limb
- **AV fistula or lymphoedema** — avoid that limb
- **Persistent failure to read** — check connections, warm the limb, reduce movement

Cycle frequently during induction and periods of instability.

## Capnography (ETCO₂)

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Capnography is mandatory for all intubated patients and during TIVA (Association of Anaesthetists and DAS guidance). It confirms tracheal placement, detects disconnection, reflects cardiac output, and warns of rebreathing.

Connect capnography before positive pressure ventilation. Confirm a sustained, normal waveform after intubation **before** fixing the tube.

**Normal waveform:** square shape, rapid upstroke, plateau (phase III), return to baseline on inspiration.

**Abnormal patterns:**
- **Rising baseline** — rebreathing (exhausted absorbent, insufficient fresh gas flow)
- **Slanted "shark-fin" upstroke** — bronchospasm or airway obstruction
- **Curare cleft** — spontaneous respiratory effort against partial paralysis

**Common pitfalls:**

| Problem | Likely causes | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Sudden loss to zero | Oesophageal intubation, disconnection, kinked sampling line, cardiac arrest | Check tube position, circuit, chest movement, pulse. Hand-ventilate 100% O₂. Call for help. |
| Low ETCO₂ with normal ventilation | Low cardiac output, PE, air embolism, hypothermia | Treat the cause |
| Rising ETCO₂ | Hypoventilation, sepsis, MH, exhausted absorbent, scavenging obstruction, CO₂ insufflation | Increase minute ventilation; address mechanical causes |
| Water in sampling line | Condensation | Replace water trap and line; use HME filter |

## Gas Monitoring: FiO₂ and Volatile Agent

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


The **oxygen analyser** is a safety-critical, independent monitor. Set a low FiO₂ alarm. If pipeline failure or cross-contamination is suspected: open the cylinder, disconnect the suspect pipeline, hand-ventilate with a reservoir bag, and escalate.

**Volatile agent monitoring** confirms delivery and protects against awareness. Cross-check the vaporiser dial against the end-tidal reading.

If agent concentration drops unexpectedly: check the vaporiser is switched on, contains the correct agent, is filled, and is properly seated. Check the sampling line and circuit for leaks.

**Desiccated CO₂ absorbent** can degrade volatile agents and produce carbon monoxide. Change absorbent according to local policy.

Avoid using the **oxygen flush** carelessly — it bypasses the vaporiser (diluting the anaesthetic) and the APL valve (risking barotrauma). Never activate it during ventilator inspiration.

| Pattern | Likely cause | Action |
|---|---|---|
| High peak, normal plateau | Increased resistance — kink, secretions, bronchospasm, biting, blocked HME | Suction, straighten circuit, bite block, deepen anaesthesia, bronchodilator |
| High peak and plateau | Reduced compliance — pneumothorax, pneumoperitoneum, position, pulmonary oedema | Call for help; assess lungs and haemodynamics |
| Low pressure / low volume | Disconnection, deflated cuff, open suction port | Reconnect, re-inflate cuff, close ports |

In paediatrics, sidestream capnography sampling can steal a significant proportion of tidal volume. Use paediatric settings, low-dead-space components, or mainstream sensors.

## Temperature

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Heat loss is inevitable under anaesthesia. Hypothermia increases bleeding, infection risk, cardiac arrhythmias, and slows drug metabolism.

Start active warming early — before it is needed, not after the patient is cold.

Core temperature probes (oesophageal, nasopharyngeal, bladder) are more accurate than skin probes. Oesophageal probes placed too proximally read falsely low. Confirm trends rather than relying on a single reading.

Rising temperature is a red flag — consider infection, transfusion reaction, or malignant hyperthermia.

## Neuromuscular Monitoring

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Use a peripheral nerve stimulator whenever neuromuscular blocking agents are given. The train-of-four (TOF) is the standard assessment. Stimulate the ulnar nerve and observe the thumb (adductor pollicis).

Quantitative monitoring is preferred where available (RCoA curriculum). Do not rely on clinical signs alone to confirm adequate reversal — residual paralysis is under-detected clinically.

Document neuromuscular recovery before extubation. Confirm acceptable TOF ratios per local policy.

**Pitfalls:** poor electrode contact gives unreliable results (re-prep skin); hypothermia alters responses; volatile agents influence fade. Facial nerve responses reflect conditions at the airway — useful for timing intubation but not for assessing reversal.

Processed EEG indices (e.g. BIS) may reduce awareness risk, particularly during TIVA when there is no end-tidal agent to guide dosing.

Use as an adjunct — not a sole indicator. Neuromuscular blockade suppresses EMG and can lower the index without increasing depth of anaesthesia. Diathermy causes artefact. Interpret alongside haemodynamics, movement, and agent delivery.

Ensure good electrode contact and calibration before induction.

## Invasive Arterial Monitoring

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


**Setup:** prime and de-air the circuit completely. Level the transducer at the right atrium (mid-axillary line). Zero to air. Perform a fast-flush (square-wave) test to assess the dynamic response.

**Damping:**
- **Overdamped** — slurred upstroke, absent dicrotic notch; underestimates systolic pressure. Remove air bubbles, shorten tubing.
- **Underdamped** — ringing/overshoot; overestimates systolic pressure. Check for excessively long, stiff tubing.

**Key safety points:**
- Re-level the transducer after repositioning the patient — a mislevelled transducer introduces hydrostatic error
- Label arterial lines clearly; secure connections to prevent inadvertent disconnection and haemorrhage
- Maintain a pressurised flush bag and aseptic technique for sampling

Zero and level CVP lines as for arterial lines. Interpret CVP as a trend alongside the clinical picture — it is heavily influenced by ventilation, PEEP, and ventricular compliance.

Advanced devices (oesophageal Doppler, pulse contour analysis) provide additional haemodynamic data. Know your local equipment and its limitations. Numbers guide decisions; they do not make them.

## Scavenging and CO₂ Absorbent

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


**Scavenging:** obstruction or misconnection causes inadvertent PEEP — rising airway pressure and ETCO₂. Remove the obstruction and reassess immediately.

**CO₂ absorbent:** exhaustion causes rebreathing and rising inspired CO₂. A working canister feels warm. Desiccated absorbent may feel cool and look dusty, and can degrade volatile agents to produce carbon monoxide. Change according to local protocol.

## Common Troubleshooting Scenarios

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


**No capnography after intubation**
Check tube depth, auscultate, confirm the sampling line is connected and the water trap is seated. Exclude oesophageal intubation. Hand-ventilate with 100% oxygen and call for help. Follow DAS guidance.

**Sudden fall in ETCO₂**
Consider disconnection, low cardiac output, pulmonary or air embolism, or hyperventilation. Correlate with the pulse and arterial trace. Treat the cause.

**Rising ETCO₂ and airway pressures**
Hypoventilation, bronchospasm, rebreathing, scavenging obstruction, exhausted absorbent, or CO₂ insufflation. Increase minute ventilation, fix mechanical causes, liaise with the surgeon.

**Low SpO₂**
Check probe position and pleth quality. Assess the airway and lungs. Increase FiO₂. Warm the limb. Send an arterial blood gas if there is a persistent discrepancy with the clinical picture.

**NIBP failing repeatedly**
Reposition or resize the cuff. Use a different limb. If haemodynamically unstable or the rhythm is irregular, insert an arterial line.

**Agent monitor reads zero**
Vaporiser off, empty, wrong agent, or not properly seated. Check the sampling line. Supplement anaesthesia immediately if in doubt. Cross-check with haemodynamics and processed EEG if available.

**High airway pressure alarm**
Check for kinks, secretions, biting, bronchospasm, tube against the carina, or patient–ventilator dyssynchrony. Switch to manual ventilation to feel compliance. Suction, adjust the tube, deepen anaesthesia, give bronchodilator as appropriate.

**Low pressure / low volume alarm**
Disconnection, deflated cuff, open circuit valve. Reconnect, re-inflate, close ports.

**Water in the sampling line**
Replace the line and water trap. Position the trap below the patient. Use HME filters to reduce condensate.

**Power or gas failure**
Switch to battery. Open the oxygen cylinder. Disconnect a suspect pipeline. Hand-ventilate with a reservoir bag. Convert to TIVA if volatile delivery is lost, with appropriate monitoring. Call for help and follow your local major incident protocol.

## Special Environments and Populations

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


**Paediatrics:** small tidal volumes and high respiratory rates amplify every monitoring error. Sidestream sampling steals a larger proportional volume — use paediatric or mainstream sensors. Minimise added dead space.

**Obstetrics:** position carefully to avoid aortocaval compression. Monitor blood pressure frequently. Movement artefact on ECG is common — ensure good electrode adhesion.

**Remote sites (MRI, endoscopy, interventional radiology):** use MR-conditional equipment where required. Long circuits increase dead space and resistance. Maintain capnography for deep sedation and any patient with an artificial airway, in line with Association of Anaesthetists guidance.

**Transfer and recovery:** maintain anaesthetic-standard monitoring throughout intra- and inter-hospital transfer. Ensure adequate battery life, oxygen supply, and continuous capnography for intubated patients. Hand over trends and key events to recovery staff.
