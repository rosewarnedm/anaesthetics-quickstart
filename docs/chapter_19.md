# Chapter 19: Drug Preparation and Dosing Calculations

Getting the arithmetic right keeps patients safe. Most preventable anaesthetic harm involves the wrong drug, dose, route, or concentration. Build habits that make errors hard and correct practice automatic.

## Core Concepts You Must Own

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


### Units and Conversions

Know these without thinking:

- 1 g = 1000 mg = 1,000,000 microgram
- 1 L = 1000 mL
- Percentage solutions (% w/v): **1% = 10 mg/mL**. So 0.5% = 5 mg/mL, 2% = 20 mg/mL.
- Ratios are a known source of error — convert immediately to mg/mL: 1:1000 = 1 mg/mL; 1:10,000 = 0.1 mg/mL.
- Always write **microgram** in full on labels. Never μg or ug.

### Concentration and Dilution

Use **C₁V₁ = C₂V₂** whenever you dilute a stock solution.

- C₁ = stock concentration; V₁ = volume of stock needed; C₂ = target concentration; V₂ = final volume.
- Convert all units to the same scale before calculating.
- After calculating, ask: *is this a plausible volume?* A ten-fold error is the classic trap — usually a microgram/mg confusion.

### Which Weight to Use

This matters for dosing accuracy:

- **Hydrophilic drugs and most neuromuscular blockers**: dose towards ideal body weight (IBW).
- **Lipophilic agents (propofol, opioids)**: initial bolus towards lean body weight; titrate thereafter.
- **Suxamethonium**: often dosed closer to total body weight.
- Frailty, age extremes, shock, pregnancy, and organ dysfunction all reduce dose requirement. When in doubt, **start low and titrate to effect**.

### Time, Context, and Dead Space

- Peak effect lags behind peak plasma concentration after a bolus. Wait before redosing.
- Context-sensitive half-time increases with infusion duration — plan to stop infusions early to allow timely wake-up.
- Line dead space is real. A flush can deliver an unintended bolus. Know your line volumes.

## Safe Preparation Workflow

### The Rights

Right patient, drug, dose, route, time, indication — and right concentration and label.

### Read the Ampoule

Name, strength, volume, route, expiry. Do this every time. Look-alike and sound-alike ampoules cause serious harm. Read aloud; point and touch.

### Asepsis

Hand hygiene before every preparation. Scrub injection ports. Sterile technique for all injectables.

### Standardisation

Use departmental standard concentrations and prefilled syringes wherever available. Keep drugs in consistent positions on the trolley — vasopressors here, opioids there, every time.

### Labelling

Label the syringe **before** you put it down. Use colour-coded, printed labels per Association of Anaesthetists (AoA) guidance. Include drug name, concentration, date and time, and your initials. An unlabelled syringe on the trolley is a patient safety incident waiting to happen.

### Second-Person Check

Mandatory for high-risk drugs: vasoactives, insulin, magnesium, large-volume local anaesthetics, and all neuraxial medications. Speak the calculation aloud; have your colleague verify.

## Calculations You Will Actually Use

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


### Dilutions

To prepare a working syringe from a concentrated stock:

1. Convert all concentrations to the same units (mg/mL or microgram/mL).
2. Apply C₁V₁ = C₂V₂ to find the volume of stock needed.
3. Make up to the final volume with an appropriate diluent.
4. Label immediately.

**Common pitfalls**: confusing mg and microgram; misreading a percentage as mg/mL; forgetting to mix after diluting.

### Infusion Rates

Where possible, use standard concentrations and programme pumps in mL/h. For weight-based infusions, dimensional analysis keeps units straight:

> mL/h = (dose in microgram/kg/min × weight in kg × 60) ÷ concentration in microgram/mL

Check your units cancel correctly, leaving mL/h. If a protocol provides a standard-concentration rate table, use it — it reduces cognitive load and error.

**Common pitfalls**: minutes versus hours; wrong concentration entered into the pump; incorrect weight.

### Local Anaesthetic Maximum Dose

1. Find the maximum dose in mg/kg for your chosen agent and route from a trusted reference.
2. Multiply by the patient's weight (use IBW or LBW in obesity; never exceed the absolute maximum).
3. Convert solution percentage to mg/mL.
4. Maximum safe volume = total allowed mg ÷ mg/mL.
5. Account for all sources: infiltration plus block volumes combined.

Reduce dose in pregnancy, frailty, and significant comorbidity. Ultrasound guidance reduces but does not eliminate toxicity risk. **Have intralipid immediately accessible and know the AoA/Resuscitation Council UK LAST management algorithm.**

### Paediatric Doses

Small patients need small doses deliverable in measurable volumes. Dilute to a concentration that your syringe and pump can accurately deliver. **Double-check every paediatric calculation with a second person.** Use departmentally approved charts or apps rather than mental arithmetic alone.

## Practical Application by Drug Class

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


### Induction and Sedation Agents

Titrate to effect. In haemodynamic compromise or sepsis, reduce the initial bolus significantly and give slowly. Avoid stacking multiple depressants simultaneously — synergy is potent.

### Opioids

Start low and titrate to respiratory rate and clinical effect. In renal impairment, avoid agents with active metabolites that accumulate. Convert intra-operative totals to a safe post-operative plan with appropriate monitoring.

### Neuromuscular Blockers and Reversal

Choose the dose scalar carefully (generally towards IBW). Redose based on train-of-four count, not the clock. Reversal dose depends on depth of block — avoid fixed-dose habits. Confirm adequate recovery objectively before extubation.

### Vasopressors and Inotropes

Use standard concentrations and infusion pumps. Start at a low rate and titrate to clinical end-points agreed with your team. Prefer a dedicated central lumen for prolonged infusions, though short-term peripheral use may be acceptable per local policy. When changing syringe or concentration, announce it aloud — a flush or concentration change can deliver an unintended bolus.

### Local Anaesthetics

Calculate your ceiling dose before drawing up anything. Use incremental injection with aspiration. Do not mix drugs in the same syringe unless compatibility is confirmed. For neuraxial administration: use NRFit connectors, a separate preparation area, and distinct labelling. **Never bring intravenous drugs into the neuraxial field.**

### Antibiotics

Correct dose, correct diluent, correct infusion time, within the surgical prophylaxis window. Dose-adjust for renal function per local policy. Confirm reconstitution instructions — not all antibiotics use the same diluent or rate.

## Infusion Set-Up: Nuts and Bolts

- Label lines at the hub. One drug per lumen where possible; dedicated lumen for vasoactives.
- Prime lines fully and record dead space.
- When changing a syringe at the same concentration, minimise the gap to avoid an infusion break.
- When changing concentration, calculate a transition plan. Avoid accidental bolusing or under-dosing during the changeover.

## Common Hazards and How to Avoid Them

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


| Hazard | Prevention |
|---|---|
| Ten-fold microgram/mg error | Convert all units before calculating; use leading zeros (0.1 mg), never trailing zeros (1.0 mg) |
| Ratio misinterpretation | Convert to mg/mL immediately; avoid 1:1000 notation in practice |
| Wrong route | NRFit for neuraxial; separate trays, labelling, and equipment |
| Look-alike ampoules | Read name aloud; point and touch; use barcode scanning where available |
| Unlabelled syringe | Label before the syringe leaves your hand |
| Sedation synergy | Add one agent at a time; allow time to assess effect |
| Paediatric decimal errors | Make measurable dilutions; double-check every time |
| Accumulation in organ impairment | Reduce dose and extend intervals; choose agents with favourable metabolism |
