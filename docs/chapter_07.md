You will give fluids and blood on almost every list. Done well, they prevent harm. Done badly — too much, too little, or the wrong type — they cause it. The goal is simple: maintain perfusion and oxygen delivery without causing fluid overload or coagulopathy.

## Core Physiology You Must Own

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


### Body Water and Where It Goes

Total body water divides into intracellular (roughly two-thirds) and extracellular (plasma plus interstitium) compartments. Isotonic crystalloids stay largely extracellular. Dextrose solutions distribute as free water across all compartments — they are not resuscitation fluids.

### The Glycocalyx

The endothelial glycocalyx is a gel-like layer lining vessel walls. It is central to fluid homeostasis. Sepsis, major surgery, and inflammation damage it, causing leak and oedema. Over-resuscitation worsens this — pouring in fluids does not refill the tank if the lining is broken.

### Oxygen Delivery

Oxygen delivery (DO₂) = cardiac output × arterial oxygen content. Haemoglobin and saturation dominate oxygen content; dissolved oxygen is trivial at normal FiO₂. Before giving a fluid bolus, ask: what is the problem? Low preload needs fluid. Vasodilation needs vasopressors. Low haemoglobin needs blood. Low saturation needs oxygen or ventilation. Treat the right problem.

### Osmolality and Tonicity

Tonicity drives water movement across cell membranes. Dextrose solutions become hypotonic once the glucose is metabolised — free water then distributes everywhere, including into the brain. Hypotonic fluids cause cerebral oedema, particularly in children. Do not use them for resuscitation or routine perioperative maintenance.

## Clinical Fluid Therapy

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


### Assessing Volume Status

No single sign is reliable. Combine history, clinical examination, and monitoring. Look at mental state, skin perfusion, capillary refill, pulse character, blood pressure trend, JVP, lung auscultation, and urine output. Supplement with point-of-care tests: lactate, base excess, haemoglobin, and electrolytes.

In ventilated patients without spontaneous effort, dynamic measures are useful — pulse pressure variation, stroke volume variation, passive leg raise, oesophageal Doppler, or echocardiography. Know their limitations before relying on them.

### The Four Categories of Fluid Therapy

Resuscitation restores circulating volume quickly when perfusion is compromised. Maintenance covers daily needs when the patient cannot eat or drink. Replacement corrects measured ongoing losses such as drains or stoma output. Redistribution of oedema requires decongestion — not more fluid. The old concept of "third-space losses" requiring large volumes is largely discredited; use clinical endpoints rather than replacing imagined losses.

### Safe Defaults

Use a balanced isotonic crystalloid (lactate- or acetate-buffered) for most resuscitation and intraoperative top-up. Give fluid as small boluses and reassess each time. Stop when the patient is no longer fluid-responsive — more fluid will only cause harm.

Avoid large volumes of 0.9% saline; its high chloride load causes a hyperchloraemic acidosis and may worsen acute kidney injury. Reserve it for specific indications such as hypochloraemic alkalosis.

For vasodilation after induction or in sepsis, start vasopressors once preload is reasonable rather than repeatedly bolusing fluid.

For postoperative maintenance, use an isotonic, low-chloride solution with some potassium unless contraindicated. Consider dextrose for patients at risk of hypoglycaemia. Follow Association of Anaesthetists guidance. In children, use isotonic maintenance post-operatively — hypotonic solutions cause dangerous hyponatraemia.

### Goal-Directed Fluid Therapy

In high-risk surgery, a structured stroke volume–guided approach avoids both under- and over-resuscitation. Use theatres tools such as oesophageal Doppler or arterial waveform analysis to optimise preload, then switch to vasopressors or inotropes if the response plateaus. Blind fluid loading does more harm than good in this context.

### Special Situations

**Sepsis.** Early balanced crystalloid resuscitation. Escalate to vasopressors when fluid is no longer moving the clinical endpoints. Avoid excess chloride. Follow local sepsis bundles.

**Trauma and major haemorrhage.** Restrict crystalloids. Activate your Major Haemorrhage Protocol (MHP) early — on clinical suspicion, not waiting for laboratory confirmation. Prioritise blood products, give tranexamic acid (TXA) promptly, keep the patient warm.

**Brain injury.** Avoid hypotonic fluids and dextrose boluses entirely. Aim for euvolaemia and normocapnia. Hypertonic saline for raised intracranial pressure is a senior-guided intervention.

**Cardiac and renal failure.** Cautious small boluses with frequent reassessment. Use vasopressors early for vasodilation. When overloaded and perfusion allows, decongest with diuretics.

**Liver disease.** Low systemic vascular resistance and low oncotic pressure make these patients prone to oedema and renal injury. Use balanced crystalloids. Albumin has specific roles (e.g. post-paracentesis, refractory septic shock) — check local guidance and involve senior input.

**Obstetric haemorrhage.** Early TXA, early blood products, avoid dilution with crystalloids. Follow your Trust's PPH protocol. PPH is haemorrhage-first, fluids second.

## Choosing a Fluid

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


### Crystalloids

Balanced solutions (e.g. Hartmann's, Plasma-Lyte) are your default. They are closer to plasma, carry less chloride, and reduce the risk of hyperchloraemic acidosis. Use 0.9% saline when specifically indicated but avoid large routine volumes.

### Colloids

Hydroxyethyl starches are contraindicated — they cause renal injury and bleeding. Gelatins have limited benefit and carry anaphylaxis risk; avoid routine use. Albumin has a role in specific indications (see above) but requires senior input.

### Dextrose-Containing Solutions

Useful for preventing or treating hypoglycaemia and as a maintenance substrate. Not for resuscitation. Avoid in neurological injury and acute hyponatraemia.

## Electrolytes and Acid–Base

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


### Sodium

Perioperative hyponatraemia is common and dangerous — seizures and cerebral oedema are the risks. The usual culprit is hypotonic fluid in a patient who has inappropriate ADH secretion (SIADH after surgery is normal). Use isotonic maintenance fluids and avoid excess free water. Symptomatic or severe hyponatraemia is an emergency; treat with hypertonic saline under senior guidance, correcting slowly to avoid osmotic demyelination. Hypernatraemia reflects water deficit; correct gradually with senior or ICU input.

### Potassium

Hypokalaemia causes arrhythmias, muscle weakness, and potentiates neuromuscular block. Replace cautiously with ECG monitoring. Correct magnesium concurrently if low — refractory hypokalaemia often reflects hypomagnesaemia. Hyperkalaemia is life-threatening. Know the ECG changes and the high-risk patient groups (renal failure, rhabdomyolysis, massive transfusion). Treat per Resuscitation Council UK algorithm: stabilise the myocardium first, then shift potassium intracellularly, then enhance elimination. Avoid suxamethonium in suspected or known hyperkalaemia.

### Calcium

Ionised calcium supports cardiac contractility and coagulation. Citrate in transfused blood chelates calcium — in massive transfusion, ionised calcium falls quickly. Check it regularly during major bleeding and replace per local guidance. Concentrated calcium salts are ideally given centrally.

### Magnesium and Phosphate

Hypomagnesaemia causes arrhythmias, bronchospasm, and makes hypokalaemia and hypocalcaemia refractory to replacement. Replace slowly and monitor reflexes and ECG. Hypophosphataemia causes muscle weakness, respiratory failure, and haemolysis — watch for it in sepsis and refeeding syndrome.

### Chloride and Acid–Base

Large volumes of 0.9% saline cause a hyperchloraemic metabolic acidosis that may worsen renal blood flow and AKI. Balanced solutions reduce this risk. Use ABG or VBG to track pH, lactate, base excess, and electrolytes. Treat the cause of acidosis — shock, sepsis — rather than chasing numbers with bicarbonate unless specifically directed.

### Glucose

Hypoglycaemia under anaesthesia is silent and dangerous. Monitor at-risk patients (diabetes on insulin, children, hepatic failure) and follow local perioperative diabetes protocols. Avoid marked hyperglycaemia equally — it worsens outcomes. Check capillary glucose regularly.

## Blood Products and Coagulation

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


### Preoperative Optimisation

Screen for anaemia early in elective patients. Treat iron deficiency and optimise haematinics. Patient Blood Management reduces transfusion and improves outcomes. Decide on group-and-save versus crossmatch based on the procedure and patient's bleeding risk, per local policy.

### Blood Components

**Red cells** restore oxygen-carrying capacity in acute or symptomatic anaemia. Transfusion thresholds are individualised — context and clinical state matter more than a single haemoglobin number. Follow NICE and British Society for Haematology guidance.

**Fresh frozen plasma (FFP)** replaces multiple coagulation factors in bleeding with coagulopathy. Not a volume expander.

**Platelets** are used for thrombocytopenic or functionally impaired bleeding. Thresholds depend on the clinical situation and whether bleeding is ongoing.

**Cryoprecipitate or fibrinogen concentrate** corrects low fibrinogen, which is often the first coagulation factor to be depleted in major haemorrhage.

**Prothrombin complex concentrate (PCC)** rapidly reverses vitamin K antagonists and has a role in some direct oral anticoagulant (DOAC) bleeds. Specific reversal agents exist for some DOACs — involve haematology early.

**Tranexamic acid (TXA)** is an antifibrinolytic. Give it early in major trauma, major haemorrhage, and postpartum haemorrhage. Earlier is better — efficacy diminishes with time.

### Massive Haemorrhage Protocol

Activate early on clinical grounds. Do not wait for laboratory results. The core principles are:

1. **Stop the bleeding** — surgery, intervention, pressure, tourniquet.
2. **Balanced blood product resuscitation** — minimise crystalloids; follow your Trust's ratio-based or viscoelastic-guided approach.
3. **Warm everything** — patient and products. Hypothermia, acidosis, and coagulopathy form a lethal triad; break it early.
4. **Correct calcium** — monitor ionised calcium and replace throughout.
5. **Track lactate, ABG, and coagulation** — use point-of-care viscoelastic tests (TEG/ROTEM) if available to guide component therapy.
6. **Reassess frequently** — and actively de-escalate once bleeding is controlled.

Clear team leadership, structured communication, and knowing your local stand-down process are as important as the clinical decisions.

### Transfusion Safety

**Consent.** Discuss benefits, risks, and alternatives where possible. Document the conversation.

**Bedside checks.** Confirm patient identity and product compatibility before every unit. Follow your Trust's two-person or electronic verification process without exception.

**Reactions — recognise and act:**

- *Acute haemolytic reaction*: fever, pain, hypotension, haemoglobinuria. Stop the transfusion immediately, resuscitate, send urgent samples, inform the transfusion laboratory. This is usually an ABO mismatch — a preventable error.
- *Febrile non-haemolytic and allergic reactions*: manage symptomatically but always exclude serious causes before restarting.
- *Anaphylaxis*: treat per Resuscitation Council UK algorithm.
- *TRALI* (transfusion-related acute lung injury): acute hypoxia and non-cardiogenic pulmonary oedema within six hours. Supportive care; diuretics are contraindicated; report to the transfusion lab.
- *TACO* (transfusion-associated circulatory overload): fluid overload with dyspnoea, raised JVP, and elevated BNP. Slow or stop the transfusion; diuretics; ventilatory support if needed.
- *Electrolyte and temperature effects*: hypocalcaemia, hyperkalaemia, and hypothermia during massive transfusion — monitor and correct actively.

Document indications, product details, and batch numbers. Complete reaction forms promptly. Apply duty of candour if harm occurs.

### Compatibility and Emergency Access

Know when group-and-save suffices and when crossmatch is needed; sample validity periods vary between labs. In life-threatening haemorrhage with no time for compatibility testing, use O-negative red cells (or O-positive for some males and older females per local policy) and switch to group-specific products as soon as they are available.

### Cell Salvage

Consider in procedures with anticipated major blood loss. Discuss with the team and patient, including any beliefs relevant to consent. Leukocyte depletion filters are required in certain circumstances — know your local indications.

### Blood Conservation

Surgical haemostasis, regional anaesthesia where appropriate, TXA, normothermia, minimal phlebotomy, careful anticoagulant management, and avoiding unnecessary transfusion all reduce blood product use. Permissive hypotension in exsanguinating trauma (a specific and senior-guided strategy) and vasopressors instead of fluids once euvolaemia is achieved are part of the conservation toolkit.

## Vascular Access and Delivery Physics

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Flow through a cannula follows Poiseuille's law: flow is proportional to the fourth power of the radius and inversely proportional to length. Short, wide-bore peripheral cannulae deliver far more volume per minute than long central lines. For major haemorrhage, two large-bore peripheral cannulae and a rapid infuser are your first priority. If peripheral access fails, intraosseous access is fast and effective. Central lines are for vasopressor infusions and monitoring — not for rapid volume replacement, unless you are using a large-bore introducer sheath.

Pressure bags and rapid infusers increase flow but introduce risks of air embolism and pressure injuries. Warm all fluids and blood products to reduce hypothermia.

## Monitoring and Reassessment

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Track clinical perfusion signs, heart rate, and blood pressure trends continuously. In major cases or shocked patients, catheterise for urine output. Check ABG or VBG serially for pH, lactate, base excess, ionised calcium, and haemoglobin. Monitor coagulation — viscoelastic tests or conventional lab (PT/APTT, fibrinogen, platelets). Maintain normothermia throughout.

### When Fluids Are Making Things Worse

Rising oxygen requirements, crackles on auscultation, oedema, raised JVP, and worsening gas exchange should prompt you to consider TACO, cardiogenic failure, or capillary leak — not another fluid bolus. Escalating ventilatory pressures, oliguria, and a tense abdomen suggest intra-abdominal hypertension or compartment syndrome. Distinguish vasodilation from hypovolaemia: if the patient is not fluid-responsive and is vasodilated, start vasopressors.

## Common Pitfalls and How to Avoid Them

- **Repeated fluid boluses for hypotension in a vasodilated patient.** After a reasonable preload, start vasopressors.
- **Large saline volumes causing hyperchloraemic acidosis and AKI.** Use balanced solutions as your default.
- **Hypotonic maintenance fluids causing hyponatraemia, especially in children.** Use isotonic solutions postoperatively.
- **Ignoring electrolytes during massive transfusion.** Monitor and replace calcium, magnesium, and potassium actively.
- **Late MHP activation.** Call it early on clinical grounds; do not wait for numbers.
- **Hypothermia in an actively bleeding patient.** Warm early and continuously — it is as important as products.
