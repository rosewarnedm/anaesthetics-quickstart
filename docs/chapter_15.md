# Chapter 15: Vascular Access

## Core principles that govern vascular access

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Reliable access is a prerequisite for safe anaesthesia. Getting it right means understanding a few key principles:

**Flow matters.** Poiseuille's law tells you that flow scales steeply with radius but falls with length. Practically: a short, wide-bore peripheral cannula gives dramatically better flow than a long, narrow central line. For rapid transfusion, two large-bore peripheral cannulae beat a single CVC every time.

**Shock collapses veins.** Peripheral veins disappear in hypovolaemia and vasoconstriction. Plan earlier for difficult access; escalate to intraosseous (IO) without hesitation when time is critical.

**Match the device to the job.** Peripheral IV for almost everything routine. IO when peripheral access fails in a time-critical situation. Central venous access only when there is a specific indication. Arterial lines for monitoring and sampling — never for infusions.

**Asepsis and air discipline save lives.** Line infections and air embolism are preventable. Treat every hub as a potential infection risk and every open lumen as a potential air entry point.

**Use ultrasound liberally.** It converts guesswork into a visual procedure. Learn needle–probe coordination early; it is a core RCoA curriculum skill.

## Asepsis, preparation, and human factors

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


- **Peripheral cannula:** aseptic non-touch technique (ANTT). Alcohol-based chlorhexidine skin prep; allow it to dry fully.
- **CVC and arterial lines:** maximal sterile barrier — cap, mask, sterile gown and gloves, full drape, sterile probe cover and gel. This is standard of care in the UK.
- Before you start: confirm the indication, explain and consent when time allows, prepare all equipment, prime lines to exclude air, and label everything at source.
- Document every attempt: site, device size, number of attempts, ultrasound use, complications, and confirmation method.

## Peripheral intravenous access

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


### Indications
Routine anaesthesia, sedation, fluids, drugs, blood products. The default starting point for almost every patient.

### Site and catheter choice
- First choice: dorsum of hand or forearm. Avoid flexures where possible.
- For major haemorrhage risk: largest bore feasible in a straight proximal forearm or antecubital vein; consider two lines early.
- Avoid: fistula arm, lymphoedema, ipsilateral axillary surgery, infected skin.

### Technique
- Tourniquet, warm the limb, palpate rather than just look. Low entry angle, bevel up, see flashback, advance the catheter off the needle. Release the tourniquet before flushing.
- Local anaesthetic infiltration is worth offering; it reduces patient distress and movement.
- Consider ultrasound guidance for difficult veins from the outset rather than as a last resort.

### Securing and maintenance
Flush to confirm patency. Secure with a transparent dressing, label with date and time, and inspect before every use.

### Escalation
Limit blind attempts. If you are struggling, use ultrasound, ask for help, or move to IO if the situation is time-critical. Resuscitation Council UK guidance is clear: after two failed IV attempts in a peri-arrest situation, insert IO without further delay.

### Pitfalls
Infiltration/extravasation, phlebitis, haematoma, accidental arterial puncture. If extravasation occurs: stop the infusion, aspirate if appropriate, elevate the limb, follow local antidote protocols for vesicants.

## Ultrasound-guided peripheral cannulation

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Use a linear high-frequency probe. Identify the vein by compressibility; arteries are pulsatile and do not compress. Note the depth and plan a shallow trajectory to keep the needle tip visible throughout.

Two main techniques:
- **Short-axis (out-of-plane):** gives a cross-sectional view; walk the tip stepwise.
- **Long-axis (in-plane):** continuous tip visualisation along the vessel.

Ultrasound-guided cannulae tend to be deeper and need robust fixation. The technique significantly improves first-pass success rates, particularly in obesity, oedema, and patients with difficult venous access.

## Intraosseous access

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


### When to use it
Peri-arrest, profound shock, or any time-critical scenario where IV access has failed. IO is a bridge, not a destination — pursue definitive venous access once the immediate crisis is managed.

### Sites
Proximal tibia is the default. Proximal humerus gives better flow rates in adults. Distal tibia is an alternative.

### Technique
Landmarks, skin prep, drill until you feel the "give" into the medullary space. Confirm with easy free-flow aspiration and a smooth flush. Use a pressure bag to achieve adequate flow rates. Secure the device well.

### Key points
- Awake patients: use intraosseous local anaesthetic before initiating fluid infusion — this is important and often forgotten.
- **Contraindications:** fracture proximal to the site, local infection or burns, previous IO in the same bone, prosthesis nearby.
- Watch for extravasation and compartment syndrome — if flow is poor or swelling develops, stop and relocate.

## Central venous access

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


### Indications
Irritant or vesicant drugs, parenteral nutrition, haemodynamic monitoring, renal replacement therapy, frequent blood sampling, unreliable peripheral access. Do not insert a CVC simply because peripheral access is difficult — the indication needs to be clinical.

### Site selection

| Site | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|---|---|---|
| Internal jugular | Ultrasound-friendly, compressible | Neck haematoma can threaten airway |
| Subclavian/axillary | Lower infection and thrombosis rates | Pneumothorax risk; difficult haemostasis |
| Femoral | Fast, no pneumothorax risk | Higher infection/thrombosis; limits mobility |

- Coagulopathy: prefer compressible sites (internal jugular or femoral); avoid subclavian.
- Renal patients: protect future fistula — avoid subclavian.

### Preparation
Full checklist pause. Ultrasound is the standard of care for all CVC insertions in the UK. Patient in Trendelenburg for internal jugular and subclavian to distend veins and reduce air embolism risk. Full sterile barrier. Monitoring running, resuscitation equipment available.

### Technique (Seldinger)
- Ultrasound-guided needle entry; aspirate dark, non-pulsatile blood; confirm venous placement with ultrasound.
- Pass the guidewire smoothly — **never let go of the wire end at any point**.
- If ectopics occur, the wire is too deep; withdraw it slightly.
- Dilate only to the necessary size. Do not over-dilate.
- Thread catheter, remove wire, aspirate and flush all lumens, secure.

### Air safety
Keep the field wet. Cap all lumens. Maintain Trendelenburg. Ask a cooperative patient to hum or hold their breath during insertion and removal.

### Confirmation and tip position
Confirm venous placement with ultrasound at the time of insertion. Confirm tip position per local policy — ECG-guided systems or chest radiograph. Femoral lines do not require chest imaging unless clinically indicated.

### Care and maintenance
Apply the central line care bundle: hand hygiene, scrub the hub before every access, closed systems, daily review, remove when no longer needed. Label lumens by intended use.

### Complications

- **Arterial puncture:** stop, remove, compress. Do **not** dilate an artery.
- **Pneumothorax:** controlled technique, ultrasound guidance, post-procedure imaging when indicated.
- **Air embolism:** head-down positioning, cap all hubs, expel air from lines.
- **Arrhythmias:** wire too deep — withdraw.
- **Catheter-related bloodstream infection:** strict asepsis, daily bundle review.
- **Guidewire loss:** maintain control at all times; verbalise "wire in" and "wire out" as a habit.

### Removal
Supine or head-down for internal jugular or subclavian. Remove on expiration or with Valsalva. Apply immediate occlusive dressing. Observe for bleeding and air entrainment. Document the removal.

## Arterial access

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


### Indications
Beat-to-beat blood pressure monitoring, frequent blood gas sampling, major surgery, vasoactive infusions, respiratory failure.

### Site selection
- **Radial:** first-line. Accessible and compressible. Assess clinically for collateral perfusion; the Allen's test alone is not sufficient to exclude or permit use.
- **Femoral:** reliable signal in shock; higher infection risk.
- **Axillary/brachial/dorsalis pedis:** alternatives for experienced operators or when radial is unavailable.
- Ultrasound improves first-pass success and reduces complications at every site.

### Technique
Maximal sterile barrier. Local anaesthetic. Ultrasound-guided puncture. Connect immediately to a primed, pressurised flush system. Zero and level the transducer at the mid-axillary line.

**Label the arterial system clearly and distinctly from venous lines.** This is a national patient safety priority.

### Waveform interpretation
Mean arterial pressure (MAP) is the most reliable value regardless of waveform quality. An overdamped trace underestimates systolic pressure; an underdamped trace overestimates it. Perform a flush test to assess damping characteristics. Check for air, kinks, or clot if the waveform is poor.

If the hand becomes pale or painful, reassess urgently — remove the line and seek senior help if ischaemia is suspected.

### Complications
Haematoma, thrombosis, ischaemia, pseudoaneurysm, accidental intra-arterial injection. Never connect infusions to arterial lines — use physical separation, distinct colour-coding, and dedicated sampling devices per local policy.

## Special populations

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


- **Paediatrics:** use topical local anaesthetic when time allows; move to IO early in shock; observe neonatal chlorhexidine precautions per local policy.
- **Elderly:** fragile skin tears easily; avoid high-pressure infusions through small distal cannulae; secure gently but firmly.
- **Obesity:** deeper targets; prioritise ultrasound; consider longer cannulae.
- **Renal patients:** protect the arm — avoid subclavian CVCs and the fistula limb entirely.
- **Coagulopathy:** compressible sites, ultrasound guidance, prolonged pressure after arterial puncture.
- **Burns/trauma:** avoid through-burn cannulation; use IO early.

- Label every line from bag to patient.
- Before starting any infusion — or in any crisis — trace every line from source to patient.
- Arterial and venous systems must be physically and visually separated at all times.
- Scrub hubs before every access without exception.

## Pitfalls and how things go wrong

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


- **Multiple blind attempts:** rising failure rate and patient harm. Stop after a reasonable number, reassess, use ultrasound, escalate.
- **Posterior wall puncture:** commoner with out-of-plane technique; use dynamic tip tracking and a shallow angle.
- **Aggressive dilation:** tears vessels or pleura. Dilate only over the wire, only to the required size, with controlled movement.
- **Air entrainment:** uncapped hubs in a head-up patient. Maintain Trendelenburg, occlude hubs immediately.
- **Guidewire loss:** inattention at a critical moment. Verbalise wire status; use checklists.
- **Artery–vein misidentification:** always confirm under ultrasound; send a blood gas sample if in any doubt.

Follow the RCoA curriculum outcomes for vascular access and the Association of Anaesthetists standards for asepsis and line care. Use insertion checklists and care bundles. Log every procedure and complication; seek feedback.

**Escalate early.** In a crash situation: if two IV attempts fail or a defined time limit has passed, move immediately to IO in line with Resuscitation Council UK ALS guidance. There is no prize for persisting with failing IV access in an unwell patient.

## Quick reference: safe defaults

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


- Prepare, prime, and label **before** you puncture anything.
- **For speed:** short and wide-bore peripheral beats long and narrow central.
- Use ultrasound for difficult peripheral access, all CVCs, and most arterial lines.
- **Full sterile barrier** for every CVC and arterial line insertion.
- **Air prevention:** Trendelenburg for internal jugular and subclavian; cap everything; never leave a hub open.
- **Hold the wire. Always. Without exception.**
- Confirm position before use, secure well, review daily, remove when no longer needed.
- When in doubt about doses, flush policies, or tip targets: check local protocols and trusted references.
