# Chapter 4: Preoperative Assessment

---

The purpose of preoperative assessment is simple: identify risk, reduce what you can, plan for the rest, and make sure the patient understands what they are agreeing to. Good preassessment prevents cancellations, complications, and nasty surprises in theatre.

The core idea is this: surgery is a physiological stress. What determines outcome is the patient's reserve versus the magnitude of the operation. Oxygen delivery depends on cardiac output and haemoglobin — if either is compromised, your margin for error shrinks. Fix what is fixable before the knife goes in.

## What Good Looks Like

A good preoperative assessment produces:

- A clear problem list — patient risks, surgical risks, and how you will address each
- Evidence of shared decision-making — the patient understands options, likely benefits, and material risks
- A documented anaesthetic plan A through D covering airway, anaesthesia technique, analgesia, fluids, and postoperative destination
- Medications and fasting managed safely
- Contingencies arranged: blood availability, HDU or ICU bed, specialist kit, senior support

## The Preoperative Interview

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


### Essentials to Cover

**The procedure.** Know what is being done, how invasive it is, the expected blood loss, patient position, and any surgical specifics — tourniquet, pneumoperitoneum, one-lung ventilation, bone cement.

**Patient priorities.** What matters to the patient? What are their goals, what would be unacceptable, and are there any ceilings of care to clarify?

**Previous anaesthetics.** Has anything gone wrong? Ask specifically about difficult airway, awareness, malignant hyperthermia (MH), severe postoperative nausea and vomiting (PONV), unexpected ICU admission, or suspected anaphylaxis. If anaphylaxis is reported, ask about the agent, timing, and features — this matters for planning.

**Allergies.** Distinguish true allergy from side-effect. Ask specifically about antibiotics, chlorhexidine, latex, adhesive dressings, local anaesthetics, contrast, and neuromuscular blocking drugs.

**Medications and substances.** Key ones to think about:

- Anticoagulants and antiplatelets — especially in patients with recent coronary stents or prosthetic valves; never stop without specialist input
- Antihypertensives — continue beta-blockers; ACE inhibitors and ARBs require individual judgement per local policy
- Diabetes drugs — insulin, SGLT-2 inhibitors, and metformin all need specific perioperative plans
- Steroids and immunosuppressants
- Parkinson's and antiepileptic drugs — missed doses cause real harm; plan alternative routes if the patient will be nil by mouth
- Herbal supplements, alcohol, smoking, recreational drugs (including cocaine — relevant to cardiovascular risk and drug interactions)

**Systems review.** You are looking for conditions that increase risk or change your plan:

- *Cardiac:* angina, heart failure, valve disease (aortic stenosis is a red flag), arrhythmias, syncope, pacemaker or ICD — get device details and confirm it has been recently checked
- *Respiratory:* breathlessness, asthma or COPD control, obstructive sleep apnoea (OSA) — use the STOP-BANG screen
- *Neurological:* stroke, seizures, neuromuscular disease
- *Renal and hepatic disease* — affect drug choice and fluid management
- *Endocrine:* diabetes and its complications, thyroid, adrenal disorders
- *Haematology:* anaemia, bleeding history, VTE history
- *Gastrointestinal:* reflux, dysphagia, bowel obstruction — all raise aspiration risk
- *Infection:* MRSA status, recent or active infection
- *Pregnancy:* test where applicable

**Functional status and frailty.** Ask how far the patient can walk on the flat, whether they can climb a flight of stairs without stopping, and whether they use any mobility aids. Ask about falls, cognition, and whether they live independently. Poor functional capacity predicts complications. Use a frailty screening tool if your department has one.

**Airway and aspiration risk.** These two questions should run in parallel throughout your assessment. OSA, obesity, reflux, delayed gastric emptying (diabetes, opioids, pain), pregnancy, bowel obstruction, and trauma all raise aspiration risk and influence your technique.

### Examination: High-Yield

- Vital signs and BMI
- **Airway:** mouth opening, jaw protrusion, neck movement, dentition (loose teeth noted and documented), beard, scars or radiotherapy to the neck. Think through each step — can you bag-mask ventilate, place a supraglottic device, laryngoscope, or perform front-of-neck access (FONA)? Use the DAS framework
- **Cardiorespiratory:** murmurs, signs of cardiac failure, wheeze, crackles, oedema
- **Abdomen:** distension, signs of obstruction
- **Neurological baseline**
- **Vascular access:** check it now, not in theatre

## Risk Stratification

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


No single score tells you what to do, but structured tools help you frame the conversation and document your reasoning:

- **ASA physical status** — a useful shorthand for overall physiological reserve
- **Cardiac risk indices** — to quantify cardiac risk for non-cardiac surgery
- **ARISCAT** — pulmonary complication risk
- **STOP-BANG** — OSA screening
- **SORT** — Surgical Outcome Risk Tool; useful for high-risk cases

The conditions that most often change your plan are: severe valve disease, pulmonary hypertension, decompensated heart failure, unstable angina, significant arrhythmia, severe COPD or asthma, advanced chronic kidney disease, active infection, profound anaemia, malnutrition, and frailty. If any of these are present, involve a senior early.

## Optimisation and Prehabilitation

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Time-to-surgery matters. Raise the flag early if the patient needs optimisation — last-minute concerns cause cancellations or, worse, proceeding unsafely.

- **Anaemia:** diagnose the cause. Treat with oral or intravenous iron depending on the timeframe. Arrange blood management early for high-risk cases
- **Cardiorespiratory:** optimise inhalers, encourage smoking cessation, treat heart failure or angina, ensure OSA patients are using their CPAP device
- **Metabolic:** improve glycaemic control, manage thyroid and adrenal disorders
- **Nutrition and frailty:** refer for dietetic input, exercise prehabilitation, and psychological support where available
- **Active infection:** treat before elective surgery; decolonise where indicated
- **Cardiac devices:** coordinate with the pacing or cardiology team; establish the perioperative plan, including magnet policy, well in advance

## Medication Management: Principles

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Follow local policy and seek pharmacy or specialist input when uncertain. The guiding principle is: stopping a medication suddenly is often more dangerous than continuing it.

- **Beta-blockers:** continue
- **ACE inhibitors and ARBs:** individualised decision, balancing hypotension risk against heart failure control — follow local policy
- **Anticoagulants and antiplatelets:** never stop independently in a patient with a recent coronary stent or prosthetic valve. Plan interruption, bridging, and neuraxial timing with haematology or cardiology input
- **Diabetes drugs:** insulin regimens need perioperative adjustment; SGLT-2 inhibitors carry a risk of euglycaemic diabetic ketoacidosis and are typically withheld for a defined period — check local guidance
- **Steroids:** consider perioperative stress-dose cover in chronic users, patients with adrenal disease, or those recently on high-dose courses
- **Parkinson's and antiepileptics:** missed doses cause real harm. Plan the route of administration if the patient will be nil by mouth. Involve the relevant specialist if needed
- **MAOIs, antipsychotics, antidepressants:** anticipate haemodynamic effects and drug interactions; seek senior or pharmacy input early
- **Herbal supplements:** stop those with bleeding or interaction risk

## Fasting and Aspiration Risk

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


The goal is to reduce gastric volume and acidity whilst avoiding unnecessary dehydration and insulin resistance. Follow your hospital's enhanced recovery policy.

The general principle: solids require a longer fast than clear fluids. Clear fluids are safe much closer to the time of surgery. Prolonged unnecessary fasting causes dehydration, hypotension, and hypoglycaemia in diabetic patients — it is not a safety measure, it is a hazard.

**High aspiration risk** requires a modified approach: symptomatic reflux, bowel obstruction, delayed gastric emptying, pregnancy, trauma, and urgent cases. Plan for rapid sequence induction (RSI), appropriate airway management, and consider awake techniques where the airway is also a concern.

Chewing gum and small sips of water are rarely grounds for cancellation — check your local policy before making that decision.

## Airway Planning

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Use multiple predictors together — no single test is reliable enough alone.

Plan A through D in line with DAS guidance:

- **Plan A:** primary technique (direct or video laryngoscopy, appropriate blade)
- **Plan B:** supraglottic airway rescue
- **Plan C:** further intubation attempts via alternative approach
- **Plan D:** front-of-neck access (FONA) — know where the equipment is and who will help

If any predictor raises concern, involve a senior and book the appropriate kit in advance. Do not leave airway planning until the morning of surgery.

Document loose teeth and denture management. Consider aspiration risk, cervical spine limitations, mouth opening, OSA, obesity, prior head and neck surgery, and tracheostomy history — all affect your plan.

## Consent and Communication

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Explain anaesthetic options (general, regional, local with sedation), material risks, benefits, and alternatives. Material risks are those that a reasonable person in this patient's situation would want to know — not just the statistically common ones.

Cover: PONV risk and your mitigation plan, postoperative pain management, likelihood of HDU or ICU admission, use of blood products and alternatives (cell salvage, antifibrinolytics), and the possibility of converting from regional to general anaesthesia.

**Capacity:** assess it formally where there is any doubt. Use an interpreter if needed — family members are not an adequate substitute for official interpretation. Document clearly. Check for any advance directives, DNACPR orders, or treatment escalation plans, and confirm their perioperative applicability with the wider team.

- Identify bleeding risk from both the surgery and the patient
- Request group and save or crossmatch as indicated; verify the antibody screen is current
- Optimise haemoglobin preoperatively
- Plan antifibrinolytics and cell salvage for major cases
- Consent for blood products. For patients who decline transfusion, document this clearly and plan alternatives prospectively — do not leave this conversation to the last minute

## Postoperative Planning

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


- **Analgesia:** multimodal; plan regional techniques early; account for OSA and opioid sensitivity; avoid neuraxial techniques in anticoagulated patients in line with local and national guidance
- **Antiemesis:** assess PONV risk factors and prescribe prophylaxis accordingly
- **Fluids:** assess volume status preoperatively; avoid salt and water overload postoperatively
- **Thromboprophylaxis:** balance VTE risk against bleeding risk; use mechanical and pharmacological measures as appropriate
- **Destination:** confirm bed availability before proceeding for high-risk cases — a last-minute cancellation because there is no HDU bed is avoidable
- **Handover:** structured and complete — airway, lines, drugs, analgesia, fluids, and any concerns

## Special Populations: Red Flags and Tips

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


**Elderly and frail patients:** delirium risk is high. Minimise deliriogenic drugs. Maintain orientation. Preserve glasses, hearing aids, and routine. Involve geriatricians for complex cases. Prioritise early mobilisation.

**Obesity and OSA:** anticipate a potentially difficult airway, reduced functional residual capacity (FRC), and opioid sensitivity. Optimise positioning. Ensure CPAP is continued perioperatively. Plan opioid-sparing analgesia.

**Cardiac devices:** plan diathermy precautions, establish the magnet policy, and confirm whether asynchronous pacing is needed. Coordinate with the device team before the day of surgery.

**Severe valve disease or pulmonary hypertension:** involve senior anaesthesia, cardiology, and critical care early. Invasive monitoring is often needed. These patients are not for trainees to manage alone.

**CKD and liver disease:** adjust drug choices, avoid nephrotoxins, anticipate coagulopathy, and manage fluid balance carefully.

**Pregnancy and non-obstetric surgery:** aspiration risk is elevated. Avoid aortocaval compression. Consider fetal wellbeing throughout. Liaise with obstetrics.

**Neuromuscular disease:** avoid MH triggers in susceptible patients. Expect altered responses to muscle relaxants. Plan for potential postoperative ventilation.

## Physiology at the Coalface

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


A few principles worth anchoring:

- **Oxygen delivery** depends on cardiac output and haemoglobin. Low haemoglobin and poor cardiac function reduce your margin for error. Fix what you can.
- **Functional capacity** reflects cardiopulmonary reserve. Poor exercise tolerance correlates with postoperative complications.
- **FRC falls** in the supine position and is further reduced by obesity and pregnancy. Preoxygenation strategy matters when airway difficulty is anticipated — a patient who desaturates rapidly gives you far less time.
- **Ageing and organ dysfunction** change pharmacokinetics and pharmacodynamics significantly. Start cautiously, titrate carefully, monitor closely.
- **OSA and respiratory depressants:** heightened sensitivity to sedatives and opioids. Plan alternatives and ensure appropriate postoperative monitoring.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Anchoring on "previous anaesthetic fine"** — new disease develops. Reassess properly every time
- **Stopping antiplatelets or anticoagulants without specialist input** — preventable MI, stroke, or bleeding
- **Missing severe aortic stenosis or pulmonary hypertension** — these change everything
- **Not screening for OSA** — leads to postoperative opioid-related respiratory events
