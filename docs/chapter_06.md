# Chapter 6: Regional and Neuraxial Anaesthesia

Regional techniques are core anaesthetic skills. Done well, they improve analgesia, reduce opioid consumption, and enable day-case pathways. Done badly, they cause serious harm. Work systematically, know your rescue plans, and never deviate from safety basics.

## Universal Safety Principles

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Every regional technique, every time:

- **Right patient, right side, right block.** Use WHO checks and STOP-Before-You-Block. Mark the site visibly before positioning the patient.
- **Consent properly.** Cover benefits, failure rate and need for GA, nerve injury, bleeding, infection, LAST, and block-specific risks (e.g. pneumothorax for supraclavicular, phrenic palsy for interscalene, haematoma and PDPH for neuraxial).
- **Monitor to Association of Anaesthetists standards.** Add capnography if sedating.
- **Rescue ready before you start.** Airway equipment, vasopressors, and intralipid must be immediately available — not in a cupboard down the corridor.
- **Asepsis.** Full sterile technique for neuraxial and deep blocks. Use chlorhexidine in alcohol and let it dry completely — wet chlorhexidine on neural tissue is neurotoxic.
- **Inject safely.** Incremental injection, frequent aspiration, visualise the needle tip before every bolus. Stop immediately for high resistance, severe pain, or paraesthesia on injection — these signal intraneural or intravascular placement.
- **Document everything.** Indication, side, LA agent, total dose, adjuvants, needle/catheter used, imaging, aspiration checks, block assessment, complications, and advice given.

## Local Anaesthetic Essentials

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


### Pharmacology you must know

- **Amides** (lidocaine, bupivacaine, levobupivacaine, ropivacaine) are hepatically metabolised. **Esters** are hydrolysed by plasma cholinesterases. Most blocks use amides.
- Potency correlates with lipid solubility; duration with protein binding; speed of onset with the proportion non-ionised at tissue pH.
- **Differential block** matters clinically: sympathetic fibres block first, then sensory, then motor. After neuraxial block, expect hypotension before loss of sensation, and sensory loss before motor weakness. Patients with dense motor block are a falls risk.

### Dosing principles

- Calculate the maximum safe total dose **before you start**, based on weight, site vascularity, patient comorbidities (age, pregnancy, hepatic disease), and any other planned blocks.
- Use the **lowest effective concentration and volume** for the clinical goal. A dense surgical block needs a higher concentration than an analgesic block.
- Adrenaline slows systemic absorption and helps flag intravascular injection — use it where appropriate, but know the contraindications (obstetric epidurals, end-arterial territories; follow local policy).

### LAST (Local Anaesthetic Systemic Toxicity)

LAST is rare but rapidly fatal. Bupivacaine is more cardiotoxic than levobupivacaine or ropivacaine — prefer the latter for high-volume blocks.

**Risk factors:** high total dose, intravascular injection, vascular sites (intercostal, paravertebral), extremes of age, pregnancy.

**Recognise the progression:**
1. Prodrome — circumoral tingling, tinnitus, metallic taste, agitation
2. CNS toxicity — seizures, loss of consciousness
3. Cardiovascular collapse — which may be sudden, especially with bupivacaine

**Respond immediately:** stop the LA, call for help, secure the airway, give oxygen, control seizures, give **lipid emulsion** per your trust's LAST algorithm (keep it on the resuscitation trolley). Avoid vasopressin in large doses. Refractory cardiovascular collapse may require ECMO — alert the team early.

## Ultrasound Basics

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


- **Linear probe** (high frequency) for superficial structures; **curvilinear** (lower frequency) for depth. Higher frequency = better resolution, less penetration.
- Optimise the image: depth just beyond the target, focal zone at the target, gain adjusted to distinguish tissue planes.
- **In-plane** technique shows the entire needle shaft and tip as a bright line. **Out-of-plane** shows a dot — confirm it is the tip, not the shaft, before injecting.
- **Always see the tip before injecting.** Use a small hydrodissection bolus of saline to confirm correct placement and open the plane.
- Watch for artefacts: anisotropy causes tendons and needles to disappear when the beam angle changes; acoustic shadowing obscures structures behind bone.
- Use a sterile probe sheath and sterile gel for neuraxial and deep peripheral blocks.

## Peripheral Nerve and Fascial Plane Blocks

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


### Choosing a block

**Upper limb:**
| Block | Covers | Key risk |
|---|---|---|
| Interscalene | Shoulder | Near-universal phrenic palsy — avoid in poor respiratory reserve |
| Supraclavicular | Arm/forearm/hand | Pneumothorax — keep needle lateral, see the tip |
| Infraclavicular | Elbow/forearm/hand | Lower pneumothorax risk; good for catheters |
| Axillary | Forearm/hand | Musculocutaneous nerve requires separate injection |

**Lower limb:**
| Block | Covers | Key risk |
|---|---|---|
| Femoral | Anterior thigh/knee | Quadriceps weakness → falls risk |
| Adductor canal | Knee analgesia | More motor-sparing than femoral |
| Sciatic (proximal or popliteal) | Posterior leg/foot | Foot drop while dense — protect the limb |

**Trunk:**
- **TAP / rectus sheath:** abdominal wall analgesia. High systemic absorption — track cumulative dose.
- **ESP, PECS, serratus:** chest wall and axilla. Generally safer planes; spread is variable.
- **Paravertebral:** unilateral thoracic analgesia; pneumothorax and hypotension risk — an advanced skill, not for independent practice early on.

### Practical tips and pitfalls

- Use **colour Doppler** near major vessels to avoid inadvertent vascular puncture.
- In plane blocks, loss of fascial resistance and spread of hydrodissection confirm the correct plane.
- **Stop for high opening pressure, nerve swelling, or sharp pain** — these suggest intraneural injection. Reposition before continuing.
- **Map the block to the incision** before starting. If coverage is inadequate before knife, supplement early with local infiltration, adjust the plan, or convert to GA. Do not discover failure on the table.
- **Post-block care matters:** protect insensate limbs from heat and pressure; use a sling for dense upper-limb blocks; give falls precautions for lower-limb motor block; check distal perfusion.

### Catheters

Use for prolonged analgesia. Tunnel and secure carefully, maintain full asepsis, review daily, and remove promptly when no longer needed. Provide clear written instructions to ward staff: infusion rate, expected effects, and red flags. Time removal relative to anticoagulation per guideline.

### Special cautions

- **Anticoagulation:** deep plexus and deep peripheral blocks carry a neuraxial-equivalent bleeding risk. Follow local policy — if uncertain, avoid and discuss with a senior.
- **Respiratory disease:** avoid phrenic-impairing blocks (interscalene, supraclavicular) in patients with marginal reserve.
- **Compartment syndrome:** dense block may mask pain — only proceed with an agreed monitoring and orthopaedic plan.
- **Bilateral blocks** double systemic LA load and (for interscalene/supraclavicular) bilateral respiratory risk. Consider alternatives.

## Neuraxial Anaesthesia

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


### Anatomy

Needle passes through: skin → subcutaneous tissue → supraspinous ligament → interspinous ligament → ligamentum flavum → **epidural space** → dura → arachnoid → **CSF**.

The spinal cord ends at approximately L1 in adults. Always insert below this level.

### Spinal vs epidural vs CSE

- **Spinal:** LA directly into CSF. Rapid, dense, predictable. Single-shot; height determined by dose, baricity, position, and CSF dynamics.
- **Epidural:** LA into the epidural space. Segmental, slower onset, titratable via catheter. Variable spread.
- **CSE:** combines the speed and density of spinal with the flexibility of an epidural catheter for prolonged duration.

### Physiology at the coalface

Neuraxial block causes sympathectomy: vasodilation, reduced preload, and hypotension. Higher blocks affect cardioaccelerator fibres (bradycardia) and can impair respiratory muscles if they reach the thoracic cord.

In pregnancy, engorged epidural veins and reduced CSF volume increase spread — dose requirements are lower. Aortocaval compression from the gravid uterus must be actively avoided with left uterine displacement.

### Technique essentials

- **Position:** maximal lumbar flexion opens interspinous spaces. A seated position is often easiest for spinals; lateral is useful in obstetrics or the sedated patient.
- **Paramedian approach** when midline is calcified or difficult to palpate.
- **Epidural:** loss-of-resistance to saline is standard. Advance slowly and deliberately — inadvertent dural puncture is a significant complication.
- **Test dose:** before epidural top-up or after catheter insertion, use an appropriate test dose to detect intrathecal or intravascular placement. The method varies by context (especially in obstetrics) — follow local protocol.
- **Incremental dosing:** never give an epidural bolus as a rapid single injection. Fractionate it, aspirate between doses, and observe the patient throughout.
- **For spinals:** use a pencil-point needle (Whitacre or Sprotte) to reduce PDPH risk. Ensure antiseptic is fully dry before inserting the needle.

### Immediate complications

**Hypotension and bradycardia**
Common, especially after spinal. Anticipate it, have vasopressors drawn up before the block, and treat promptly. In obstetrics, add left uterine displacement and consider a proactive vasopressor infusion per local protocol.

**High or total spinal**
Rapidly ascending numbness, dyspnoea, difficulty speaking, hypotension, and bradycardia — this is a critical emergency. Call for help immediately. Secure and manage the airway, ventilate, give vasopressors and fluids, and tilt supine/left lateral as needed. Early recognition and action prevent cardiac arrest.

**Intravascular epidural injection**
Causes LAST — treat accordingly.

**Accidental dural puncture**
Recognise CSF return through an epidural needle. Discuss with a senior: options include converting to an intrathecal catheter or resiting the epidural. Counsel the patient and document clearly. Monitor for PDPH.

### Ongoing risks

**PDPH (Post-Dural Puncture Headache)**
Typical presentation: postural, bifrontal/occipital headache within 1–5 days. Worse sitting or standing, better lying flat. Manage conservatively first (hydration, simple analgesia). For persistent or severe PDPH, **epidural blood patch** is the definitive treatment — discuss with a senior and follow local protocol.

**Epidural haematoma or abscess**
These are rare but catastrophic. **Red flags requiring urgent action:**
- New severe back pain
- Progressive motor weakness
- Sensory loss
- Bladder or bowel dysfunction

Stop the infusion, escalate immediately, arrange urgent MRI, and obtain neurosurgical review. **Time to decompression is everything.** This is not a wait-and-see situation.

**Catheter problems**
Patchy or unilateral block suggests catheter malposition. Troubleshoot by adjusting depth, volume, or concentration — or replace. Watch for intrathecal migration with any catheter.

### Anticoagulation and neuraxial

Timing of insertion and catheter removal relative to anticoagulants and antiplatelets is critical. Follow current Association of Anaesthetists/ESRA-aligned guidance and your trust's protocol. **If in doubt, defer the neuraxial and seek senior or haematology input.** An avoidable haematoma causing paraplegia is an unacceptable outcome.

## Sedation with Regional Anaesthesia

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Sedation facilitates patient comfort during block placement and surgery but carries airway risk. Apply UK sedation standards:

- Continuous monitoring including capnography
- Titrate slowly; aim for minimal to moderate sedation
- Preserve airway reflexes and patient cooperation
- Be ready to manage the airway per DAS principles

**If you cannot rescue the airway, do not sedate the patient.**

## Assessment and Decision-Making

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


### Before the block

- Is regional the right choice? Consider contraindications: coagulopathy, infection at site, sepsis, allergy (including chlorhexidine and latex), raised ICP (for neuraxial), patient refusal.
- Document baseline neurovascular status.
- Calculate the LA dose plan. Draw up vasopressors. Confirm intralipid is present.

### Before the knife

Test the block systematically using cold or pinprick in the relevant dermatomes. If the block is inadequate, **say so early and supplement or convert to GA**. Do not drift into a crisis on the operating table.

### Post-operatively

- Anticipate block wear-off and provide rescue analgesia proactively as part of a multimodal plan.
- Give ward staff clear written instructions: expected effects, limb protection, falls precautions, catheter care if relevant, and red flags to escalate.

## Special Contexts

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


### Obstetrics

- **Labour epidural:** use dilute LA with opioid for motor-sparing analgesia. Monitor mother and foetus regularly. Manage hypotension with left uterine displacement and vasopressors.
- **Spinal/CSE for caesarean section:** anticipate profound sympathectomy. Have vasopressors drawn up and a proactive infusion strategy per local protocol.
- Epidural veins are engorged: intravascular placement is more likely. Confirm catheter position carefully with an appropriate test strategy before every bolus.

### Day-case surgery

Choose blocks with predictable offset. Provide written discharge instructions covering limb protection, falling risk, what to do if the block has not worn off, and how to contact the unit. Ensure a safe escort home.

### Obesity and spinal disease

Landmark identification is harder. Use pre-procedural neuraxial ultrasound to estimate depth and identify the midline. Consider a paramedian approach and longer needles. Ask for senior help early rather than making repeated attempts.

### Pre-existing neuropathy or diabetes

Peripheral nerves are already compromised — "double crush" makes injury more likely. Use gentle technique, avoid high injection pressure, and document baseline deficits meticulously before the block. Consent should reflect the increased risk.
