## Priorities and Mindset

Oxygenate first. Ventilate second. Intubate only when necessary.

Always have a plan for failure before you start. Brief the team. Agree stop points and escalation triggers in advance. Declare problems early — saying "this is becoming CICO" out loud changes the room.

Capnography is non-negotiable. No sustained waveform means no confirmed airway. Treat absent CO₂ as oesophageal intubation until proven otherwise.

## Core Physiology That Matters

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


**Functional residual capacity (FRC)** is your oxygen reserve. It falls with obesity, pregnancy, supine positioning, sedation, and anaesthesia. Less FRC means faster desaturation.

**Pre-oxygenation** replaces nitrogen with oxygen and buys you time. Quality matters: tight mask seal, high FiO₂, coached breathing, head-up, PEEP if needed. Duration alone is not enough.

Some patients — the critically ill, the pregnant, the obese — desaturate alarmingly fast regardless. Use CPAP, head-up positioning, and apnoeic oxygenation to extend your safe window.

SpO₂ lags behind reality. ETCO₂ reflects ventilation in real time but can mislead in low cardiac output states. Read both alongside the clinical picture.

## Planning and Assessment

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Take a history. Ask about previous difficult airways, OSA, reflux, head and neck surgery or radiotherapy, and limited mouth or neck movement.

Look at the patient. Mouth opening, teeth, jaw protrusion, neck mobility, beard, obesity, and pregnancy all matter. No single sign reliably predicts difficulty — patterns and context do.

Decide: awake or asleep? Spontaneous breathing or paralysed? Mask, supraglottic airway (SGA), or tracheal tube? Have Plans A through D.

**Check your equipment before every case:**
- Oxygen sources and suction (two working)
- Mask and airway adjuncts
- Second-generation SGA
- Direct and video laryngoscope, with spare blades
- Bougie and stylet
- Front-of-neck access (FONA) kit — know where it is
- Functioning capnography

Brief the team. Allocate roles. State your plans and your limits before you induce.

## Positioning and Optimisation

The sniffing position aligns the oral, pharyngeal, and laryngeal axes. In obese patients, ramp to bring the ear level with the sternal notch. Head-up positioning improves both oxygenation and your laryngoscopic view.

Have suction immediately ready. Document any throat pack in and out.

## Pre-oxygenation and Apnoeic Oxygenation

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Aim for a tight mask seal. Coach the patient to breathe normally, or assist gently. Use PEEP and head-up positioning to recruit alveoli and improve FRC.

High-flow nasal oxygen or simple nasal cannulae during laryngoscopy provides apnoeic oxygenation — use this in any patient at risk of desaturation.

In aspiration-risk patients, balance the need for oxygenation against the risk of gastric insufflation. Follow your local RSI policy.

## Basic Airway Manoeuvres

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Head tilt–chin lift opens most adult airways. Use jaw thrust if cervical spine injury is possible.

For mask ventilation, use a two-hand, two-person technique when needed. Aim for visible chest rise at the lowest pressure that works. Don't chase large tidal volumes — you will inflate the stomach instead.

## Airway Adjuncts

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


**Oropharyngeal airway (OPA):** Size by measuring from the corner of the mouth to the angle of the jaw. Inserts to relieve tongue obstruction in an unconscious patient.

**Nasopharyngeal airway (NPA):** Useful in trismus or light anaesthesia where an OPA is not tolerated. Lubricate well. Avoid with suspected base of skull fracture or significant coagulopathy.

Use a bite block alongside any supraglottic device, particularly at emergence.

## Supraglottic Airways

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Prefer second-generation devices — they allow positive pressure ventilation and have a gastric drainage channel.

Insert at adequate depth of anaesthesia. Size appropriately. Check cuff pressure with a manometer and use the minimum that seals.

If it is not working: adjust depth, reposition the head and neck, optimise cuff pressure, or drain the stomach channel. If it still fails, replace it.

Remember: an SGA is not a definitive airway. Aspiration risk persists.

## Tracheal Intubation: Core Technique

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Choose direct (DL) or video laryngoscopy (VL) based on the case and local practice. VL improves the view but does not remove the need for good technique.

Position well. Open the mouth fully. Control the tongue. Find the epiglottis first — then look for the cords.

Use bimanual laryngoscopy or external laryngeal manipulation to improve your view. Release the manipulation when passing the tube if it helps.

**Use a bougie whenever the cords are not fully visible.** Clicks and resistance to withdrawal are helpful — do not force the tube.

With hyperangulated VL blades, shape the stylet to match the screen view. Withdraw it early once the tube is through the cords.

Check tube depth using the markings and reassess after any position change. Inflate the cuff to the minimum pressure that seals — use a manometer.

## Confirmation and Securing

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Continuous waveform capnography with sustained exhaled CO₂ is definitive confirmation of tracheal placement. If you are uncertain, remove the tube and oxygenate.

Also check: bilateral chest movement, equal air entry, no gurgling over the epigastrium, and stable observations.

Secure the tube well. Reassess after any patient movement or repositioning. Document everything.

## Rapid Sequence Induction (RSI): Principles

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


RSI is used when aspiration risk or the urgency of airway control outweighs the risk of the technique. Follow your local policy.

Optimise pre-oxygenation — use nasal oxygenation to extend your safe apnoea time.

Cricoid pressure: understand what it is for, its limitations, and — critically — when to release it. If it is obstructing your view or preventing ventilation, release it. Patient safety overrides protocol.

Avoid mask ventilation if aspiration risk is high. If you must ventilate, use low pressures. Have suction immediately to hand. Have a clear failed airway plan before you give the drugs.

## Difficult Airway and Rescue

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Follow DAS principles for unanticipated difficult intubation:
- Limit your attempts
- Change something each time (position, blade, adjunct, operator)
- Prioritise oxygenation
- Call for help early

**Failed intubation with adequate ventilation:** consider waking the patient, proceeding with an SGA, or using fibreoptic or alternative techniques depending on surgical urgency.

**Can't Intubate, Can't Oxygenate (CICO):** declare it clearly and immediately. Proceed to FONA using the scalpel-bougie-tube technique — the DAS default. Know where your kit is. Know the steps. Practise them before you need them.

Do not persist with repeated unmodified laryngoscopy. Every failed attempt causes more oedema and bleeding and makes the next attempt harder.

## Extubation: Plan and Execute

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Extubation is an airway intervention. Assess the risk: airway oedema, difficult mask ventilation, OSA, aspiration risk, airway surgery.

For most patients: suction the pharynx, sit them up, and extubate fully awake with intact protective reflexes. Deep extubation is only appropriate in selected, low-risk cases where it is specifically indicated.

For high-risk extubations, consider an airway exchange catheter and have full reintubation equipment ready.

Post-extubation: give oxygen, monitor closely, and have CPAP or nebulised adrenaline available for stridor or oedema. Escalate early if obstruction is suspected.

Follow DAS extubation guidance for at-risk patients.

## Special Populations

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


**Obesity:** Reduced FRC, rapid desaturation, often difficult mask ventilation. Ramp, head-up, PEEP/CPAP, two-person mask technique, early VL. Plan for every step being harder.

**Pregnancy:** Aspiration risk, airway oedema, fast desaturation. RSI principles apply. Avoid nasal instrumentation. Use left uterine displacement. Call for help early.

**Paediatrics:** Small airways, high resistance, very fast desaturation. Gentle technique, appropriate sizing (use local charts), check for a leak around the tube. Seek senior support early — this is a different skill set.

**Trauma/C-spine:** Manual in-line stabilisation, no head tilt. Anticipate blood and secretions — have two suctions running. Consider awake techniques if the patient is cooperative.

**Airway bleeding or soiling:** Suction first. Consider a rapid SGA as a bridge to oxygenation, then move to definitive control.

**Neck pathology or post-radiotherapy:** Restricted mobility, fibrotic tissue. Consider awake fibreoptic intubation or VL by an experienced operator. Plan for FONA.

## Pharmacology Touchpoints

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


**Induction agents:** Balance haemodynamic stability against suppression of airway reflexes. Anticipate hypotension — have vasopressors drawn up.

**Opioids:** Blunt the laryngoscopy response. Time the dose appropriately.

**Neuromuscular blockade (NMB):** Improves intubating conditions and mask ventilation quality. Before you give it, know your rescue oxygenation strategy if intubation fails — you now have a paralysed patient.

**Reversal:** Confirm adequate recovery before extubating. Use quantitative neuromuscular monitoring. Follow local protocols.

**Topical anaesthesia for awake intubation:** Effective mucosal anaesthesia, ideally with an antisialagogue, significantly improves success. Calculate and document your local anaesthetic doses carefully. Know the signs of local anaesthetic systemic toxicity (LAST) and ensure Intralipid is available per local protocol.

## Common Pitfalls

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


- **Poor pre-oxygenation and positioning** → avoidable desaturation. Fix the basics before every case.
- **Repeated unmodified laryngoscopy** → trauma, oedema, bleeding. Change your approach or escalate.
- **Missing oesophageal intubation** because of misting or chest movement. Trust only capnography.
- **Overinflated cuffs** → mucosal ischaemia, nerve injury, sore throat. Use a manometer every time.
- **Mask leak** from beard, edentulous mouth, or poor technique. Two-person mask, gauze to fill gaps, or place an SGA early.
- **High-pressure ventilation** → gastric insufflation and regurgitation. Use the lowest pressure that works.
- **Persisting with cricoid** when it is obstructing your view. Reassess continuously.
- **Tube too deep** → right mainstem intubation. **Too shallow** → accidental extubation. Check the depth mark and secure carefully.
- **Equipment unfamiliarity.** Handle and practise with your own department's kit. Checklists prevent omissions.

## Emergencies: Recognition and First Steps

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


**Laryngospasm:** Jaw thrust, 100% oxygen, CPAP. Deepen anaesthesia. If desaturation persists, give a small dose of fast-acting muscle relaxant per local protocol.

**Bronchospasm:** Hand-ventilate on 100% oxygen. Deepen anaesthesia. Give bronchodilators per local guidance. First exclude a mechanical cause — check for tube kinking or endobronchial intubation.

**Aspiration:** Head-down, suction the oropharynx, secure and protect the airway, oxygenate and ventilate. Consider tracheal suctioning. Inform the surgeon and escalate to ICU as needed.

**Negative pressure pulmonary oedema:** Occurs after obstruction against a closed glottis (e.g. laryngospasm). Treat with oxygen and PEEP; consider reintubation if severe. Prevent by avoiding struggling at emergence.

**Post-extubation stridor or oedema:** Oxygen, nebulised adrenaline, steroids, CPAP per protocol. Prepare for reintubation. Call ENT early if upper airway compromise is developing.

**Dental or airway trauma:** Inform the patient, document carefully, involve maxillofacial surgery if needed.

**Airway fire (ENT/laser):** Minimise FiO₂, avoid nitrous oxide, use wet pledgets, communicate clearly with the surgeon. If ignition occurs — stop gas flow and follow your local protocol immediately.

## Monitoring: What the Numbers Mean

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


**Capnography:** A square waveform confirms ventilation. A rising ETCO₂ suggests hypoventilation or increased CO₂ production. A "shark fin" pattern suggests bronchospasm. Low ETCO₂ can mean hyperventilation, low cardiac output, or a circuit leak.

**Pulse oximetry:** A delayed indicator. Act on trends, not single readings. Do not wait for the number to fall before responding to a clinical concern.

**Cuff pressure manometry:** Routine use. Overinflation causes real harm.

**Quantitative neuromuscular monitoring:** Use before extubating a patient who has received NMB.

When in doubt, come back to these:

- Head-up, high FiO₂, good mask technique — every time.
- Use second-generation SGAs when choosing an SGA.
- Use VL when any predictors of difficulty are present; have a bougie ready for all intubations.
- Limit laryngoscopy attempts. Change something each time. Oxygenate between attempts.
- Use a cuff manometer for every cuffed airway device.
- No capnography trace = no tube. No exceptions.
- For high-risk extubations: prepare for failure before you extubate.
