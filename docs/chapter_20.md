# Chapter 20: Simulation and Crisis Management

---

Simulation lets you fail safely. Crises are rare, fast, and unforgiving. Rehearse the moves before you need them.

---

## Why Simulation Matters

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Anaesthetic crises are too infrequent to learn from real events alone. Simulation compresses experience: you encounter a malignant hyperthermia or a CICO scenario before it happens for real.

More importantly, simulation teaches you *how you behave under pressure* — not just what you know. Communication fractures, fixation creeps in, and time perception distorts. Recognising this in a simulator is far safer than discovering it in theatre.

Use simulation to build automatic first steps. If the opening moves are ingrained, you have cognitive headspace left to lead.

## Human Factors: Core Concepts

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


**Non-Technical Skills (ANTS)** are as important as clinical knowledge in a crisis:

- **Situation awareness** — gather information, understand what is happening, anticipate what comes next.
- **Decision-making** — diagnose, choose a course of action, review as things evolve.
- **Task management** — plan, prioritise, delegate.
- **Team working** — lead clearly, follow when appropriate, support colleagues.

**Stress narrows performance.** Moderate arousal sharpens focus; excessive stress causes tunnel vision, fixation, and poor working memory. Counter it with deliberate micro-pauses and cognitive aids.

**Common cognitive traps:**

- *Fixation* — persisting with a plan that is not working.
- *Confirmation bias* — only registering information that fits your current diagnosis.
- *Premature closure* — stopping at the first plausible answer rather than the correct one.
- *Authority gradient* — junior staff not speaking up; senior staff not inviting challenge.

**Countermeasures that work:**

- Declare the emergency out loud.
- Take "10 seconds for 10 minutes" — a brief deliberate pause to think before acting.
- Use closed-loop communication.
- Build a shared mental model: state the problem, your plan, and the priorities so the whole team knows.

## Universal First Moves in a Crisis

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Develop a default script and rehearse it until it is automatic.

1. **Stop. Announce** — "This is an emergency." Say it clearly.
2. **Call for help early** — specify who you need and where they should come.
3. **ABCDE** — oxygenate first; support perfusion next; then everything else.
4. **100% oxygen** — hand-ventilate to feel compliance and chest movement.
5. **Check capnography** — no trace means wrong place until proved otherwise.
6. **Simplify** — remove complexity; disconnect from the ventilator if in doubt; bag with a simple circuit.
7. **Allocate roles** — airway, circulation, drugs, scribe.
8. **Start a timer** — anchor time for drug intervals, CPR cycles, and reassessment.
9. **Open the algorithm** — designate someone to read it aloud; you follow it.
10. **Document** — a scribe records events, interventions, and times in real time.

These steps are not optional extras. They are the framework that stops a difficult situation becoming a catastrophe.

## Communication Under Pressure

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


- Use **names and roles**: face the person, use their name, state the task.
- **Closed loop**: give the instruction, get a verbal readback, confirm when complete.
- **Sterile cockpit**: one voice leads; limit background chatter.
- **SBAR** for handover and escalation: Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation.
- Actively **invite challenge**: "If you think I'm missing something, say so."

Poor communication is implicated in the majority of critical incidents. Practise it as a skill, not an afterthought.

## Using Simulation Well

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


**Pre-brief**: establish psychological safety, confidentiality, and the fiction contract (what happens in the sim stays in the sim for learning purposes). Name one or two specific skills to practise — vague goals produce vague learning.

**Deliberate practice**: short, focused repetitions. Escalate difficulty gradually. In-situ drills in your own theatre test kit location, call systems, and local pathways — not just your clinical knowledge.

**Debrief** is where most learning happens. Use advocacy–inquiry: describe what you observed, ask what the learner was thinking, explore the gap. Avoid blame; focus on systems and decisions.

**Translate to action**: one behaviour to keep, one to change, one to try next time. Log reflective learning and link it to your RCoA curriculum outcomes and workplace-based assessments.

## Airway Crises

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


### Can't Intubate, Can't Oxygenate (CICO)

The priority is **oxygenation, not intubation**. Do not perseverate with a failing laryngoscopy.

Work through DAS Plan A → B → C, changing something meaningful each attempt: device, blade, approach, or operator. If oxygenation cannot be maintained and rescue techniques have failed, **declare CICO and proceed immediately to front-of-neck access (FONA)**. Use the scalpel–bougie–tube technique as taught locally. Delay kills.

Pitfalls: calling for help too late; repeated attempts causing progressive oedema and bleeding; forgetting to oxygenate between attempts.

### Lost Capnography or No Ventilation

Hand-ventilate and troubleshoot systematically:

1. **Tube** — displaced, obstructed, or in oesophagus?
2. **Circuit** — disconnection or leak?
3. **Gas supply** — pipeline failure?
4. **Patient** — bronchospasm, laryngospasm, tension pneumothorax?

No capnography trace means tube is in the wrong place until multiple cues confirm otherwise. A supraglottic airway device can buy time if intubation is failing.

### Laryngospasm and Bronchospasm

**Laryngospasm**: remove the stimulus, apply jaw thrust and CPAP with 100% oxygen. Deepen anaesthesia if appropriate per local guidance. Suxamethonium at a smaller-than-intubating dose can break severe laryngospasm; involve your senior.

**Bronchospasm**: allow prolonged expiration, avoid air-trapping, use low tidal volumes. Treat triggers. Do not miss anaphylaxis presenting as bronchospasm.

## Breathing and Ventilation Problems

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


**Dynamic hyperinflation** (breath-stacking): high airway pressures, rising CO₂, and hypotension in a ventilated patient. Disconnect the circuit and allow full passive exhalation before resuming gentle ventilation.

**Circuit failure or exhausted soda lime**: rising inspired CO₂, unexplained tachycardia. Switch to a self-inflating bag; change the canister.

**Tension pneumothorax**: sudden hypoxia, hypotension, and rising airway pressures. Needle decompression if the patient is haemodynamically unstable; involve the surgeons early.

## Circulatory Crises

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


### Hypotension and Arrhythmias

Think systematically: **depth, volume, pump, tone**. Check the obvious first — is the monitoring correct, is the patient adequately ventilated, is anaesthesia too deep?

Correct reversible causes: hypovolaemia, anaphylaxis, electrolyte disturbance, hypoxia, hypercarbia. Use vasopressors and chronotropes per local protocols. Do not treat the monitor; treat the patient.

### Massive Haemorrhage

**Activate the massive haemorrhage protocol early** — it is far easier to stand it down than to catch up after delay.

Secure large-bore access; send blood samples promptly. Use balanced blood components (packed red cells, fresh frozen plasma, platelets, cryoprecipitate) guided by local protocol and point-of-care testing. Maintain temperature with warming devices, correct acidosis, and replace calcium — all three drive coagulopathy if neglected.

Pitfalls: delayed activation; inadequate venous access; unmeasured ongoing losses.

### Anaphylaxis

Stop the suspected trigger immediately. Follow the Resuscitation Council UK anaphylaxis algorithm.

Priorities: airway, 100% oxygen, **early intramuscular adrenaline**, and volume resuscitation. Prepare for repeat dosing. Send tryptase samples at timed intervals and arrange allergy follow-up.

Pitfalls: attributing bronchospasm or hypotension to "light anaesthesia" when the patient is actually in anaphylaxis; delay to adrenaline; continuing the trigger.

### Local Anaesthetic Systemic Toxicity (LAST)

Neurological signs (agitation, seizures) and cardiovascular signs (arrhythmia, arrest) may be delayed after the injection. Maintain vigilance for at least 30 minutes after a large regional block.

Stop the local anaesthetic, control the airway, manage seizures, and follow your local lipid emulsion therapy protocol. Avoid drugs that may compound toxicity (propofol is not a substitute for lipid emulsion). Plan for prolonged monitoring.

Pitfalls: cumulative dosing across teams (surgeon plus anaesthetist); wrong concentration drawn up.

### Malignant Hyperthermia (MH)

A rare but potentially fatal pharmacogenetic crisis triggered by volatile agents and suxamethonium.

**Stop the trigger immediately** — switch to total intravenous anaesthesia, use a clean circuit. Call for help, activate the MH protocol, and locate the dantrolene kit. Hyperventilate with 100% oxygen, cool actively, treat hyperkalaemia and acidosis. Give dantrolene as per protocol — reconstitution is labour-intensive and requires multiple hands. Anticipate recrudescence and plan ICU admission.

Pitfalls: delayed recognition; not having enough staff to reconstitute dantrolene rapidly; inadequate venous access.

## Neurological and Regional Anaesthesia Crises

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


**High or total spinal**: rapid onset hypotension, bradycardia, and respiratory compromise. Support the airway, ventilate if needed, give vasopressors and fluids per protocol. Anticipate cardiac arrest and be ready to resuscitate. In obstetrics, communicate immediately with the obstetric team.

**Postoperative agitation**: always check ABC first. Hypoxia and hypercarbia both cause agitation. Only after excluding these should you consider pain, emergence delirium, or drug effects.

## Equipment, Environment, and Systems

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


### Oxygen or Power Failure

Multiple simultaneous alarms should prompt you to think *system failure*, not multiple patient problems.

Open the cylinder oxygen supply, disconnect the pipeline if contamination is suspected, and hand-ventilate with a self-inflating bag if necessary. Declare an emergency; contact estates. Stop elective surgery.

Pitfalls: backup cylinders that are empty because nobody checked them; unfamiliarity with the machine's emergency modes.

### Airway Fire

Stop ventilation and oxygen flow immediately. Remove burning material. Irrigate with saline. Re-establish the airway and assess for inhalation injury. Communicate with the surgeon — they need to stop using diathermy or laser.

This is rare but catastrophic. Know the principle: remove fuel, remove ignition source, remove oxygen.

## Paediatric and Obstetric Nuances

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


**Paediatrics**: children desaturate faster than adults. Equipment sizing errors are common under pressure — use a length-based tool (Broselow tape or equivalent). Weight-based drug calculations must be practised with decision aids. Follow Resuscitation Council UK paediatric guidance.

**Obstetrics**: you have two patients. Maternal haemodynamics differ from the non-pregnant baseline. The airway is more difficult and more prone to failed intubation. Know your local drills for high spinal, postpartum haemorrhage, eclampsia, and amniotic fluid embolism. Use obstetric-specific algorithms; these cases require multidisciplinary team communication.

## Sedation and Remote Site Rescue

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Always plan for rescue to general anaesthesia, even if you intend only sedation. Oxygen, suction, capnography, airway adjuncts, and reversal agents must be immediately to hand — not in the next room.

If airway obstruction or apnoea occurs: airway manoeuvres, adjuncts, ventilation support, and early senior help. Monitoring gaps and inadequate staffing at remote sites are well-recognised risks. Follow Association of Anaesthetists sedation standards.

## Leading the Team

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


**Before you start**: brief the team — names, roles, anticipated surgical plan, triggers to call for help. WHO team brief principles apply.

**During a crisis**: one person leads, assigns tasks, and steps back to think rather than doing everything. If you cannot lead, say clearly who is. Use regular summaries: "State, Plan, Risks, Roles" every few minutes.

**Leadership is a skill, not a personality trait.** It can be practised in simulation before you need it for real.

## Debrief and Learning After a Crisis

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


A **hot debrief** immediately after: what went well, what to improve, any immediate actions needed for safety.

Log the event and complete incident reporting per local governance requirements. A **cold debrief** for complex cases gives the wider team time to reflect and identify system improvements.

Close the loop: translate learning into education, protocol updates, or equipment changes. Without this step, the same errors recur.
