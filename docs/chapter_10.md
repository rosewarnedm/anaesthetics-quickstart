# Chapter 10: Subspecialty Anaesthesia

Each subspecialty has its own hazards. Know what makes a case different, what can go wrong, and when to call for help. Plan with the surgical team. Use checklists. Follow your department's SOPs and national guidance.

## Paediatric Anaesthesia

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


**Why it is different**

Children are not small adults. The airway is anatomically unfavourable — large head, large tongue, cephalad larynx, narrow subglottis. Oxygen consumption is high and reserves are low, so desaturation and bradycardia happen fast.

**What to do**

Outside simple day-case work in older children, senior support is the default. Size all equipment before induction. Keep the child warm and maintain glucose. Preoxygenate well — a two-person mask seal is often needed. Consider airway adjuncts early rather than late.

**What to avoid and watch for**

- **Laryngospasm** — avoid airway stimulation at light planes; treat promptly with CPAP, deepening, or drugs per your protocol
- **Bradycardia** — usually hypoxia; fix oxygenation first
- **Dosing errors** — always use a paediatric chart or validated calculator
- **Post-tonsillectomy bleeding** — full stomach, hypovolaemia, potentially difficult airway; get senior help early

## Obstetric Anaesthesia

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


**Why it is different**

Pregnancy makes the airway harder to manage, aspiration more likely, and drug handling different. There are two patients. Maternal safety always comes first.

**What to do**

Offer early neuraxial analgesia in labour — it reduces the chance of needing GA later. Always use left lateral uterine displacement after 20 weeks. Give antacid prophylaxis. For Caesarean section, neuraxial anaesthesia is the default; if GA is needed, senior presence is essential. For PPH: activate the major haemorrhage protocol early, give tranexamic acid, and follow the uterotonic sequence per local protocol. In pre-eclampsia, control blood pressure, use magnesium sulphate as directed, restrict fluids, and check coagulation before neuraxial blockade.

**What to avoid and watch for**

- **Failed intubation** — follow obstetric difficult-airway guidance; prioritise oxygenation, use a supraglottic airway early, and proceed to front-of-neck access if failing
- **High or total spinal** — be ready to support ventilation and circulation
- **Supine hypotension** — always displace the uterus
- **Neonatal compromise** — call the paediatric team early; follow NLS guidance

## Neurosurgical Anaesthesia

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


**Core principle**

Cerebral perfusion pressure = MAP − ICP. Protect both. Carbon dioxide is a potent cerebral vasodilator; small changes in PaCO₂ significantly affect cerebral blood flow. Target normocapnia unless otherwise directed. Volatile agents increase cerebral blood flow; TIVA is often preferred when brain relaxation or neurophysiology monitoring is needed.

**What to do**

Induce and emerge smoothly — coughing and straining raise ICP. Position the head up to aid venous drainage. Follow local protocols for osmotherapy or CSF drainage. In the sitting position, be vigilant for venous air embolism; monitor with precordial Doppler and end-tidal CO₂.

**What to avoid and watch for**

- Hyperventilation as a routine reduces cerebral blood flow and risks ischaemia
- Pressure on the eyes or endotracheal tube from positioning
- Perioperative seizures — ensure an anticonvulsant plan is in place where indicated

## Thoracic Anaesthesia

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


**Core principle**

One-lung ventilation (OLV) creates an obligatory shunt. Hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction (HPV) partially compensates — do not blunt it with excessive volatile agent, over-distension, or alkalosis. High airway pressures cause lung injury; use lung-protective strategies throughout.

**What to do**

Isolate the lung with a double-lumen tube or bronchial blocker; confirm position with fibreoptic bronchoscopy. During OLV, use low tidal volumes, appropriate PEEP, and accept modest hypercapnia. Good analgesia — epidural, paravertebral, or erector spinae plane block — is essential for respiratory recovery.

**What to avoid and watch for**

- **Hypoxaemia on OLV** — check tube position first, increase FiO₂, apply CPAP to the non-dependent lung or PEEP to the dependent lung, or briefly reinstate two-lung ventilation; involve the surgeon
- Major haemorrhage — know the major transfusion protocol
- Postoperative respiratory failure — plan for HDU or ICU in advance

## Cardiac and Major Vascular (Awareness for the Novice)

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


You will not lead these cases in your first 18 months. Understand the physiology.

Aortic cross-clamping sharply increases afterload; declamping causes vasodilation, acidaemia, and hypotension. Heparinisation and reversal with protamine are standard; protamine reactions can be severe. EVAR carries contrast nephropathy and vascular access complication risks.

**Your role**

Meticulous arterial line and large-bore IV placement under supervision. Vigilance for ischaemia on ECG, urine output, temperature, and glucose.

**What to avoid**

Underestimating bleeding and coagulopathy. Request early point-of-care or viscoelastic testing and follow the local massive transfusion pathway.

## ENT and Shared Airway

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


**Core principle**

You share the airway with the surgeon. Communication is a safety-critical act. The airway is often the pathology itself.

**What to do**

Plan A to D per DAS guidance before you start. Agree "down time" with the surgeon explicitly. Document throat packs in and out — this is a never event if forgotten. For laser surgery: reduce FiO₂, avoid nitrous oxide, use laser-safe tubes, and keep saline on the field (fire requires fuel, oxidiser, and ignition — remove at least one).

**What to avoid and watch for**

- **Post-tonsillectomy bleeding** — treat as a full stomach with an unstable airway; resuscitate, get senior help, have suction ready
- **Jet ventilation** — chest movement and end-tidal CO₂ are unreliable; barotrauma is a real risk; close team coordination is essential
- Airway oedema after prolonged airway surgery — consider a leak test before extubation and HDU admission

## Maxillofacial and Dental

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Trauma distorts anatomy. Awake fibreoptic intubation or a surgical airway plan may be needed. Nasal intubation is common — check for base-of-skull fracture first. Secure the airway before intermaxillary fixation. Keep wire cutters at the bedside. Document throat packs. Plan extubation carefully with the surgeon present if there is any concern.

**Watch for** bleeding and aspiration. Reintubation after fixation is very difficult.

## Trauma and Orthopaedics

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Treat every trauma patient as a full stomach until proven otherwise. Protect the C-spine if indicated. Resuscitate with balanced blood product transfusion per the major transfusion protocol — damage control resuscitation.

**Hip fracture** — spinal and GA are both acceptable; choose based on patient, procedure, and your skill level. Multimodal analgesia and regional nerve blocks reduce pain and delirium.

**Tourniquet** — limit inflation time per local policy. Expect hypertension and pain even under regional anaesthesia. Anticipate hypotension on deflation.

**Bone cement implantation syndrome** — increases FiO₂ during cementing, reaming, and pressurisation; be prepared for rapid haemodynamic deterioration.

**Watch for** fat embolism syndrome (hypoxia, confusion, petechiae) — supportive care, high FiO₂. Prevent positioning injuries; protect eyes, nerves, and pressure points meticulously.

## General Surgery and Laparoscopy

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Pneumoperitoneum reduces respiratory compliance and venous return, raises airway pressures, and increases ICP. CO₂ absorption causes hypercapnia — increase minute ventilation to compensate. Trendelenburg exaggerates all of these.

**What to do**

Use RSI if aspiration risk exists. Decompress the stomach with an orogastric tube after induction. Use lung-protective ventilation throughout. Give multimodal PONV prophylaxis. Consider TAP or rectus sheath blocks for analgesia.

**Watch for** facial and airway oedema after steep Trendelenburg — be cautious at extubation. Liaise with the surgeon if insufflation pressures are high.

## Urology

**TURP/TURBT** — irrigation fluid absorption causes TUR syndrome: fluid overload, electrolyte disturbance, and neurological changes. Spinal anaesthesia allows early detection of mental state changes. Monitor haemodynamics and electrolytes closely. Risk of significant postoperative bleeding.

**Robotic prostatectomy** — prolonged steep Trendelenburg and pneumoperitoneum; anticipate glottic and airway oedema; extubate with caution.

**Sedation for ESWL/endoscopy** — screen for OSA; have a clear airway rescue plan in a potentially remote environment.

**Watch for** laser safety issues (same principles as ENT). Elderly patients are at high risk of delirium and cardiac events.

## Plastics and Burns

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


**Burns** — assess the airway early. Inhalational injury evolves over hours; have a low threshold for early intubation before oedema makes it impossible. Use a burns-specific fluid resuscitation formula — avoid over-resuscitation. Hypothermia is a constant threat; warm aggressively.

**Free flaps** — perfusion is everything. Avoid hypotension and vasoconstriction. Regional anaesthesia promotes flap flow. Follow the surgeon's anticoagulation instructions.

**Tumescent local anaesthesia** — large volumes of local anaesthetic are used. Calculate the maximum safe dose; monitor for LAST throughout.

**Watch for** difficult IV access, distorted anatomy, and pressure injuries in long cases.

## Ophthalmic

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


**Core principle**

Avoid anything that raises intraocular pressure — coughing, straining, external pressure. An open globe is a surgical emergency with a full-stomach management risk.

**What to do**

Manage elderly comorbidity carefully with a minimal disturbance technique. Regional eye blocks are effective but carry serious risks (globe perforation, retrobulbar haemorrhage, brainstem spread) — have resuscitation equipment ready and monitor continuously.

**Oculocardiac reflex** — traction on extraocular muscles causes bradycardia. Ask the surgeon to stop, deepen anaesthesia, and give an anticholinergic if it persists.

**Open globe** — avoid suxamethonium if possible given fasciculation-related IOP rise; ensure a smooth RSI.

## Bariatric and the Patient with Obesity

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


**Core principle**

Reduced functional residual capacity means rapid desaturation. OSA and obesity hypoventilation syndrome are common. The airway is more likely to be difficult. Drug pharmacokinetics are altered — dose using the appropriate weight scalar for each drug and check a reference.

**What to do**

Position in the ramped position. Preoxygenate thoroughly — consider HFNO or CPAP. Have a clear difficult-airway plan; use apnoeic oxygenation. Use multimodal, opioid-sparing analgesia. Ensure VTE prophylaxis per protocol.

**Watch for** postoperative hypoventilation and airway obstruction — use CPAP early and be cautious with opioids. Rhabdomyolysis and pressure injuries can occur in prolonged cases.

## Remote Sites: Radiology, Endoscopy, ECT, MRI

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


**Core principle**

The standard of care is identical to theatre. Remote locations mean less help, awkward patient access, and specific environmental hazards.

**What to do**

Bring the theatre to the patient: full monitoring, complete airway kit, emergency drugs, suction, and backup oxygen. In MRI, use only MRI-compatible equipment; use long breathing circuits; protect the patient's hearing. In endoscopy, aspiration and airway obstruction risk under deep sedation is significant — consider GA with airway protection when risk is high. For ECT, provide brief GA with muscle relaxation; manage the autonomic swings; protect teeth and airway; observe mental health legislation requirements.

**Watch for**

- Radiation exposure — time, distance, and shielding
- Sedation creep — follow Association of Anaesthetists and Academy of Medical Royal Colleges sedation standards; always have a rescue plan before you start

## Regional and Neuraxial Across Specialties

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Regional anaesthesia improves analgesia and reduces opioid use and complications. It is not risk-free.

Obtain proper consent. Manage anticoagulants per national guidance — timing around neuraxial procedures is critical. Use ultrasound guidance as the default where feasible. Calculate local anaesthetic doses carefully to avoid LAST.

**Watch for**

- **LAST** — recognise early (neurological then cardiovascular symptoms) and treat immediately with intralipid per the AAGBI/Association of Anaesthetists protocol
- Epidural haematoma — a neurosurgical emergency; know the symptoms and escalate immediately
- Interscalene block causes ipsilateral diaphragmatic paresis — avoid in patients with significant respiratory compromise
- Block failure — always have a rescue plan

## Cross-cutting Special Populations

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


**Frailty and older patients** — optimise physiology, not just numbers. Prevent delirium: treat pain, maintain sleep patterns, restore vision and hearing aids early, minimise benzodiazepines and unnecessary lines.

**Diabetes** — individualise fasting and insulin management per local protocol. Risk of delayed gastric emptying. Monitor glucose and potassium perioperatively.

**OSA** — screen proactively. Use opioid-sparing analgesia. Extubate fully awake. Start CPAP early postoperatively. Consider a monitored bed.

**Malignant hyperthermia risk** — use a trigger-free technique. Check dantrolene is available and in date. Prepare the anaesthetic machine per published guidelines before the case.

Brief the surgical and theatre team before every case: anticipated key events, airway-sharing plan, expected blood loss, positioning risks, and special equipment.
