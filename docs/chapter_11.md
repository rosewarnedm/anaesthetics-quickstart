# Chapter 11: Perioperative Medicine

Perioperative medicine is about getting the right patient to the right operation at the right time, in the best possible condition, with a clear plan for recovery. It is a team sport. Your role as the anaesthetist: assess, optimise, communicate risk, and shepherd the patient through the whole pathway.

**The core principle:** oxygen delivery (DO₂) = cardiac output × arterial oxygen content. Most perioperative harm flows from inadequate DO₂, infection, thrombosis, bleeding, pain, delirium, or medication error. Keep this framework in mind throughout.

## Pre-assessment: What You Must Cover

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


A thorough pre-assessment prevents avoidable surprises in theatre. Cover:

**History:** Surgical indication and urgency. Comorbidities and previous anaesthetics. Airway concerns. Exercise tolerance and functional capacity. Frailty, falls, cognition. Bleeding or clotting history. Allergies — specifically drugs, latex, and chlorhexidine. Aspiration risk (reflux, gastroparesis, diabetes, obesity, pregnancy).

**Medications:** Review everything. Pay particular attention to cardiovascular drugs, anticoagulants and antiplatelets, diabetes therapies (especially insulin and SGLT2 inhibitors), steroids, Parkinson's drugs, antiepileptics, antidepressants, opioids, methadone or buprenorphine, CPAP, and the contraceptive pill or HRT.

**Examination:** Airway assessment. Cardiorespiratory examination. Signs of heart failure or fluid overload. BMI and neck circumference. Peripheral access. Note venous anatomy if central access may be needed.

**Investigations:** Order targeted tests only when the result will change management. Useful tests include ECG, FBC, renal function and electrolytes, group and screen, HbA1c, iron studies, and echo if there are new or worsening cardiac symptoms. Avoid untargeted panels — if you would not act on an abnormal result, do not request the test.

**Risk stratification:** Use ASA physical status, procedure-specific risk tools (including emergency laparotomy scores), frailty scores, and OSA screening (e.g. STOP-Bang). These inform postoperative level-of-care decisions and guide shared decision-making — they are not gatekeepers.

**Common pitfalls:**
- Ordering tests without a plan for acting on the results
- Under-recognising frailty or cognitive vulnerability
- Missing high-risk physiology: pulmonary hypertension, severe aortic stenosis, decompensated heart failure

## Optimisation: What Moves the Needle

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


### Cardiovascular

Continue beta-blockers and antianginals — do not stop them, and never start beta-blockade for the first time just before surgery. New or worsening cardiac symptoms need proper assessment before proceeding with elective surgery. Recent coronary stents require careful antiplatelet planning with cardiology and the surgical team.

Decompensated heart failure is a red flag. Stabilise before elective surgery. For valvular disease, severe symptomatic aortic stenosis or mitral stenosis carries high risk — obtain an echo if clinically suspected and involve cardiology.

For arrhythmias, ensure rate or rhythm control and review anticoagulation. For pacemakers and ICDs, identify the device type and arrange magnet availability or reprogramming with the device team before surgery.

**Principle:** stable preload and afterload, sinus rhythm where possible, no tachycardia, no sustained hypotension.

### Respiratory

Optimise inhalers before surgery. Treat active exacerbations; do not proceed electively through a flare. Encourage smoking cessation — any reduction helps, and stopping several weeks out meaningfully reduces complications. Consider prehabilitation for deconditioned patients.

Screen for OSA using a validated tool. Ensure patients continue CPAP. Plan opioid-sparing analgesia and appropriate postoperative monitoring.

Pulmonary hypertension is high-risk physiology. Avoid hypoxia, hypercarbia, acidosis, hypothermia, and uncontrolled pain — all increase pulmonary vascular resistance and can precipitate right ventricular failure. Involve the specialist team early.

### Diabetes and Endocrine

Aim for reasonable glycaemic control perioperatively — avoid both hyperglycaemia and hypoglycaemia. Plan list order so diabetic patients are not left fasting unnecessarily long. Use a variable-rate intravenous insulin infusion (VRIII) where indicated per local protocol. Monitor capillary glucose throughout.

**SGLT2 inhibitors must be withheld before surgery** — they carry a risk of euglycaemic diabetic ketoacidosis even with normal glucose levels. Confirm the local timing guidance.

Insulin pump or closed-loop system users need a clear plan agreed with the diabetes team beforehand.

Patients on long-term steroids may need perioperative supplementation — follow local protocol. Severe untreated thyroid disease increases risk; treat before elective surgery if possible.

### Renal

CKD increases the risks of fluid overload, hyperkalaemia, metabolic acidosis, and bleeding. Dose-adjust renally cleared drugs. Avoid nephrotoxins. Plan dialysis timing around surgery. Protect arteriovenous fistulas: no blood pressure measurements or cannulae on that arm.

### Haematology and Blood Conservation

Preoperative anaemia is common and worsens outcomes. Identify the cause. Iron deficiency is frequently correctable — intravenous iron is an option when timelines are short. Follow patient blood management principles and avoid reflexive transfusion.

For sickle cell disease or thalassaemia, involve haematology early. Maintain oxygenation, normothermia, hydration, and analgesia throughout — sickling crises are triggered by physiological stress.

### Frailty, Cognition, and Nutrition

Frailty independently predicts postoperative harm. Screen routinely. Tailor care: involve therapists early, simplify medication regimens, and put delirium prevention measures in place from the outset.

Malnutrition increases complications. Involve dietitians and encourage protein-rich nutritional supplementation as prehabilitation where time allows.

Screen for alcohol dependence. If present, arrange withdrawal prophylaxis and thiamine supplementation. Aim for preoperative reduction or abstinence where feasible.

## Medication Management: Safe Principles

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


**Continue:** beta-blockers, antianginals, inhalers, antiepileptics, antidepressants (check for serotonergic interactions), Parkinson's drugs, chronic steroids, and CPAP.

**Parkinson's drugs must not be withheld for prolonged periods.** Missing doses can cause rigidity, aspiration, delirium, and a neuroleptic malignant syndrome-like state. Use enteral or transdermal routes if the patient is nil by mouth.

**Consider withholding or modifying:**

- **ACE inhibitors/ARBs:** Individualise based on haemodynamics and surgical magnitude. Either way, have a plan for intraoperative blood pressure management.
- **SGLT2 inhibitors:** Stop preoperatively per local guidance.
- **Metformin:** Follow local guidance regarding fasting, renal function, and contrast.
- **Anticoagulants and antiplatelets:** Timing of interruption depends on the drug, renal function, thrombotic risk, and procedural bleeding risk. For neuraxial and regional anaesthesia, follow Association of Anaesthetists and RA-UK timing guidance precisely. Involve haematology or cardiology for complex cases.
- **Warfarin:** Plan cessation and bridging where indicated; confirm INR before proceeding.
- **Biologics and immunosuppressants:** Dose timing around surgery requires balancing infection risk against disease flare — involve the prescribing team.
- **HRT/OCP:** Consider VTE risk relative to surgical risk; follow local policy.

For patients on chronic opioids, do not stop abruptly. Anticipate tolerance and opioid-induced hyperalgesia. Plan multimodal and regional analgesia and coordinate with the pain service. Buprenorphine and methadone require a specific perioperative plan agreed with addiction or pain specialists.

**Key pitfalls:**
- Forgetting to stop SGLT2 inhibitors → euglycaemic DKA
- Withholding Parkinson's drugs → rigidity, delirium, NMS-like crisis
- Stopping beta-blockers → rebound ischaemia
- Restarting anticoagulants without considering neuraxial timing → epidural haematoma
- Failing to identify chlorhexidine or latex allergy

## Fasting and Aspiration Risk

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Follow Association of Anaesthetists fasting guidance. Liberal clear fluids until a short interval before anaesthesia improves patient comfort and reduces perioperative hypotension.

Solid food requires a longer fast. Modify for fatty meals, gastroparesis, pregnancy, obesity, diabetes, or significant oesophageal pathology.

If increased aspiration risk is present, plan for a rapid sequence induction, head-up positioning, and suction immediately available. Gastric point-of-care ultrasound can be helpful if you are trained in its use.

Carbohydrate drinks are appropriate as part of an enhanced recovery pathway where there are no contraindications.

## Infection and VTE Prophylaxis

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


**Antibiotic prophylaxis:** Give the correct agent at the correct dose and timing — typically within 60 minutes of incision, per local microbiology guidance. Redose intraoperatively for prolonged procedures. Always check for relevant allergies, including chlorhexidine.

**VTE prophylaxis:** Assess individual risk. Provide mechanical prophylaxis (compression stockings, pneumatic devices) and pharmacological prophylaxis as indicated. Coordinate timing carefully with neuraxial and regional anaesthesia plans, following Association of Anaesthetists and RA-UK guidance.

## Blood Management

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Treat anaemia before surgery where possible. Intraoperative cell salvage is appropriate for high-blood-loss procedures. Use antifibrinolytics for major surgery per guideline. Minimise iatrogenic losses.

**Massive haemorrhage:** Know your hospital protocol. Call for help early. Early balanced component therapy, calcium replacement, temperature management, and point-of-care coagulation testing are the pillars of management.

For patients who decline blood products (including Jehovah's Witnesses), have a detailed documented discussion preoperatively about which interventions are acceptable. Do not leave this until crisis point.

## Intraoperative Bundle: Safe Defaults

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


**Monitoring:** Standard monitoring as a minimum. Add invasive monitoring for high-risk patients or major surgery — calibrate your monitoring to the clinical question.

**Haemodynamics:** Maintain perfusion pressure to protect heart, brain, and kidneys. Treat the cause of hypotension: inadequate preload, excessive anaesthetic depth, vasodilation, or arrhythmia. Use balanced crystalloids; avoid large volumes of chloride-rich fluid.

**Fluids:** Distinguish maintenance from resuscitation. Target euvolaemia. Fluid overload is not safer than fluid restriction — positive balance is independently harmful. Use dynamic assessments of fluid responsiveness in major surgery where available.

**Temperature:** Start active warming from induction. Do not let the patient cool. Hypothermia increases bleeding, infection risk, arrhythmia, and recovery time.

**Analgesia:** Multimodal as the default. Paracetamol, NSAIDs or COX-2 inhibitors (if safe), regional or neuraxial techniques, and appropriate adjuncts. Minimise systemic opioids, particularly in patients with OSA, frailty, or chronic opioid use.

**PONV:** Assess risk before anaesthesia. Give multimodal prophylaxis using agents from different classes. Avoid emetogenic triggers where possible. Ensure adequate hydration and analgesia.

**Glycaemia:** Monitor and manage to avoid extremes. Use local insulin protocols.

**Antibiotics:** Before incision. Redose when indicated.

**Common pitfalls:**
- Inducing a haemodynamically unstable or high-risk patient without a vasopressor immediately available
- Giving excess fluid thinking it is the safe choice — it is not
- Allowing the patient to become hypothermic after induction
- Omitting PONV prophylaxis in high-risk patients

## Postoperative Plan and Care

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


**Level of care:** Base this on preoperative risk, surgical magnitude, and intraoperative events. Do not assume the ward is always appropriate. Early senior review of deteriorating patients reduces failure-to-rescue.

**PACU:** Give a structured handover — use SBAR or local equivalent. Discharge from recovery should be criteria-based, with clear instructions for ward monitoring and escalation thresholds.

**Early complications to anticipate:**

- *Airway and respiratory:* Obstruction, hypoventilation (OSA, opioids), atelectasis, bronchospasm, aspiration
- *Cardiovascular:* Haemorrhage, hypotension, arrhythmia, myocardial infarction, heart failure
- *Renal:* AKI from hypoperfusion, nephrotoxin exposure, or sepsis
- *Neurological:* Delirium, stroke, prolonged regional block
- *Pain and PONV:* Reassess and treat proactively — undertreated pain drives most other complications
- *Temperature:* Monitor and maintain normothermia

**Delirium:** Anticipate it rather than react to it. Non-pharmacological prevention is first-line: reorientate, ensure glasses and hearing aids are in, maintain a day-night cycle, give adequate analgesia without benzodiazepines, mobilise early, manage bowel and bladder, and treat reversible causes (infection, withdrawal, metabolic disturbance). Use validated screening tools. Pharmacological treatment is reserved for when non-pharmacological measures are insufficient or the patient is at risk of harm.

**OSA:** Resume CPAP postoperatively as soon as feasible. Use opioid-sparing analgesia. Consider high-dependency monitoring for patients with severe OSA, especially after major surgery.

**Mobilisation and nutrition:** Start early feeding if safe. Remove lines, urinary catheters, and drains as soon as possible — they restrict mobility and introduce infection risk. Prevent constipation and ileus with a proactive bowel regimen.

**Thromboprophylaxis:** Restart once haemostasis is secure. Align timing with any neuraxial or regional techniques still in place.

**Handover to ward and primary care:** Summarise what happened, what to watch for, medication changes made, follow-up required, and any pending results. Be explicit — do not assume the ward team will piece it together.

## Special Situations You Should Recognise Early

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


**Difficult airway or high aspiration risk:** Plan with DAS principles. Have your backup plans and equipment ready before induction, not after a failed attempt.

**Pulmonary hypertension and right heart failure:** Avoid all pulmonary vasoconstrictors — hypoxia, hypercarbia, acidosis, hypothermia, and pain. Maintain right ventricular coronary perfusion pressure. Involve the specialist team preoperatively.

**Myasthenia gravis and other neuromuscular disorders:** These patients have unpredictable sensitivity to neuromuscular blocking drugs. Prefer regional techniques where feasible. Plan postoperative ventilatory support if indicated.

**Malignant hyperthermia (MH):** Know the triggering agents (volatile agents and suxamethonium). Take a family history — it matters. Have dantrolene immediately available. Use a trigger-free technique in susceptible patients and refer for MH investigation postoperatively.

**Immunosuppressed patients and transplant recipients:** High infection risk
