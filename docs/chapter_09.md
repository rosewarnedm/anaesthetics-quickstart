## Principles and First Moves

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


See something wrong — say it out loud and hit the buzzer. Early help is never embarrassing; delayed help is dangerous.

Your default response to almost any crisis is the same: **100% oxygen, hand-ventilate, check capnography, call for help.** These four actions buy time for everything else.

Capnography is your truth source. A flat trace means oesophageal intubation, circuit disconnection, or cardiac arrest until proven otherwise — never assume it is a monitor fault.

If the machine is suspect, abandon it. Grab a self-inflating bag and a cylinder. Fix the patient before you fix the equipment.

Allocate roles clearly. Use closed-loop communication ("Can you draw up adrenaline?" — "I'm drawing up adrenaline now" — "Adrenaline is drawn up"). Use cognitive aids — DAS cards, anaphylaxis algorithms, ALS cards — without embarrassment. That is what they are for.

Maintain anaesthesia throughout. A paralysed, aware patient is an avoidable catastrophe.

## Recognising Deterioration

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Act on trends, not just absolute numbers. Worry early about:

- Falling SpO₂ or ETCO₂
- Rising peak airway pressures
- Hypotension or bradycardia that was not there before
- New wheeze, rash, or urticaria
- Unexpected rigidity or rising temperature
- Alarms from pumps or ventilators

Always ask: is this the **patient** or the **equipment**? Hand-ventilate, feel the chest, palpate the pulse, check capnography. These three steps clarify almost everything quickly.

## Airway Emergencies

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


### Loss of Airway or Ventilation

Clues: desaturation, absent or lost ETCO₂, silent or obstructed chest, paradoxical movement.

Act immediately:
1. Call for help. Give 100% O₂.
2. Two-handed mask seal, insert airway adjuncts, optimise head position.
3. If laryngospasm or biting is the cause, consider neuromuscular blockade.
4. If intubation is needed, use videolaryngoscopy early. Limit attempts — each attempt risks oedema and bleeding, making the next attempt harder.
5. If intubation fails, place a supraglottic airway (SGA).
6. If you cannot intubate **and** cannot oxygenate (CICO): perform emergency front-of-neck access (FONA) per DAS guidance without delay.

The DAS adult difficult airway algorithm must be in your head before you step foot in theatre. Know where your FONA kit is kept.

### Laryngospasm

Partial: stridor. Complete: silence, paradoxical chest movement, rapidly falling SpO₂.

Respond with jaw thrust, 100% O₂ with CPAP, remove the stimulus, and deepen anaesthesia. If this fails or SpO₂ is falling fast, give a neuromuscular blocker — do not wait. After resolution, watch for negative-pressure pulmonary oedema (crackles, pink frothy secretions, hypoxia).

### Bronchospasm

Clues: wheeze, shark-fin ETCO₂ waveform, high airway pressures, poor tidal volumes.

Before treating bronchospasm, exclude tube obstruction or kink — suction and check first. Then give 100% O₂, deepen anaesthesia, and use bronchodilators per your local protocol. If there is also hypotension or a rash, treat for anaphylaxis.

### Aspiration

Head down, suction the oropharynx. If the patient is not intubated, intubate and suction the trachea. Ventilate with lung-protective settings and PEEP. Routine prophylactic antibiotics are not indicated for chemical pneumonitis — treat only if infection supervenes.

## Breathing: Hypoxia and Hypercapnia

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Use **DOPES** as a mental checklist:

| Letter | Cause | Quick check |
|--------|-------|-------------|
| **D** | Displacement (endobronchial or oesophageal) | Check tube depth, auscultate, capnography |
| **O** | Obstruction (kink, secretions, biting) | Suction, bite block, change tube |
| **P** | Pneumothorax | Unequal chest, hyper-resonance, tracheal deviation — decompress urgently |
| **E** | Equipment failure | Hand-ventilate, swap circuit |
| **S** | Stacked breaths / air trapping | Lengthen expiratory time, briefly disconnect |

A sudden drop in ETCO₂ with hypotension should make you think **pulmonary embolus or gas embolus** — support circulation, give 100% O₂, and call for help immediately.

## Circulation: Hypotension and Shock

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Confirm the reading is real. Check ETCO₂ — a low reading often reflects low cardiac output, not just ventilation.

Pattern-match quickly:

- **Vasodilation** — anaesthetic depth, neuraxial block, sepsis
- **Hypovolaemia** — haemorrhage, inadequate filling
- **Obstructive** — tension pneumothorax, tamponade, PE
- **Cardiogenic** — ischaemia, arrhythmia
- **Anaphylaxis** — see below
- **High neuraxial block** — see below

Safe defaults while you think: reduce anaesthetic depth if appropriate, give a cautious fluid bolus, start a vasopressor, ask the surgeon to pause, and send a blood gas (Hb, potassium, calcium, lactate).

Do not chase numbers without a diagnosis. Do not miss occult haemorrhage. Do not forget calcium during massive transfusion.

### Bradycardia

Common causes: surgical traction, vagal stimulus, high spinal, hypoxia. Treat the cause first. Follow the Resuscitation Council UK bradycardia algorithm for haemodynamic instability.

### Tachyarrhythmias

Correct reversible causes — pain, hypovolaemia, hypoxia, hypercapnia, electrolyte disturbance — before reaching for antiarrhythmics. Unstable tachyarrhythmia requires synchronised cardioversion with senior help.

### Cardiac Arrest in Theatre

Call the arrest, start ALS, and think about **theatre-specific reversible causes**:
- Tension pneumothorax
- Pulmonary or gas embolus
- Massive haemorrhage
- Electrolyte crisis
- Local anaesthetic toxicity
- Malignant hyperthermia
- High spinal
- Anaphylaxis

High-quality, uninterrupted CPR. Designate a team leader. Defibrillator must be immediately to hand in every theatre.

## Specific Crises You Must Know

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


### Anaphylaxis

Clues: sudden cardiovascular collapse, bronchospasm, rising airway pressures, flushing, urticaria, angio-oedema — often in combination.

Act:
1. Stop all likely triggers.
2. Call for help. Secure the airway early — oedema progresses fast.
3. Give adrenaline per Association of Anaesthetists guidance. Do not delay.
4. Give high-volume IV fluids and adjunct drugs per guideline.
5. Take serial tryptase samples at the correct time intervals per guideline.
6. Document agents given and the exact timeline. Refer for allergy follow-up. Report the reaction through national surveillance systems.

The commonest pitfall is delayed adrenaline. It is the first-line treatment — not antihistamines, not steroids.

Watch for **biphasic reactions**: the patient can deteriorate again hours later.

### Malignant Hyperthermia (MH)

Triggers: volatile agents and suxamethonium.

The earliest reliable sign is an **unexplained rapid rise in ETCO₂** despite increased minute ventilation. Tachycardia, rigidity, hyperkalaemia, and acidosis follow. Temperature rise is a **late** sign — do not wait for it.

Act:
1. Declare MH. Call for the MH kit and allocate specific roles immediately.
2. Stop all triggers. Switch to a clean circuit or apply activated charcoal filters per local policy.
3. Give dantrolene urgently — this is life-saving.
4. Active cooling. Treat hyperkalaemia and acidosis. Plan ICU admission.
5. Contact the UK MH Investigation Unit for advice and to arrange testing for the patient and family.

Do not attribute an unexplained ETCO₂ rise to light anaesthesia or CO₂ rebreathing without excluding MH. Know where your dantrolene is kept before you start any trigger-containing anaesthetic.

### Local Anaesthetic Systemic Toxicity (LAST)

Presentation follows a spectrum: perioral tingling, metallic taste, tinnitus, agitation → seizures → cardiovascular collapse → cardiac arrest.

Act:
1. Stop the LA injection. Call for help.
2. Secure the airway and oxygenate.
3. Control seizures with a benzodiazepine.
4. Start **lipid emulsion therapy** per RA-UK protocol immediately.
5. Modified ALS if arrest occurs. Prolonged resuscitation may be needed.

Prevention: calculate maximum safe dose using appropriate weight, aspirate before injection, use incremental dosing, use ultrasound guidance, know where your intralipid is kept. Under-recognition and delayed lipid therapy are the commonest errors.

### High Neuraxial Block / Total Spinal

Risk situations: epidural top-ups, accidental intrathecal injection, obstetrics.

Clues: rapidly ascending sensory block, weak or absent arms, hoarse or absent voice, dyspnoea, hypotension, bradycardia, apnoea, loss of consciousness.

Act:
1. Call for help immediately.
2. In pregnancy: left uterine displacement.
3. 100% O₂. Support ventilation early — do not wait for apnoea.
4. Vasopressors and fluids per local guidance. Atropine for bradycardia.
5. If the patient is conscious, reassure them clearly and repeatedly that the paralysis is temporary.

Prevention: always fractionate epidural doses, test catheter position before a top-up, and never give a large rapid bolus.

### Massive Haemorrhage

Recognise early: look at the surgical field, suction canisters, swabs — do not wait for the monitor to alarm.

Act:
1. Activate the Massive Haemorrhage Protocol. Communicate with theatre, blood bank, and porters simultaneously.
2. Gain large-bore IV access rapidly.
3. Transfuse balanced blood components. Give tranexamic acid early per protocol.
4. Warm all fluids and the patient actively.
5. Monitor blood gas frequently — correct temperature, calcium, potassium, and coagulopathy proactively.

The **lethal triad** — hypothermia, acidosis, coagulopathy — is a self-reinforcing cycle that kills. Break it early. Remember that massive transfusion causes citrate-induced hypocalcaemia; replace calcium.

### Gas or Air Embolism

Risk situations: sitting neurosurgical cases, laparoscopy, central line insertion or removal, hepatic surgery.

Clues: sudden change in ETCO₂ (CO₂ embolus may cause a transient rise then fall), hypotension, hypoxia, "mill-wheel" murmur, arrhythmias.

Act:
1. Call for help. Give 100% O₂.
2. Stop insufflation or air entry; flood the surgical field with saline.
3. Aspirate via a central venous catheter if in situ and appropriately positioned.
4. Position left lateral head-down if feasible.
5. Support circulation; start CPR if needed.

Do not continue insufflation while the patient is compromised.

### Neck Haematoma After Thyroid or Neck Surgery

Clues: swelling, stridor, dysphagia, distress, venous engorgement.

Act fast: **open the wound at the bedside** — release sutures and clips to decompress. This can be immediately life-saving. Call for senior airway help and the operating surgeon simultaneously. Prepare for a potentially very difficult airway. Re-intubate early if in doubt; waiting risks complete loss of the airway.

### Awareness During Anaesthesia

Risk situations: TIVA with disconnected or kinked cannula, pump failure, empty vaporiser, paralysis without confirmed adequate hypnosis.

If suspected intra-operatively: give additional hypnotic and analgesic agent promptly. Tell the patient clearly afterwards what happened, that you are sorry, and that support is available. Document fully and arrange structured follow-up.

Prevention: always monitor end-tidal agent concentration with alarms set, use reliable IV access and anti-siphon valves for TIVA, and apply your pre-list checklist properly.

### Hyperkalaemia and Drug-Related Crises

Hyperkalaemia causes bradycardia, peaked T waves, widened QRS, and asystole. Treat per your local emergency protocol.

Suxamethonium in patients with burns, denervation injury, prolonged immobility, or neuromuscular disease can cause life-threatening hyperkalaemia. Know your contraindications and use an alternative.

In unexplained deterioration, always check glucose early — hypoglycaemia is common and easily missed.

### Airway Fire

High FiO₂ combined with diathermy or laser near airway equipment is the risk scenario.

Act: stop O₂ and N₂O immediately, remove the burning material (including the tracheal tube if burning), extinguish with saline, re-establish the airway, assess injury (bronchoscopy), provide supportive care. For high-risk procedures, minimise FiO₂ before activating the ignition source.

## Equipment and Gas Supply Failures

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Trust your oxygen analyser and capnography over your instincts about the machine.

- **Ventilator failure**: switch to manual ventilation. If still unsafe, self-inflating bag with cylinder O₂.
- **Suspected pipeline failure or cross-contamination**: open your O₂ cylinder fully, disconnect the wall pipeline to prevent backflow, and verify with the oxygen analyser.
- **Circuit disconnection or misassembly**: hand-ventilate and replace the circuit. Check one-way valves and CO₂ absorber.
- **Power failure**: activate battery backups. Be ready to bag manually and provide hand suction.

The cardinal error is spending time troubleshooting the machine whilst the patient desaturates. **Treat the patient first.**

## Monitoring Clues You Must Read

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


**Capnography waveform patterns:**

| Pattern | Think |
|---------|-------|
| Flat trace | Oesophageal intubation, disconnection, cardiac arrest |
| Sudden fall | PE, gas embolus, severe hypotension, disconnection |
| Rising baseline | Exhausted CO₂ absorber, rebreathing |
| Shark-fin | Bronchospasm, airway obstruction |

**Pulse oximetry:** lags behind real-time events by 20–30 seconds. Poor perfusion generates artefact. Always correlate with a palpable pulse.

**Blood pressure:** oscillometric NIBP becomes unreliable in shocked patients. Place an arterial line early if haemodynamic instability is ongoing.

## Post-Anaesthesia Care Unit (PACU) Crises

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Problems do not stop when the patient leaves theatre. Common PACU emergencies:

- **Airway obstruction** from sedation or residual neuromuscular block: airway manoeuvres, adjuncts, reversal agent if appropriate.
- **Laryngospasm or stridor**: treat as intra-operatively; consider nebulised adrenaline per local guidance.
- **Opioid-induced respiratory depression**: stimulate, give oxygen, titrate reversal per local protocol, monitor closely afterwards.
- **Surgical bleeding or haematoma** (tonsil, thyroid, carotid): call the surgeon immediately and have a low threshold for returning to theatre.
- **Emergence delirium**: first exclude hypoxia, hypercarbia, pain, and hypoglycaemia before attributing to anaesthetic agents.
- **PONV with aspiration risk**: protect the airway, suction, treat with antiemetics per protocol.
