## Core Principles You Will Use Daily

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


### Pharmacodynamics — what the drug does to the body

Most anaesthetic drugs act at a small number of key receptors: GABA-A (propofol, volatiles, barbiturates, benzodiazepines all enhance inhibition here), NMDA (ketamine blocks it), mu-opioid receptors, and nicotinic acetylcholine receptors at the neuromuscular junction (NMBDs).

**Potency does not mean better.** Titrate to effect. Drugs frequently act synergistically — opioids reduce the volatile agent you need, which reduces haemodynamic instability.

**MAC** is a measure of volatile potency, not a target. Use end-tidal agent concentration, clinical signs, and processed EEG together. MAC falls with age, hypothermia, and co-administered sedatives or opioids.

### Pharmacokinetics — what the body does to the drug

After a bolus, the drug peaks in the brain *after* a delay. If you stack boluses before seeing the effect of the first, you will overshoot. Resist the urge.

**Context-sensitive half-time** means that offset depends on how long you have been infusing. Remifentanil clears rapidly regardless of infusion duration. Fentanyl and propofol accumulate — offset slows the longer you run them.

**Clearance pathways matter clinically:**
- High-extraction drugs (e.g. propofol) are flow-dependent — low cardiac output means less clearance and more drug effect.
- Capacity-limited drugs accumulate with liver disease or enzyme inhibition.
- Organ-independent metabolism is a safety advantage: remifentanil is cleared by plasma esterases; atracurium and cisatracurium undergo Hofmann elimination and ester hydrolysis. Both work predictably in organ failure.

**Protein binding and lipid solubility** are altered in hypoalbuminaemia, pregnancy, neonates, and the elderly — more free (active) drug for a given dose.

### Patient factors that shift drug response

| Population | Key consideration |
|---|---|
| Elderly | Increased sensitivity; slower clearance. Reduce doses and titrate slowly. |
| Obese | Dosing scalar varies by drug — check local guidance for total vs ideal vs lean body weight. |
| Pregnant | Reduced MAC; altered protein binding; physiological airway oedema; aspiration risk. |
| Renal/hepatic impairment | Prefer organ-independent clearance; watch for metabolite accumulation (e.g. morphine-6-glucuronide). |
| Chronic medications | SSRIs/SNRIs increase serotonin syndrome risk with tramadol; antihypertensives amplify hypotension; enzyme inducers or inhibitors alter drug offset. |

## Intravenous Induction and Sedation Agents

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


### Propofol

GABA-A potentiation. Gives hypnosis, amnesia, and a useful antiemetic effect.

**Cardiovascular effects are significant** — vasodilation and myocardial depression cause hypotension. This is exaggerated in hypovolaemia, sepsis, and the elderly. Have a vasopressor drawn up before you induce any sick or frail patient.

**Pain on injection** is common. Use a large forearm vein, give lidocaine or an opioid first, or both.

**TIVA hazards** deserve respect. Line disconnection causes awareness. Use dedicated labelled lines, anti-siphon valves, occlusion alarms, and monitor with end-tidal CO2 and processed EEG. Always have a volatile back-up plan.

### Thiopentone

Barbiturate, GABA-A. Preserves haemodynamics slightly better than propofol in some shocked patients; also antiepileptic. Avoid in porphyria. Repeated dosing causes prolonged somnolence — context-sensitive accumulation applies.

### Etomidate

GABA-A. Minimal cardiovascular depression — useful when haemodynamic instability is the overriding concern. However, even a single dose suppresses adrenal steroidogenesis. Use selectively; expect myoclonus and PONV.

### Ketamine

NMDA antagonism. Provides analgesia, amnesia, and dissociation. Stimulates the sympathetic nervous system — heart rate and blood pressure typically rise. Bronchodilates. Maintains respiratory drive and airway reflexes better than other agents (though not reliably — do not treat this as a guarantee).

Useful in haemodynamic instability, bronchospasm, and as an opioid-sparing adjunct. Be alert to salivation, emergence phenomena, and raised intracranial and intraocular pressure in susceptible patients. A benzodiazepine or anticholinergic co-administered where appropriate can mitigate these.

### Midazolam

GABA-A. Valuable for premedication, anxiolysis, amnesia, and seizure control. Respiratory depression is real — be cautious in the elderly, where it can cause prolonged confusion. Hepatic clearance means effect is prolonged in liver disease.

Flumazenil reverses benzodiazepines but has a shorter half-life than midazolam — re-sedation is a genuine risk. Its use in mixed overdose can precipitate seizures. Use with senior guidance.

### Dexmedetomidine

Alpha-2 agonist. Provides sedation with minimal respiratory depression and useful analgesic sparing. Main hazards are bradycardia and hypotension. Follow local policy regarding perioperative use.

## Opioids and Multimodal Analgesia

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


### Principle

No single analgesic is sufficient. Build a multimodal plan — combine opioid and non-opioid analgesics to improve analgesia and reduce side-effects of each component.

### Short-acting opioids

Fentanyl, alfentanil, and remifentanil all act at mu-receptors. They blunt the response to airway manipulation and provide intraoperative analgesia.

**Remifentanil** is cleared by plasma esterases regardless of infusion duration — rapid, predictable offset. This is its great strength and its trap. You must have a post-operative analgesic plan in place *before* emergence.

**Hazards common to this class:** respiratory depression, chest wall rigidity with rapid high-dose boluses, bradycardia, PONV, and opioid-induced hyperalgesia with prolonged high-dose infusions. Do not use opioid infusions to compensate for light anaesthesia — inadequate depth needs to be addressed directly.

### Longer-acting opioids

Morphine and oxycodone are mainstays of post-operative analgesia. Active metabolites accumulate in renal impairment — morphine in particular. Neuraxial opioids can cause delayed respiratory depression; ensure monitoring is appropriate.

### Tramadol

Mixed mechanism. Lowers the seizure threshold and has serotonergic activity — combining it with SSRIs, SNRIs, or MAOIs risks serotonin syndrome. Have a lower threshold to use an alternative when these drugs are prescribed.

### Naloxone

Reverses opioid effects but also reverses analgesia. Acute pain, hypertension, and pulmonary oedema can follow. Titrate carefully. The duration of action may be shorter than the opioid — observe for re-narcosis.

### Non-opioid analgesics

**Paracetamol** is a safe, effective cornerstone. Watch cumulative dosing and dose-reduce in significant liver disease.

**NSAIDs and COX-2 inhibitors** provide meaningful opioid-sparing but carry real risks: renal hypoperfusion (especially with hypovolaemia), GI bleeding, platelet dysfunction, and bronchospasm in sensitive asthmatics. Respect surgical bleeding concerns and local protocols.

**Adjuncts** — low-dose ketamine, alpha-2 agonists, and gabapentinoids — have a place in selected patients within a multimodal plan. Balance the analgesic benefit against sedation and other adverse effects.

## Neuromuscular Blocking Drugs and Reversal

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


### The key principle

**Quantitative neuromuscular monitoring is standard of care.** Use it to guide dosing and to confirm adequate recovery before extubation. This is an Association of Anaesthetists and RCoA expectation.

### Depolarising: Suxamethonium

Fast onset, short duration. The agent of choice for most rapid sequence inductions.

**Screen the history for contraindications:**
- Hyperkalaemia risk: burns, denervation, neuromuscular disease, crush injury, prolonged immobility, severe sepsis — upregulated acetylcholine receptors release potassium on depolarisation and can cause cardiac arrest.
- Pseudocholinesterase deficiency — prolonged block.
- Malignant hyperthermia susceptibility — absolute contraindication.
- Raises intragastric and intraocular pressure; causes masseter spasm and myalgia.

### Non-depolarising: Rocuronium, Vecuronium, Atracurium, Cisatracurium

Competitive antagonists at the nicotinic receptor.

**Rocuronium** has a fast onset at sufficient dose. Minimal histamine release. Reversed by sugammadex.

**Sugammadex** encapsulates aminosteroid NMBDs (rocuronium, vecuronium) and reverses even deep block reliably. Hazards: rare bradycardia or asystole; anaphylaxis; reduced efficacy in severe renal failure; recurarisation if underdosed. **It does not replace a failed-airway plan.** Sugammadex is not a substitute for front-of-neck access or DAS algorithms.

**Atracurium and cisatracurium** undergo Hofmann elimination and ester hydrolysis — organ-independent clearance makes them useful in renal and hepatic failure. Atracurium releases histamine at higher doses; cisatracurium does so much less. A laudanosine metabolite accumulates with very high or prolonged exposure but is rarely clinically significant.

### Reversal with Neostigmine

Inhibits acetylcholinesterase and enhances neuromuscular transmission. Requires some spontaneous recovery to be present — if the block is deep, neostigmine is unreliable and may paradoxically worsen weakness. Always pair with glycopyrrolate or atropine to prevent bradycardia and excess secretions. Confirm adequacy with quantitative monitoring before extubation.

## Inhalational Anaesthetics

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


### How uptake and offset work

Lower blood:gas solubility means faster equilibration between circuit and brain. Desflurane is fastest, then sevoflurane, then isoflurane. Higher alveolar ventilation speeds induction; high cardiac output slows it (more blood washes drug away from the lungs). Trend the end-tidal agent concentration — it reflects brain partial pressure with a lag.

### Sevoflurane

Smooth inhalational induction; low airway irritation; bronchodilator. Suitable for gas inductions. Follow local guidance on fresh gas flows in low-flow techniques.

### Desflurane

Very fast titration makes depth adjustment responsive. However, it causes airway irritation and sympathetic stimulation — do not use for induction or at light depth in an awake patient. High global warming potential; local sustainability policies may restrict or prohibit its routine use.

### Isoflurane

Reliable and cost-effective. Slower kinetics than sevoflurane; some airway irritation. Still in routine use.

### Nitrous oxide

Rapid onset and offset; useful analgesic properties. **Hazards:** expands closed gas spaces — avoid where there is pneumothorax, pneumocephalus, bowel obstruction, or middle-ear disease. Diffusion hypoxia occurs on discontinuation if supplemental oxygen is not given. Increases PONV. Interferes with B12-dependent metabolism with prolonged or repeated exposure.

### Safety habits

- Agent-specific keyed filling prevents wrong-agent errors. Do not tip vaporisers.
- Check for leaks as part of your machine check.
- Know how to deliver a trigger-free technique: all potent volatiles and suxamethonium trigger malignant hyperthermia. Know your hospital's MH protocol and how to prepare a clean machine.

## Local Anaesthetics

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


### Mechanism and classification

Local anaesthetics (LAs) block voltage-gated sodium channels. Smaller, unmyelinated fibres are blocked first — pain and temperature before motor function.

**Amides** (lidocaine, bupivacaine, levobupivacaine, ropivacaine, prilocaine) are metabolised hepatically. These are the agents you will use most.

**Esters** (chloroprocaine, tetracaine) are hydrolysed by plasma esterases. True LA allergy is more common with esters and often relates to a para-aminobenzoic acid metabolite. Most reported "LA allergy" is actually a reaction to preservatives or vasovagal in origin — but take any history seriously and clarify.

### Practical use

Adrenaline added to LA reduces systemic absorption, prolongs duration, and acts as a marker of intravascular injection (tachycardia). Avoid in end-artery territories and where circulation is compromised.

Ultrasound guidance improves block success and reduces the dose needed, but does not eliminate intravascular injection.

### Local Anaesthetic Systemic Toxicity (LAST)

**Prevent it:**
- Calculate and document the maximum safe dose before you start.
- Account for additive doses when performing multiple blocks or combining with infiltration.
- Aspirate before injecting; inject incrementally; maintain verbal contact; use ultrasound and inject with low pressure.

**Recognise it:**
Perioral tingling, tinnitus, metallic taste, agitation → seizures → cardiovascular collapse. With long-acting agents (bupivacaine), onset can be delayed by many minutes.

**Manage it:**
Stop injecting. Call for help. Airway, oxygenation, ventilation. Control seizures — avoid large propofol doses if the patient is haemodynamically compromised. Give lipid emulsion therapy per your local LAST protocol. Follow modified resuscitation algorithms (avoid large doses of vasopressin and calcium-channel blockers). Escalate to critical care and complete reporting.

### Neuraxial anaesthesia

Sympathetic block causes hypotension. In pregnancy, maintain left lateral tilt. Use fluids and vasopressors as indicated — vasopressors are first-line in most obstetric spinals.

High or total spinal results from excessive spread or inadvertent intrathecal dosing during epidural top-up. Be ready to support the airway and circulation immediately.

Prilocaine and benzocaine sprays can cause methaemoglobinaemia. Suspect it when the patient looks cyanosed and does not respond to oxygen.

## Antiemetics

Risk-stratify every patient (Apfel score is a practical tool). A multimodal approach is more effective than any single agent.

**First, reduce emetogenic drivers** where safe — minimise volatiles, nitrous oxide, and opioids.

**Then add prophylactic antiemetics:**

| Class | Example | Pitfall |
|---|---|---|
| 5-HT3 antagonist | Ondansetron | QT prolongation |
| Corticosteroid | Dexamethasone | Hyperglycaemia; infection risk |
| Dopamine antagonist | Droperidol, haloperidol | QT prolongation; extrapyramidal effects |
| H1 antagonist | Cyclizine | Sedation; anticholinergic effects |

Timing and combinations matter — follow local guidance.

## Vasoactive Drugs

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


**Phenylephrine** — pure alpha-agonist; increases SVR; can cause reflex bradycardia. Good for anaesthetic-induced vasodilation when cardiac output and heart rate are adequate.

**Metaraminol** — predominantly alpha with some indirect action; can be given as bolus or infusion. Widely used in UK practice.

**Ephedrine** — indirect sympathomimetic; increases heart rate and cardiac output. Tachyphylaxis occurs quickly. Avoid in tachyarrhythmias.

**Noradrenaline** — alpha-predominant with beta-1 effect; titratable infusion for vasodilatory hypotension. Follow local policy on dilution and peripheral versus central administration.

**Atropine / Glycopyrrolate** — for bradycardia. Glycopyrrolate does not cross the blood-brain barrier, making it preferable in most perioperative settings.
