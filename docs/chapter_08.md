# Chapter 8: Acute and Chronic Pain Management

Pain management is a core anaesthetic skill. Get the basics right and you prevent harm, reduce complications, and help patients recover faster.

## Core Principles

Pain has biological, psychological, and social dimensions. Your job is not simply to lower a score — it is to help patients **function**: cough, breathe deeply, mobilise.

- **Multimodal analgesia** combines drugs with different mechanisms to achieve better pain control with fewer opioid side effects. Think in layers, not single agents.
- **Sedation** is a more reliable early warning sign of opioid toxicity than respiratory rate. Monitor it actively.
- Plan analgesia from pre-op through to discharge. Review early. Document clearly.

## Pain Biology You Actually Use

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


**The pain pathway:** transduction (peripheral nociceptors activated) → transmission (Aδ/C fibres to dorsal horn) → modulation (spinal and descending pathways) → perception (brain). Each step is a potential target.

**Central sensitisation:** repeated noxious input lowers the threshold for pain ("wind-up"), producing hyperalgesia and allodynia. Pre-emptive multimodal analgesia and avoiding remifentanil-only techniques help prevent this.

**Know your drug mechanisms:**
- **Opioids:** μ-receptor agonism. Analgesia, sedation, respiratory depression, constipation, pruritus. Tolerance to analgesia develops faster than to constipation.
- **NSAIDs:** COX inhibition reduces prostaglandins. Risks: renal, GI, bleeding, NSAID-exacerbated respiratory disease, cardiovascular.
- **Paracetamol:** central COX modulation. Opioid-sparing. Hepatotoxic in overdose.
- **Local anaesthetics:** sodium channel blockade. Toxicity is dose and plasma-level dependent — CNS features first, then cardiovascular collapse.
- **Ketamine:** NMDA antagonism. Reduces central sensitisation. Psychotomimetic side effects.
- **Neuropathic pain** responds poorly to opioids alone. Membrane stabilisers (gabapentinoids, TCAs, SNRIs) are more appropriate.

## Assessing Pain

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Use a simple 0–10 scale, but also assess **function** — can the patient take a deep breath, cough, sit up?

**Screen for risk before prescribing:**
- OSA or obesity
- Elderly or frail (delirium risk)
- Renal or hepatic impairment
- Asthma (NSAID-exacerbated respiratory disease)
- Anticoagulation
- Pre-existing opioid or gabapentinoid use
- Chronic pain background

**Neuropathic features** (burning, shooting, electric-shock quality, allodynia) change your treatment plan. Ask about them.

**Acute-on-chronic:** establish their baseline medication and last dose. Preventing withdrawal is as important as treating the acute pain.

## Acute Pain Management

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


### Multimodal Analgesia

**Safe default scaffold:**
1. Regular paracetamol
2. Add NSAID if not contraindicated
3. Layer regional technique where appropriate
4. Short-acting opioid for breakthrough

Give analgesics **on time**. PRN-only regimens after major surgery cause pain to spiral, requiring higher opioid doses to catch up.

**Avoid:**
- Hidden duplicate paracetamol (e.g., in combination products)
- NSAIDs in acute kidney injury, active GI bleeding risk, NSAID-exacerbated respiratory disease, or third-trimester pregnancy
- PRN-only prescribing for major surgery

---

### Opioids

Use the lowest effective dose for the shortest duration. Prefer oral once the patient can tolerate it.

- Do not start long-acting opioids for acute pain in opioid-naïve patients.
- Do not add background infusions to PCA in opioid-naïve adults without senior or Acute Pain Service (APS) input.

**Monitoring:** prioritise sedation score alongside respiratory rate and SpO₂. Remember — supplemental oxygen can mask hypoventilation.

**Concomitant risk:** benzodiazepines, gabapentinoids, and alcohol significantly increase the risk of respiratory depression when combined with opioids.

**Naloxone must be immediately accessible** wherever opioids are given. Be aware that naloxone is shorter-acting than most opioids — monitor closely after reversal.

---

### Patient-Controlled Analgesia (PCA)

Appropriate for moderate to severe acute pain when the oral route is not feasible.

- Follow your local protocol for setup.
- Educate the patient: **only they press the button.** "PCA by proxy" (relatives pressing on their behalf) is dangerous.
- No background infusion in opioid-naïve adults.
- Combine with antiemetic and laxative prescriptions from the start.
- **Sedation precedes desaturation.** Act early, not late.

---

### Regional Analgesia

Regional techniques blunt nociception at source. Benefits include reduced opioid consumption, less nausea, lower delirium risk, and fewer pulmonary complications. Where appropriate, regional should be your first choice, not a last resort.

**Peripheral nerve blocks and catheters:**
- Match the block to the surgery — fascia iliaca block for hip fracture, adductor canal for knee, erector spinae plane for thoracic or breast surgery.
- Use ultrasound guidance to reduce vascular puncture and systemic toxicity.
- For catheters: maintain asepsis, secure the line well, use a dedicated infusion chart, review daily. Remove if you find signs of infection, unexpected motor block, or evolving neurology.
- **Anticoagulation and timing:** always check RA-UK/ESRA guidance (or your local policy) before inserting or removing any catheter. If in doubt, do not proceed — ask a senior.

**Neuraxial analgesia (epidural/spinal opioid):**
- Provides superior dynamic analgesia after thoracoabdominal and major surgery, allowing coughing and early mobilisation.
- Risks: hypotension, motor block, urinary retention, pruritus, and respiratory depression from neuraxial opioids.
- Monitor: blood pressure, sensory level, motor block, sedation, respiratory rate, SpO₂, catheter site.
- **Red flag:** new back pain with leg weakness, saddle anaesthesia, or loss of bladder or bowel function is an emergency. This is spinal cord compression until proved otherwise. Request urgent MRI and neurosurgical discussion immediately — time to decompression is critical.
- Anticoagulation intervals for insertion and removal are non-negotiable. Check local policy every time.

**Local anaesthetic systemic toxicity (LAST):**

Recognise it: perioral tingling → tinnitus → agitation → seizures → cardiovascular collapse.

**Prevention:** calculate maximum safe dose, aspirate before each injection, use ultrasound, inject incrementally.

**Management:**
1. Stop injecting
2. Call for help
3. Airway, ventilation with 100% oxygen
4. Treat seizures
5. Start **lipid emulsion** per your local protocol
6. Modified ALS — avoid high-dose adrenaline and propofol for arrhythmia. Refer to the Association of Anaesthetists LAST guidance and your Trust algorithm.

Know where the lipid emulsion is kept wherever you are performing blocks.

### Non-opioid Systemic Analgesics

**Paracetamol:** foundation drug. Check cumulative daily dose and liver function. IV is not superior to oral if the gut works — use IV only when the oral route is unavailable.

**NSAIDs:** effective for inflammatory and bone pain. Always assess renal function, bleeding risk, asthma history, and GI/cardiovascular risk. Add gastroprotection where indicated.

**Gabapentinoids (pregabalin, gabapentin):** useful for neuropathic features or in opioid-tolerant patients. Cause sedation and falls risk, especially when combined with opioids or in the elderly and those with OSA. Require renal dose adjustment.

**Ketamine (low-dose analgesic):** valuable opioid-sparing adjunct in major surgery, chronic opioid users, or when neuraxial is contraindicated. Side effects include psychotomimetic phenomena, nausea, and cardiovascular stimulation. Use within an established local protocol with senior oversight.

**Lidocaine infusion:** can reduce pain and ileus after abdominal surgery. Requires a protocolised pathway with monitoring — do not use ad hoc.

**Alpha-2 agonists (clonidine, dexmedetomidine):** opioid-sparing with sedative properties. Watch for bradycardia and hypotension. Senior-led ward use only.

**Tramadol and tapentadol:** mixed mechanisms. Risk of serotonin syndrome and seizures, particularly with SSRIs and MAOIs. Many drug interactions.

**Codeine and dihydrocodeine:** pro-drugs dependent on CYP2D6 metabolism — highly variable effect. Avoid in children. Ultra-rapid metabolisers are at risk of toxicity.

**Always co-prescribe antiemetics and laxatives when starting any opioid.**

### Acute-on-Chronic Pain and Opioid Tolerance

Do not stop a patient's baseline opioids perioperatively — you will precipitate withdrawal and undertreated pain simultaneously.

- Continue baseline opioids (including methadone or buprenorphine) and prescribe additional short-acting analgesia for acute needs.
- **Buprenorphine:** generally continue; splitting the dose may help. Higher-than-usual opioid doses may be needed for breakthrough — involve APS or addiction services early.
- **Naltrexone:** standard opioids may be ineffective. Plan regional and non-opioid strategies and liaise with the prescribing team.
- Expect higher requirements and a slower wean. Agree and document a taper plan before discharge.

### Special Populations

| Group | Key points |
|---|---|
| **Hip fracture** | Fascia iliaca or femoral nerve block early — reduces pain, delirium risk, and opioid consumption. Prioritise this. |
| **Elderly/frail** | Start low, go slow. High delirium risk — avoid anticholinergics and benzodiazepines. Regional and non-opioids first. |
| **OSA/obesity** | Minimise opioids and sedatives. Regional techniques first. Consider enhanced monitoring or HDU. |
| **Renal impairment** | Avoid or limit NSAIDs. Morphine metabolites accumulate — consider alternatives. Adjust gabapentinoid dose. |
| **Hepatic disease** | Paracetamol within safe limits per local guidance. Avoid long-acting opioids. Caution with tramadol. |
| **Pregnancy/breastfeeding** | Avoid NSAIDs in late pregnancy. Most short-term opioids are compatible with breastfeeding with monitoring. Liaise with obstetrics and neonatology. |
| **Paediatrics** | Weight-based prescribing. Avoid codeine. Regional where appropriate. Follow paediatric-specific protocols. |

When using opioids or ketamine for painful procedures outside theatre, follow Association of Anaesthetists and RCUK sedation standards: trained team, full monitoring (including capnography where indicated), and rescue drugs and skills immediately to hand.

## Discharge and Opioid De-escalation

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


- Prescribe the **smallest practical quantity** — default to days, not weeks.
- Convert IV to immediate-release oral. Do not initiate long-acting opioids for acute pain at discharge.
- Provide a written weaning plan. Counsel on side effects, driving restrictions, alcohol, laxatives, and when to seek help.
- If the patient is opioid-tolerant or leaving on higher doses than their baseline, communicate this clearly to the GP.

## Chronic Pain: What the Novice Needs

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


### The Biopsychosocial Model

Chronic pain is not simply ongoing tissue damage. Central sensitisation, mood, sleep quality, beliefs, and social circumstances all drive disability. The goal is **improved function and quality of life**, not complete pain elimination. Set realistic expectations with the patient from the outset.

---

### Assessment

**History:** onset, pattern, red flags (weight loss, fever, cancer history, trauma, progressive neurology, sphincter disturbance), neuropathic descriptors, functional impact, sleep, mood, prior treatments, substance use.

**Examination:** focused neurological and musculoskeletal screen. Look for allodynia and trophic changes (suggestive of CRPS).

**Red flags require urgent imaging and specialist assessment.** Do not mask them with escalating opioids.

---

### Pharmacology in Chronic Pain

**Non-cancer chronic pain:** long-term opioid escalation carries limited benefit and significant harm. If opioids are used, set a clear exit strategy, assess impact on function (not just pain score), screen for risk, and follow local opioid prescribing agreements.

**Neuropathic pain:** first-line options are TCAs or SNRIs; gabapentinoids are alternatives or adjuncts. Titrate slowly. Review the benefit-to-harm ratio regularly. Avoid polypharmacy and always check organ function and drug interactions. Follow NICE guidance and local sequencing recommendations.

**Topical agents:** lidocaine or capsaicin for focal neuropathic pain or osteoarthritis flares. Low systemic risk.

**Pitfalls:**
- Stacking opioids, gabapentinoids, and benzodiazepines together — this combination causes falls and respiratory depression.
- Continuing ineffective drugs because stopping feels wrong. If there is no functional gain after adequate trial, deprescribe.

---

### Non-pharmacological Core

Pacing, graded exercise, physiotherapy, sleep hygiene, weight management, and CBT-based programmes are evidence-based components of chronic pain management. Encourage self-management. Early signposting to a pain management programme improves long-term outcomes. Consider employment, social support, and access to services.

---

### Cancer Pain (Brief Essentials)

Use a stepwise approach:
- Regular non-opioids ± adjuvants for mild pain
- Add opioids for moderate to severe pain; treat breakthrough with rapid-onset preparations
- Identify the mechanism: bone pain responds to NSAIDs, radiotherapy, and bisphosphonates; neuropathic pain needs adjuvants; raised intracranial pressure or visceral capsule stretch responds to corticosteroids

Anticipate and co-prescribe for constipation, nausea, and delirium.

Complex regimens and syringe drivers should involve the palliative care team. Do not provide definitive dose advice in isolation — use local palliative guidelines.

---

### Interventional Options and Referral

Refer to pain services for refractory neuropathic pain, CRPS, persistent post-surgical pain, vertebral fractures, or diagnostic uncertainty. Interventional procedures (nerve blocks, radiofrequency ablation, neuromodulation) are specialist — do not offer ad hoc blocks for chronic pain without a clear pathway and follow-up plan.

## Common Pitfalls and How Things Go Wrong

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


- **Undertreating early** → central sensitisation, immobility, pulmonary complications.
- **Over-reliance on IV opioids/PCA** without a non-opioid or regional scaffold → toxicity with poor functional outcomes.
- **Failing to monitor sedation** with PCA or epidural → late recognition of respiratory depression or high block.
- **Ignoring anticoagulation timing** around neuraxial or catheter procedures → catastrophic haematoma.
- **Not planning for opioid-tolerant patients** → withdrawal or uncontrolled pain perioperatively.
- **Discharging large opioid supplies** without a taper plan → dependence and community harm.
- **Missing red flags in "chronic back pain"** → cord compression or infection.
