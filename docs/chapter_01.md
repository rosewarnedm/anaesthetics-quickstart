## The Big Picture: Oxygen Delivery (DO₂) and Demand (VO₂)

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Tissues survive when oxygen delivery meets demand. That is the central equation of anaesthetic physiology.

**DO₂ = cardiac output × arterial oxygen content (CaO₂).** CaO₂ depends overwhelmingly on haemoglobin concentration and its saturation; PaO₂ contributes relatively little. VO₂ rises with fever, shivering, sepsis, agitation, and surgical stress. Anaesthetic agents reduce cardiac output and blunt compensatory responses, so the margin narrows at exactly the wrong moment.

The practical implication: optimise all three components — haemoglobin, saturation, and flow. A normal SpO₂ does not guarantee adequate DO₂ if the patient is anaemic or has a low cardiac output. Anaemia can be clinically silent until physiological stress exposes it. Do not chase PaO₂ in isolation.

**Key habits:** preoxygenate to build reserve, keep the patient warm, treat pain and agitation promptly, and when hypotension and hypoxaemia coexist, address both simultaneously.

## Haemoglobin and the Oxygen–Haemoglobin Dissociation Curve

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


The relationship between PaO₂ and oxygen saturation is sigmoid, not linear. The upper, flat portion means SpO₂ stays reassuringly high over a wide range of PaO₂. The lower, steep portion means a small further fall in PaO₂ produces a large drop in saturation — the warning comes late and falls fast.

**Right shift** (acidosis, hypercapnia, hyperthermia, raised 2,3-DPG) — haemoglobin releases oxygen more readily to tissues. **Left shift** (alkalosis, hypocapnia, hypothermia, fetal haemoglobin) — haemoglobin holds onto oxygen, potentially reducing tissue availability despite adequate saturation.

Maintain normocapnia and normothermia as defaults. Avoid pronounced hypocapnia in traumatic brain injury except as a short-term temporising measure whilst awaiting definitive intervention.

**Watch out for:** carbon monoxide poisoning and methaemoglobinaemia, both of which cause pulse oximetry to overread. Severe anaemia with a high SpO₂ still delivers dangerously low DO₂.

## Control of Ventilation and CO₂

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Ventilation is driven primarily by CO₂ acting on central chemoreceptors via CSF pH. The hypoxic drive through peripheral chemoreceptors is a secondary mechanism that becomes important only when the CO₂ response is chronically blunted — as in some patients with COPD.

Opioids and anaesthetic agents depress both drives. Combine them with obstructive sleep apnoea and you have a high-risk situation for postoperative hypoventilation.

End-tidal CO₂ (EtCO₂) monitoring is mandatory for all general anaesthetics — it reflects alveolar ventilation and indirectly reflects cardiac output. Aim for normocapnia unless there is a specific indication otherwise.

In obstructive lung disease, use slower respiratory rates with prolonged expiratory times to avoid gas trapping. In COPD, permissive hypercapnia may be appropriate — do not hyperventilate to chase a "normal" number.

**Watch out for:** a low EtCO₂ with a good waveform does not exclude a high PaCO₂ if dead space is large. A sudden drop in EtCO₂ should prompt you to check for circuit disconnection, leak, or a fall in cardiac output.

## Lung Volumes, Compliance and Resistance

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Functional residual capacity (FRC) is the oxygen reservoir available during apnoea. General anaesthesia reduces FRC through diaphragmatic relaxation, supine positioning, and loss of respiratory muscle tone. Obesity, pregnancy, and pneumoperitoneum compound this further.

Compliance falls with atelectasis, pulmonary oedema, and fibrosis. Resistance rises with bronchospasm and secretions. Both make ventilation harder and gas exchange worse.

Reduced FRC shortens safe apnoea time — this matters most in the patient you are most worried about. Preoxygenation replaces alveolar nitrogen with oxygen, buying time. Head-up positioning for preoxygenation helps when it is safe to do so.

Use lung-protective ventilation (modest tidal volumes, appropriate PEEP) as your default. Recruit atelectasis judiciously, particularly after prolonged surgery or desaturation.

**Watch out for:** excessive PEEP impairs venous return and can cause hypotension. In obstructive disease, PEEP can worsen dynamic hyperinflation — the ventilator pressure alarms and the patient becomes hypotensive. Recognise auto-PEEP early.

## V/Q Mismatch, Shunt and Diffusion

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Hypoxaemia during anaesthesia is most commonly caused by V/Q mismatch and shunt. Atelectasis forming quickly after induction is the main culprit — collapsed alveoli are perfused but not ventilated, creating an intrapulmonary shunt.

The key clinical distinction: V/Q mismatch responds to supplemental oxygen; true shunt does not. If increasing FiO₂ fails to improve oxygenation, think shunt — atelectasis, consolidation, pulmonary oedema.

Treat with PEEP and recruitment manoeuvres; prevent with good positioning and ventilation strategy. One-lung ventilation and lateral positioning exaggerate V/Q mismatch — anticipate and plan.

**Watch out for:** very high FiO₂ can worsen absorption atelectasis over time. Do not ignore the haemodynamic cost of aggressive PEEP.

## Preoxygenation and Apnoea Time

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


The goal is to wash out nitrogen and fill the FRC with oxygen, creating a reservoir that buys time during apnoea. Efficacy depends on FiO₂ delivered, mask seal, duration, and patient factors.

A tight-fitting mask and high fresh gas flow are non-negotiable. Coach the patient — tidal breathing or deep breaths per local protocol. Patients with obesity, pregnancy, sepsis, or significant respiratory disease desaturate rapidly; optimise positioning (ramped or head-up) and consider apnoeic oxygenation via nasal cannulae as an adjunct, consistent with DAS principles for managing oxygenation during airway management.

**Watch out for:** beards, edentulous mouths, and agitation all compromise mask seal. Vigorous mask ventilation without adequate airway tone risks gastric insufflation.

## Pulse Oximetry: Uses and Limits

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


The pulse oximeter measures arterial oxyhaemoglobin saturation — not CaO₂, not PaO₂. It is a relative measure with inherent limitations.

The plethysmography waveform tells you about signal quality. A poor waveform in a cold, vasoconstrictive, or peripherally shut-down patient makes the reading unreliable. Warm the site, reposition the probe, and interpret the number accordingly. Treat trends and clinical context, not isolated values.

**Watch out for:** carboxyhaemoglobin and methaemoglobin both cause SpO₂ to misread. Severe anaemia and CO poisoning can both give a falsely reassuring reading. Nail varnish, motion artefact, and dysrhythmias also cause errors.

## Capnography: The Airway Lifeline

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Continuous waveform capnography is mandatory for all general anaesthetics and is the primary tool for confirming tracheal intubation. A flat trace after intubation means oesophageal placement until conclusively proven otherwise — follow DAS guidance and escalate promptly.

Learn to interpret the waveform shape as well as the number. A normal square waveform confirms ventilation. A sloped upstroke suggests obstructive disease. No waveform after intubation is an emergency.

Rising EtCO₂: consider hypoventilation, increased CO₂ production (fever, sepsis, malignant hyperthermia), exhausted soda lime, or rebreathing. Falling EtCO₂: consider hyperventilation, low cardiac output, pulmonary embolism, or circuit disconnection. A sudden fall to near-zero is disconnection or cardiac arrest until proven otherwise.

## Cardiovascular Physiology: Pressure, Flow and Resistance

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


**MAP ≈ cardiac output × systemic vascular resistance.** Cardiac output = heart rate × stroke volume, where stroke volume depends on preload, afterload, and contractility. Venous return drives preload; positive pressure ventilation reduces venous return by raising intrathoracic pressure.

Anaesthetic agents typically reduce SVR, depress myocardial contractility, and impair baroreceptor responses. Hypotension is therefore common at induction and during maintenance.

Diagnosing the cause of hypotension matters — the treatment differs entirely:
- **Vasodilation** (volatile agents, neuraxial block): vasopressor
- **Hypovolaemia**: fluid, but only if preload-responsive
- **Pump failure**: inotrope
- **Obstruction** (tension pneumothorax, cardiac tamponade, massive PE): specific intervention

Use small, titrated steps and reassess frequently. Reaching for fluid reflexively for every episode of hypotension risks volume overload without addressing the cause.

**Watch out for:** PEEP, pneumoperitoneum, and certain patient positions all impair venous return and can drop cardiac output — ventilator settings and surgical conditions are cardiovascular variables too.

## Autoregulation and Organ Protection

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


The brain and kidneys autoregulate blood flow across a range of perfusion pressures. In chronic hypertension, this range shifts right — the organ is accustomed to higher pressures and becomes vulnerable at pressures that would be normal in another patient.

Coronary perfusion occurs predominantly during diastole. Tachycardia shortens diastolic time and reduces coronary filling — particularly relevant in ischaemic heart disease.

Maintain MAP close to the patient's usual level, especially in neurological and renal disease. Brief periods of hypotension can cause ischaemia to vulnerable organs even if the overall anaesthetic is otherwise unremarkable. Tailor targets to the individual and the surgical context.

**Watch out for:** deliberate hypotension techniques to reduce surgical bleeding can cross into ischaemic territory. Do not ignore diastolic pressure and pulse pressure in patients with coronary disease.

## Neuraxial Block Physiology

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Spinal and epidural anaesthesia produce sympathetic block, causing vasodilation and venous pooling. The higher the block, the more sympathetic outflow is affected. Block of the cardiac accelerator fibres (T1–T4) can cause bradycardia in addition to hypotension. Reduced preload is the dominant mechanism.

Anticipate and manage proactively: position the patient appropriately, use judicious co-loading with crystalloid, and have vasopressors immediately available per local protocols.

In pregnant patients, aortocaval compression from the gravid uterus dramatically worsens hypotension — always apply left uterine displacement.

**Watch out for:** a high or total spinal causes rapid cardiovascular collapse and apnoea. Recognise it early: sudden profound hypotension, loss of consciousness, and inability to breathe after a spinal dose. Manage supportively and call for help immediately. Vasopressors alone are insufficient if fluid is not also given, but fluid alone rarely resolves vasoplegia.

## Cerebral Physiology: ICP, CPP and CO₂

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


**CPP = MAP − ICP** (or CVP if higher). Adequate CPP is necessary to maintain cerebral blood flow. CO₂ is a potent cerebrovascular regulator: hypercapnia vasodilates and raises ICP; hypocapnia vasoconstrictes and lowers ICP and CBF.

Maintain normocapnia as the default. Controlled, brief hypocapnia can temporise a dangerous rise in ICP whilst definitive treatment is organised — but it is a bridge, not a solution, and prolonged hypocapnia causes cerebral ischaemia.

Keep the head in a neutral position and elevate slightly to aid venous drainage if haemodynamics allow. Avoid hypoxia and hypotension — both worsen secondary brain injury.

**Watch out for:** excessive PEEP raises intrathoracic pressure, impedes cerebral venous drainage, and can increase ICP. Balance respiratory and neurological goals carefully.

## Renal Physiology and Fluids

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


The kidney autoregulates over a pressure range, but this autoregulation is impaired by chronic disease, contrast agents, and sustained hypotension. Urine output is a useful but imperfect and delayed marker of renal perfusion — oliguria has many causes, and not all of them require fluid.

Use balanced crystalloids as the default for intraoperative fluid. Large volumes of saline cause hyperchloraemic metabolic acidosis, which can be misleading on blood gas interpretation and may have haemodynamic effects. Target fluid therapy to clinical signs, dynamic indices, and point-of-care ultrasound where appropriate rather than giving fluid by protocol.

**Watch out for:** giving repeated fluid boluses in response to oliguria when the patient is not hypovolaemic causes harm through oedema without improving renal perfusion. Identify the cause of oliguria before treating it.

## Acid–Base in Practice

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


The Henderson–Hasselbalch relationship underpins clinical acid–base interpretation. pH is determined by the ratio of CO₂ (controlled by ventilation) to bicarbonate (controlled by the kidneys and metabolic processes). Disorders are respiratory (CO₂ change) or metabolic (bicarbonate change), with partial compensation in the opposite direction.

Correct metabolic acidosis by treating the cause — restore perfusion, stop haemorrhage, treat sepsis. Ventilation changes PaCO₂; it does not fix a metabolic problem, it compensates temporarily. Aggressively hyperventilating to improve a pH number in metabolic acidosis risks cerebral vasoconstriction.

Remember that EtCO₂ may not reflect PaCO₂ accurately when dead space is high. In critically ill or ventilated patients, check arterial blood gases when the clinical picture does not fit the monitoring.

**Watch out for:** high FiO₂ masks hypoventilation — SpO₂ can remain acceptable whilst PaCO₂ rises dangerously. CO₂ narcosis is a real risk in this scenario.

## Electrolytes and Excitability

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Potassium sets the resting membrane potential. Hyperkalaemia suppresses cardiac conduction and risks arrest; hypokalaemia predisposes to arrhythmias, weakness, and potentiates neuromuscular blockers. Correct significant derangements before elective cases.

Ionised calcium affects myocardial contractility, vascular tone, and coagulation. Rapid blood transfusion chelates calcium via citrate — in massive transfusion, check ionised calcium and replace early.

Magnesium modulates neuromuscular transmission, NMDA receptors, and arrhythmia risk. Hypomagnesaemia prolongs QT interval and predisposes to torsades de pointes.

**Watch out for:** QT prolongation is multifactorial — electrolyte abnormalities and certain anaesthetic drugs can combine to increase arrhythmia risk. Check the ECG when you know about electrolyte problems.

## Temperature and Metabolic Rate

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Anaesthesia impairs thermoregulation by resetting the hypothalamic threshold and causing peripheral vasodilation, redistributing heat from the core to the periphery. The patient cools rapidly in the first hour after induction.

Hypothermia impairs coagulation, increases surgical site infection, prolongs drug metabolism, and predisposes to arrhythmias. Prevention is far more effective than treatment — start active warming before induction, use warmed intravenous fluids, and maintain it throughout, consistent with Association of Anaesthetists guidance.

**Watch out for:** starting warming late in a long case rarely rescues the situation. Monitor temperature in all but the briefest procedures.

## Haemostasis, Blood and Coagulation Physiology

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Effective coagulation requires adequate coagulation factors, platelet function, ionised calcium, a normal pH, and normothermia. The "lethal triad" of hypothermia, acidosis, and coagulopathy is self-perpetuating — each makes the others worse.

In major haemorrhage, follow your local major haemorrhage protocol. Give tranexamic acid early when indicated. Correct fibrinogen early — it falls before other clotting factors and is essential to clot formation. Do not allow temperature and pH to drift whilst concentrating on giving blood products.
