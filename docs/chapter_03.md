## Core Physics You Actually Use

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


### Pressure, Flow and Resistance

Flow equals pressure gradient divided by resistance. This is the Ohm's law analogue for gas flow, and it matters every time you ventilate a patient.

Resistance rises with narrow tubes, kinks, secretions and bronchospasm. A small reduction in airway radius causes a disproportionately large rise in resistance — this is the key message from Poiseuille's law. Use the largest sensible endotracheal tube, suction early, and treat bronchospasm promptly.

### Laminar vs Turbulent Flow

Laminar flow is quiet and efficient. Turbulent flow is noisy, wasteful and harder to generate. At low flow rates, flow depends on gas viscosity; at high flow rates, it depends on gas density. This is why heliox (low density) can improve flow past a partial obstruction — it shifts flow towards the laminar regime. Rough surfaces, connectors and high flow rates all promote turbulence.

### Compliance

Compliance is the volume change you get per unit of pressure applied. A stiff lung or chest wall has low compliance — you need higher pressures to deliver the same tidal volume. Watch your airway pressure trends. Rising pressures for the same volume mean something has changed: oedema, pneumothorax, pneumoperitoneum, secretions, or a one-lung situation. Reassess frequently.

### Gas Laws

These underpin the machine and the physiology:

- **Boyle's law**: compressing a gas raises its pressure. Squeezing a bag or trapping gas raises pressure — relevant to barotrauma.
- **Charles's law**: cold gases lose heat. Unhumidified gases damage the airway mucosa. Humidify when you can.
- **Dalton's law**: total pressure equals the sum of partial pressures. The concentration of oxygen and volatile agent you dial in directly affects the patient.
- **Henry's law**: gas dissolves in proportion to pressure above it. Relevant to venous gas embolism and to nitrous oxide expanding air-containing spaces.

### Vapour, Volatiles and MAC

Volatile agents exist in liquid form in the vapouriser; the saturated vapour pressure determines how much vapour is produced at a given temperature. Modern variable-bypass vapourisers are temperature-compensated and agent-specific. Keep them upright, fill them on a flat surface using the keyed filler, and do not mix agents.

MAC (minimum alveolar concentration) is a population-based potency measure — it is the alveolar concentration at which 50% of patients do not move to a surgical stimulus. It is a reference point, not a personal target. Use end-tidal agent concentration alongside clinical signs to titrate depth, and follow local guidance.

## The Anaesthetic Machine and Gas Supply

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


### Gas Sources and Safety

Pipeline oxygen is the primary supply; cylinders are the backup. Non-interchangeable connectors and pin-index systems prevent misconnections, but they are not foolproof — always check labels yourself. Keep an oxygen cylinder attached, turned on, and confirmed to have adequate contents before every list.

### Three Pressure Zones

The machine has three pressure zones: high pressure (cylinders and regulators), intermediate pressure (pipeline inlet and machine regulators), and low pressure (flowmeters, vapouriser, common gas outlet). Leaks and misconnections behave differently depending on where they occur — a low-pressure leak can be equally dangerous and harder to detect.

### Oxygen Protection Systems

Oxygen fail-safe devices and hypoxic guards are designed to limit hypoxic gas delivery when oxygen pressure falls. They can fail. The **oxygen analyser is the only direct measurement of delivered oxygen concentration** — treat it as mandatory, set audible alarms, and never silence it without addressing the cause.

### Flowmeters, Vapourisers and the Oxygen Flush

Rotameters are gas-specific and fragile. The bobbin must spin freely and sit within the calibrated range.

Vapourisers require a settled temperature before the output is reliable. The **pumping effect** — where positive pressure from a ventilator briefly increases vapour output — is reduced in modern designs but remains a reason to respect manufacturer guidance.

The oxygen flush delivers high-flow oxygen directly past the vapouriser. This means it **dilutes volatile agent**, risking awareness, and it can cause **barotrauma** if activated during inspiration. Avoid using the flush while connected to the patient, especially with a supraglottic airway.

### Machine Check and Pipeline Failure

Perform a full machine and breathing system check before every list, using the Association of Anaesthetists checklist. The electronic self-test does not replace a manual leak check.

If you suspect a pipeline failure or crossover:
1. Trust the oxygen analyser and your patient — not the pipeline colour.
2. Open the oxygen cylinder and disconnect the pipeline connector.
3. Increase FiO₂, call for help, and ventilate with a self-inflating bag if necessary.
4. Follow your local emergency protocol.

### Scavenging

Scavenging removes waste anaesthetic gases. Active systems can apply negative pressure — an obstruction can cause unexpected positive or negative pressure effects in the breathing system. Never connect a breathing system directly to wall suction. Confirm scavenging connections before starting every list.

## Breathing Systems

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


### The Circle System

The circle is the workhorse of theatre anaesthesia. It consists of inspiratory and expiratory limbs with unidirectional valves, a CO₂ absorber, a reservoir bag, the APL valve, and a Y-piece. CO₂ is removed chemically, so rebreathing is possible at low fresh gas flows while still being safe — making it economical and humidifying.

**What to check:**

- **Unidirectional valves**: a stuck valve causes rebreathing. The capnograph baseline will rise.
- **CO₂ absorbent**: when exhausted, the inspired CO₂ trace on the capnograph rises from zero. Replace regularly. Desiccated absorbent can react with some agents to produce toxic compounds — avoid running prolonged dry gas flows through idle machines.
- **Low-flow anaesthesia**: reduces waste and preserves warmth, but risks hypoxic mixtures and agent under-delivery if not monitored. A functioning oxygen analyser and agent monitor are mandatory.

### The APL Valve and PEEP

The APL (adjustable pressure-limiting) valve governs peak pressure during manual ventilation and vents gas to the scavenging system. Closing it fully causes pressure to rise rapidly — barotrauma risk is real. Never leave it fully closed. Apply PEEP modestly and titrate to oxygenation and airway mechanics.

### Mapleson Systems and the Bain Circuit

Mapleson systems do not use a CO₂ absorber — rebreathing prevention depends on fresh gas flow.

The key principle:
- **Spontaneous ventilation** → Mapleson A is most efficient.
- **Controlled ventilation** → Mapleson D/E/F (including the Bain circuit) are more efficient.

The **Bain circuit** is a coaxial Mapleson D: fresh gas travels down the inner limb inside the outer expiratory limb. It is compact and warm. However, **disconnection of the inner limb causes severe rebreathing** — always perform an occlusion (Pethick) test before use. Fresh gas flow requirements are higher than for a circle; use local guidance.

## Ventilators in Theatre

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


### Modes

- **Volume control**: delivers a set tidal volume; airway pressure varies with lung mechanics. Simple and predictable.
- **Pressure control**: limits airway pressure; tidal volume varies. Useful when you want to protect against barotrauma or in patients with highly variable compliance.
- **Pressure support**: augments the patient's own spontaneous effort. Used during emergence or shared ventilation.

Always set alarms: high pressure, low volume, apnoea, and disconnection.

### Reading the Waveforms

- **Rising peak pressure, stable plateau** → increased resistance (kinked tube, bronchospasm, secretions).
- **Rising peak and plateau together** → reduced compliance (pneumothorax, oedema, abdominal insufflation, position change).
- **Sudden fall in pressure or delivered volume** → disconnection or significant leak. Check the patient first, then the circuit, then the machine.

### Safe Setup

Choose a mode appropriate to the airway and surgery. Base tidal volumes on predicted body weight. Apply modest PEEP. Titrate FiO₂ to effect. After any change — position, circuit, tube — reconfirm ventilation with chest movement, capnography, airway pressures, and delivered volumes.

### Key Hazards

- **Barotrauma**: excessive pressures or inappropriate oxygen flush during inspiration.
- **Awareness**: empty vapouriser, wrong circuit position, or TIVA line disconnection. Cross-check end-tidal agent concentration continuously.
- **Increased resistance from saturated filters**: replace when clogged — they can be an underappreciated cause of high airway pressures.

## Airway Equipment Essentials

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


### Endotracheal Tubes

Use high-volume, low-pressure cuffs. Monitor cuff pressure — overinflation causes mucosal ischaemia; underinflation risks aspiration and leaks. Secure the tube carefully; axial or lateral movement can dislodge it or advance it into a main bronchus.

**Confirm tracheal placement with continuous capnography.** Persistently absent CO₂ waveform means oesophageal placement until proven otherwise — do not wait.

### Supraglottic Airways

Second-generation devices (with a gastric channel and higher seal pressure) are preferred for most anaesthetics. Avoid excessive cuff pressure. Confirm position and ventilation with capnography and a leak assessment.

### Heat and Moisture Exchangers (HMEs) and Filters

These provide passive humidification and filter bacterial and viral particles. They increase circuit dead space and resistance — significant in smaller patients. Replace when saturated or if airway resistance is unexpectedly high.

## Monitoring: Minimum Standards and Practical Use

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


The Association of Anaesthetists and the RCoA mandate continuous monitoring of oxygenation, ventilation, circulation and temperature for every general anaesthetic. **Capnography is required whenever the airway is instrumented.** It is strongly recommended for moderate to deep sedation. These are not optional extras.

### Pulse Oximetry

The pulse oximeter uses two wavelengths of light to estimate the proportion of oxyhaemoglobin. It is invaluable — but understand its limits:

- It **lags behind** changes in alveolar ventilation, particularly in preoxygenated patients. A normal SpO₂ does not mean adequate ventilation.
- **Poor perfusion, motion, cold peripheries, nail coverings and dyes** all cause artefact or failure.
- **Carboxyhaemoglobin** reads falsely high. **Methaemoglobin** drives readings towards an intermediate value regardless of true saturation.
- Always correlate the reading with the waveform quality. Treat the patient, not the number.

### Capnography

This is arguably the most important monitor in theatre. Infrared absorption measures CO₂ in expired gas breath-by-breath.

**Normal waveform phases:**
1. Phase I — dead-space gas (no CO₂)
2. Phase II — rapid upstroke as alveolar gas mixes
3. Phase III — alveolar plateau
4. End-tidal CO₂ value
5. Rapid downstroke on inspiration

**What the waveform tells you:**

| Pattern | Likely cause |
|---|---|
| Absent waveform | Oesophageal intubation, disconnection, apnoea |
| Rising baseline (inspiratory CO₂) | Rebreathing (exhausted absorbent, stuck valve) |
| Shark-fin shape | Airway obstruction or bronchospasm |
| Sudden drop in EtCO₂ | Disconnection, cardiac arrest, massive embolism |
| Gradual fall in EtCO₂ | Hyperventilation, falling cardiac output |
| Gradual rise in EtCO₂ | Hypoventilation, rising metabolism, rebreathing |

Watch the waveform shape, not just the number. Artefact from a kinked sampling line or flooded water trap is common — always interpret in context.

### ECG

Continuous monitoring detects rate, rhythm and — with five-lead systems in the appropriate lead — ischaemia. Diathermy and movement cause artefact that can mimic arrhythmias or mask ischaemia. If in doubt, palpate the pulse or look at the arterial trace before treating. Place defibrillation pads before draping in high-risk patients.

### Blood Pressure

Non-invasive oscillometry measures mean arterial pressure most reliably. Cuff size and limb position matter — a cuff too small reads high; a limb below heart level reads high. Arrhythmias reduce accuracy.

**Invasive arterial monitoring** is indicated for beat-to-beat monitoring, vasoactive infusions, and major surgery. Practical principles:

- Zero the transducer at the level of the heart.
- Keep tubing short and stiff to avoid overdamping.
- Perform a fast-flush test and check for a square-wave response to assess system damping.
- Raising or lowering the transducer relative to the heart directly and immediately affects the displayed pressure — account for this with position changes.

### Inspired Oxygen and Agent Monitoring

The **inspired oxygen analyser** is non-negotiable. Set audible alarms. A functioning analyser is the definitive safeguard against delivering a hypoxic mixture.

The **agent analyser** confirms vapouriser delivery and allows end-tidal-based depth titration. A sudden fall in the agent trace should prompt you to check the vapouriser level, the circuit connections, and whether the patient is still breathing volatile agent at all.

### Neuromuscular Monitoring

Use a peripheral nerve stimulator for all non-depolarising neuromuscular blockade. Quantitative (acceleromyography or mechanomyography) monitoring is current best practice.

Key points:

- The **ulnar nerve/adductor pollicis** correlates well with pharyngeal and respiratory muscle recovery — safer for extubation decisions.
- The **facial nerve/orbicularis oculi** reflects central onset faster but **overestimates recovery** — do not use it to confirm adequate reversal.
- Ensure supramaximal stimulation. Do not rely on elapsed time. Train-of-four ratio should be confirmed before extubation; use local protocols for reversal agents and targets.

### Depth of Anaesthesia (Processed EEG)

Processed EEG monitors reduce but do not eliminate awareness risk. Use them in TIVA, patients at high risk of awareness, and those with fragile physiology where clinical signs are unreliable. Artefact from electrocautery and movement affects readings. Always interpret the number in the context of the patient.

### Temperature

Perioperative hypothermia is common and harmful — it increases bleeding, infection risk, cardiac events and drug metabolism unpredictability. Monitor core temperature in longer or major cases. Use forced-air warming, fluid warming, and keep the patient covered. The breathing circuit provides modest warmth but does not reliably prevent hypothermia.

### Urine Output and Other Monitoring

A urinary catheter is indicated for major surgery, renal risk, or prolonged cases. Urine output is a **late marker** of renal perfusion — do not use it as your primary guide to haemodynamic status. Additional monitoring (cardiac output, cerebral oximetry, glucose) should be guided by the clinical context and local protocols.

## Preoxygenation and Apnoeic Oxygenation

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Preoxygenation washes nitrogen out of the functional residual capacity and replaces it with oxygen, extending the safe apnoea time. It requires a good mask seal and adequate time — end-tidal oxygen measurement confirms effectiveness where available.

Head-up positioning, continuous positive airway pressure, and appropriate fresh gas flow all improve preoxygenation. In the obese or pregnant patient, or anyone with reduced FRC, effective preoxygenation is especially important.

## Common Pitfalls and How to Avoid Them

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


**Ignoring the oxygen analyser.** It is the definitive safeguard against hypoxic mixtures. If it alarms, act — do not silence it first.

**Silencing alarms.** Always fix the cause before silencing. Set appropriate limits at the start of every case.

**APL valve left fully closed.** Pressure rises rapidly and dangerously. Make a habit of leaving it partially open whenever you are not actively ventilating.

**Empty or switched-off vapouriser.** Check the level before induction. Cross-check the dial setting against the agent monitor throughout.

**TIVA line disconnection.** This is a significant awareness risk. Use dedicated IV access, anti-siphon valves, pump alarms, and keep the line visible. Consider processed EEG monitoring.
