Most peri-operative harm is not technical — it stems from poor communication, teamwork failures, and unsafe handover. Checklists and structured conversation create a shared mental model and catch errors before they reach the patient. In UK practice, using the WHO Surgical Safety Checklist, following NatSIPPs/LocSSIPs principles, and adhering to Association of Anaesthetists handover guidance is the expected standard, not optional best practice.

## The WHO Surgical Safety Checklist

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


### The purpose

The checklist exists to ensure the right patient has the right operation performed safely. It forces a deliberate pause, surfaces hazards, and aligns the team. It is a brief, engaged conversation — not a tick-box ritual.

### The structure

Three stages: **Sign In** (before induction), **Time Out** (before incision), **Sign Out** (before the patient leaves theatre). Many units add a list **Brief** at the start of the day and a **Debrief** at the end — both are valuable habits.

### Your role as the anaesthetist

You lead or co-lead Sign In. You contribute actively at Time Out and Sign Out. Champion the pause. Ask for quiet. Insist on full team participation. If something is unclear or unsafe, say so early and escalate.

### Sign In: before induction

Verify identity, procedure, and site/side against the patient, notes, and skin marking. Confirm valid consent is present. Declare your airway and aspiration risk assessment, share your plan, and name your fallback. State allergies, fasting status, and relevant comorbidities. Confirm your machine check, suction, and essential drugs are ready. Discuss antibiotic timing, thromboprophylaxis, and — importantly — agree a plan if the patient arrests or if there are treatment-limitation decisions in place.

**Safe default: do not induce until all critical uncertainties are resolved.**

### Time Out: before incision

The whole team stops, faces the patient, and is silent. Everyone introduces themselves by name and role — this flattens hierarchy and invites challenge. Confirm patient, procedure, and site again. Each discipline then declares their anticipated critical events: the anaesthetist covers airway and haemodynamic concerns; the surgeon covers critical steps, expected blood loss, and implants; the scrub practitioner covers sterility, kit, and counts. Confirm antibiotics have been given or are scheduled. Agree communication cues — for example, when to give reversal, or how specimens will be handled.

### Sign Out: before leaving theatre

Confirm procedure performed (including any deviations), that counts are correct, and that specimens are correctly labelled. State the post-operative destination and hand over the key plan: oxygen, analgesia, antiemetics, fluids, monitoring needs, and escalation triggers. Flag specific risks — airway swelling, residual block, bleeding risk. If time permits, debrief: what went well, what to improve, any near-misses.

### Emergencies and non-theatre settings

Use an abbreviated verbal checklist focused on identity, site, life-threats, and roles. LocSSIPs adaptations exist for interventional radiology, endoscopy, and the emergency department. Document deviations and your rationale. Return to full checks when feasible.

### Common pitfalls

- **Tick-box behaviour**: demand eye contact and genuine engagement.
- **Noise and distraction**: call "quiet for checklist"; pause music and diathermy.
- **Wrong-site risk**: cross-check consent, imaging, and skin marking together.
- **Missed or late antibiotics**: clarify timing at Sign In; confirm at Time Out.
- **No plan for airway difficulty or major haemorrhage**: surface it early, get resources into the room before you start.
- **Hierarchy silencing challenge**: explicitly invite concerns and use graded assertiveness.

## Communication essentials

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


### Behaviours that keep patients safe

Use names. Speak clearly. One conversation at a time. Use **closed-loop communication**: assign a task to a named person, they repeat it back, you confirm. Use call-outs for key events — "incision", "giving heparin", "one minute to extubation". Share your plan and the reasoning behind it so the whole team understands what you are anticipating.

### Tools to use

- **SBAR** (Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation) for escalation and handover.
- **Graded assertiveness** (e.g. PACE or CUSS) when you have a safety concern: state your concern, say you are uncomfortable, signal a safety issue, stop the procedure if needed. Start early — do not wait until crisis point.
- **Sterile cockpit**: during induction, intubation, and emergence, minimise non-essential talk and interruptions.
- **Cognitive aids** during crises: use them without embarrassment; allocate a dedicated reader if possible.

### Leadership and followership

In a crisis, identify or assume a leader and announce roles clearly. Minimise task-overload on any one person. If you are not leading, anticipate needs, close loops, and speak up. Civility is not just good manners — it genuinely improves team performance and patient safety.

## Handover: transferring care safely

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


### Core principles

Handover is a high-risk transition. Treat it as a procedure. Use structured verbal and written handover. Make it two-way: check understanding explicitly ("can you summarise back what I've said?"). Stay until the receiving clinician accepts the patient and monitoring is established. Follow local documentation standards.

### Theatre to PACU: what to hand over

Cover the following:

- **Identity and procedure**: patient identifiers, what was done, any deviations.
- **Airway and anaesthesia**: devices used, laryngoscopy grade, any difficulty or trauma, aspiration risk, airway swelling, what to do if the patient deteriorates.
- **Intra-operative events**: hypotension, hypoxia, arrhythmia, anaphylaxis, major haemorrhage, difficult ventilation, any suspicion of malignant hyperthermia.
- **Analgesia**: techniques used (regional, neuraxial, IV, oral), last doses, catheter details, infusion settings, rescue options, and monitoring required for toxicity or motor block.
- **Antiemetics**: what was given; strategy if PONV breaks through.
- **Fluids and blood products**: what was given, current access, urine output if relevant.
- **Drugs of note**: antibiotics (agent and timing), anticoagulants, reversal agents, long-acting opioids or sedatives.
- **Current physiology**: airway device, oxygen delivery, haemodynamics, temperature, glucose if relevant.
- **Specific risks and instructions**: positioning concerns, pressure areas, tourniquet time, drains, isolation requirements.
- **Observation and escalation plan**: what to expect, what triggers a call to anaesthetics or surgery, any treatment limitations.

**Safe defaults**: do the handover at the bedside with monitors attached and oxygen connected. Use SBAR or your local PACU proforma. Do not rely on memory. Explicitly state fasting restrictions and any block-safety precautions.

### PACU to ward

Confirm fitness for discharge against local criteria. Hand over anaesthetic and surgical summary, allergies, and relevant comorbidities. Ensure the ward team understands the analgesia plan (including regional catheter orders and monitoring frequency), antiemetic strategy, oxygen prescription and weaning plan, fluid plan, next antibiotic dose, thromboprophylaxis timing, wound and drain instructions, and specific risks to watch for. State clearly who to call and under what circumstances.

### ICU/HDU transfers

Use the formal critical care transfer process and documentation. Include ventilator settings, vasoactive infusions, arterial and central access, sedation and analgesia targets, and pending investigations. Ensure monitoring and equipment meet transfer standards from theatre to the receiving unit.

### Regional and neuraxial specifics

State block type, site, technique, drugs, and timing. Detail catheter information and pump settings. Specify neurological and vascular checks required, motor block precautions, and mobilisation restrictions. State the toxicity risk window and who to call for local anaesthetic systemic toxicity (LAST). For neuraxial techniques, document sensory level, motor status, expected regression, and anticoagulation timings. The removal plan for an epidural catheter must align with local thromboprophylaxis guidance — know this, and document it explicitly.

### Documentation

Complete the anaesthetic record contemporaneously. Record key events and deviations. Note that WHO checklist steps were completed (or explain any adaptation). Use incident reporting for near-misses — this is how systems improve.

## Making it work on the day

Arrive early for the list brief. Know the plan for each case before you start. In the anaesthetic room, run your own micro-brief: airway plan A/B/C, lines, antibiotics, blood availability, monitoring, patient-specific hazards. Call for "quiet for Sign In" and model the behaviour you want from the team. Pre-warn PACU about high-risk cases or special requirements.

During emergence, declare a sterile cockpit. Announce key steps early so the team can anticipate.

For handover, face the receiving clinician. Speak, pause, invite questions, and confirm understanding before you leave.

If something seems wrong, say so early and clearly. Use graded assertiveness. Escalate to the most senior person available if not resolved.

If identity, consent, site, or safety is uncertain — **do not proceed**. Resolve it first. Document your reasoning.

## Pitfalls to recognise

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


- Rushing or skipping checks for "simple" or "quick" cases.
- Conducting the checklist while everyone continues their tasks and no one is listening.
- Vague language and assumptions — "the usual plan" is not a plan.
- Failing to hand over critical information: a difficult airway or anaphylaxis must be communicated, not omitted.
- Noisy, corridor-based handovers with no documentation.
- Authority gradient silencing junior voices — actively invite challenge.
- Public handovers that breach confidentiality — use a private space.

- **WHO Surgical Safety Checklist** and local **LocSSIPs** (NatSIPPs framework): brief, check-in, time-out, sign-out, debrief.
- **Association of Anaesthetists**: safe handover in the peri-operative period; monitoring standards; syringe labelling.
- **RCoA curriculum**: human factors, communication, and safe transfer competencies.
- **Resuscitation Council UK**: team leadership and closed-loop communication during resuscitation.
- **DAS airway guidance**: human factors principles apply at induction and extubation; airway plans must be shared and handed over explicitly.
