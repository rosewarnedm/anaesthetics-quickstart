## Core Principles

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Regional anaesthesia improves analgesia, reduces opioids, and can be the primary anaesthetic. It demands the same preparation and vigilance as general anaesthesia (GA).

- **Always have a plan B.** Be ready to convert to GA if the block is inadequate or unsafe.
- **Monitor to Association of Anaesthetists standards** — including capnography when sedating with supplemental oxygen.
- **Use a checklist.** Confirm patient, procedure, side, consent, allergies, and anticoagulation before needle-to-skin.
- **Asepsis is non-negotiable.** Sterile gloves, probe cover, sterile gel, skin prep — and let it dry.
- **Calculate total local anaesthetic dose before you start.** Include every planned injection. Reduce for frailty, pregnancy, and comorbidity. Check local protocols.
- **Document everything:** what you used, what you found, the result, and the plan if it fails or wears off.

## Local Anaesthetic Pharmacology: Why It Matters

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Local anaesthetics block sodium channels. Small fibres (pain, temperature) are affected before motor fibres.

Key properties to understand:
- **Lipid solubility** → potency and block depth.
- **pKa** → onset speed. More unionised drug at tissue pH means faster onset.
- **Protein binding** → duration.

**Vasoconstrictors** (e.g., adrenaline) slow systemic absorption and may extend block duration. Avoid in end-artery territories and compromised perfusion. Do not rely on the adrenaline intravascular marker if the patient is under beta-blockade or GA.

**Systemic absorption** depends on site vascularity, volume, and total dose. Fascial plane blocks use large volumes — the cumulative dose adds up fast.

**Local anaesthetic systemic toxicity (LAST)** is the feared complication. Prevention is far easier than rescue.

Practical choices:
- Use lower concentrations when motor preservation matters (mobilisation, physiotherapy).
- Match agent and concentration to goal: dense surgical block versus prolonged analgesia versus motor-sparing.
- Calculate dose on lean body weight where appropriate.

## Ultrasound: The Physics You Need

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


- **High frequency** = better resolution, less depth. Use a linear probe for superficial structures; curvilinear for deep.
- **Nerves** appear as a "honeycomb" in short axis. Anisotropy can make them disappear — fan the probe slightly to find them again.
- **In-plane needling** gives you continuous tip visualisation. Out-of-plane requires meticulous control. While learning, use in-plane.
- **Optimise your image:** depth so the target is mid-screen, gain adjusted for clear tissue planes without "snow."
- **Hydrodissection** with small aliquots of saline or 5% dextrose helps confirm the plane before committing local anaesthetic.
- Colour Doppler before puncture — map the vessels every time.

## Injection Safety and Nerve Protection

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Stop and reassess if:
- You feel or see high opening injection pressure.
- The patient reports pain, paraesthesia, or radiating discomfort during injection.
- The needle tip is not clearly visible.

Intrafascicular injection causes nerve injury; extrafascicular intraneural injection may not — but avoid both. Use shallow needle angles to improve tip visibility and reduce the risk of fascicle impalement. Use an injection pressure monitor if available.

## Planning the Block

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


**Match block to surgery:** identify dermatomes, myotomes, and anticipated pain sources. Decide single-shot versus catheter upfront.

**Patient factors that change your plan:**
- **Respiratory compromise:** interscalene causes near-universal hemidiaphragmatic paresis — avoid in patients with limited respiratory reserve or contralateral diaphragm dysfunction.
- **Anticoagulation:** timing is critical for neuraxial and deep non-compressible blocks. Follow RA-UK–aligned local guidance. When uncertain, delay and discuss with a senior.
- **Pre-existing neuropathy:** not an absolute contraindication, but document the baseline carefully.
- **Infection:** never inject through infected skin. Systemic sepsis is a relative contraindication — seek senior input.

**Logistics:**
- Block where you can monitor, resuscitate, and call for help immediately.
- Intralipid must be immediately to hand whenever injecting potentially cardiotoxic agents.

## Peripheral Nerve Blocks: Common Examples and Pitfalls

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


**Interscalene (shoulder)**
- Excellent shoulder coverage. Almost universal hemidiaphragmatic paresis — avoid in significant respiratory disease.
- Hoarseness and Horner's syndrome can occur. Beware intravascular injection near the vertebral artery.

**Supraclavicular (arm/forearm)**
- Dense brachial plexus block. Risk of pneumothorax if too medial or deep — keep the pleura in view throughout.

**Infraclavicular/axillary (distal upper limb)**
- Lower phrenic nerve risk. Multiple cords often need separate local anaesthetic deposition. Don't chase nerves with the tip out of plane — reposition instead.

**Femoral/adductor canal (knee)**
- Adductor canal preserves far more quadriceps power than femoral nerve block.
- Falls risk with any degree of motor block — warn the patient, label the limb, and protect it.

**Sciatic/popliteal (foot/ankle)**
- Excellent distal analgesia. Be aware of vascular proximity; secure the limb for longer needles.

**Ankle block**
- Purely sensory, distal branches only — no tourniquet pain cover.
- Do not use adrenaline in end-artery territories or digits.

**Fascial plane blocks (TAP, rectus sheath, ESP, PECS, serratus)**
- These provide analgesia, not surgical anaesthesia — set expectations with the surgical team.
- Volumes are often large; track cumulative dose carefully.
- Spread is variable and unpredictable.

**Safe defaults while learning:**
- Scout scan before prepping to plan your approach.
- Use in-plane technique.
- Aim for circumferential spread around the nerve ("donut sign") without nerve swelling.

## Intravenous Regional Anaesthesia (IVRA / Bier's Block)

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Local anaesthetic injected intravenously into an exsanguinated, tourniquet-isolated limb.

Key risks: systemic toxicity on tourniquet deflation; tourniquet pain.

- Use a strict checklist. Double-cuff tourniquet, reliable padding, proper exsanguination.
- Use only agents approved for IV use per local policy. Some agents are contraindicated by this route.
- Monitor continuously throughout. Be prepared to manage LAST on deflation.
- Contraindications include severe peripheral vascular disease, sickle cell disease, active limb infection, and inability to tolerate a tourniquet.

## Neuraxial Essentials (Spinal and Epidural)

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


**Physiology you must understand:**
- Sympathetic block causes vasodilation, reduces venous return, and produces hypotension and bradycardia. Treat early with vasopressors and fluids.
- A very high or total spinal compromises ventilation and consciousness. Support the airway and circulation immediately.

**Spinal anaesthesia:**
- Spread is determined by baricity, dose, patient position, and CSF dynamics.
- Allow skin prep to dry. Keep chlorhexidine away from neuraxial needles and drugs. Maintain strict sterility throughout.
- Monitor block level and haemodynamics continuously. Have vasopressors drawn up and ready.
- Post-dural puncture headache (PDPH): postural headache after dural puncture — escalate early. Epidural blood patch per local guidance.

**Epidural:**
- Loss of resistance to saline is the standard UK technique. False loss of resistance can occur in obese or scarred backs.
- Test dose principles aim to detect intrathecal or intravascular catheter placement — follow and interpret per local policy.
- Inject incrementally, aspirate between aliquots, and allow time to assess effect before re-dosing.
- Secure catheters well, label clearly, and maintain asepsis for every access.

**Obstetrics:**
- Engorged epidural veins and reduced CSF volume increase cephalad spread — dose incrementally with heightened vigilance.
- Maintain left uterine displacement, use vasopressors early, and follow continuous fetal and maternal monitoring protocols.

**Anticoagulation and neuraxial:**
- Timing around antiplatelets, LMWH, heparin, warfarin, and DOACs is critical. Follow RA-UK–aligned local guidelines exactly. When uncertain, delay and discuss with a senior.

## Complications and How to Manage Them

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


**Block failure**
Common in early practice. Recognise it early. Offer rescue analgesia, repeat the block only if safe within total dose limits, or convert to GA. Converting early is not failure — delayed conversion is.

**LAST**
- *Prevention:* dose calculation, ultrasound visualisation, frequent aspiration, incremental injection.
- *Recognition:* perioral tingling, tinnitus, agitation, seizures, arrhythmias, hypotension, collapse. CNS features may precede cardiovascular collapse, or cardiovascular collapse may be the first sign.
- *Management:* stop injecting, call for help, secure the airway, give oxygen, control seizures, start lipid emulsion per RA-UK algorithm. Follow Resuscitation Council UK guidance for arrest with lipid therapy.

**Nerve injury**
- Usually neurapraxia — most resolve.
- Never inject against resistance or into a painful needle position.
- Document pre-existing neurological deficits.
- Escalate new, severe, or progressive deficits urgently. Early neurology input and governance reporting.

**Bleeding and haematoma**
- Apply pressure for superficial sites. Deep bleeds (paravertebral, psoas, neuraxial) can be catastrophic. Involve seniors immediately. Anticoagulation timing is your primary preventive tool.

**Infection**
- Rare with single-shot techniques if aseptic. Catheters carry higher risk. Remove a catheter if infection is suspected and start appropriate treatment.

**Pneumothorax**
- Risk with supraclavicular, infraclavicular, and paravertebral blocks. Keep the pleura in view. Manage per BTS/local protocol if suspected.

**Phrenic nerve palsy**
- Anticipate with interscalene and high-volume supraclavicular blocks. Avoid in patients with limited respiratory reserve. Treat supportively and consider an alternative block.

**Falls and injury to insensate limb**
- Educate the patient and ward team. Label the limb clearly. Provide slings or boots as appropriate. Warn about heat sources on numb skin.

**Compartment syndrome**
- Regional analgesia is not contraindicated, but use motor-sparing concentrations where possible and ensure robust monitoring.
- Educate the whole team: escalate disproportionate pain, pain on passive stretch, or tense compartments urgently. Do not dismiss pain as "block wearing off."

## Practical Workflow: A Safe Default

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


1. **Pre-procedure** — indication, anatomy, consent, allergies, anticoagulants, baseline neurology. Calculate and write down the maximum total dose. Set up monitoring, IV access, oxygen, resuscitation drugs, and intralipid.
2. **Scan and plan** — identify target, vessels, pleura, and needle path. Rehearse the trajectory.
3. **Asepsis and time-out** — sterile prep, drape, probe cover, sterile gel. Team brief and side confirmation.
4. **Needle insertion** — in-plane. Advance under continuous vision. Do not move what you cannot see.
5. **Injection** — aspirate frequently. Inject incrementally. Watch spread. Stop if resistance or pain.
6. **Assessment** — test sensory and motor function. Share realistic onset times with the team. Decide early whether to supplement, convert, or proceed.
7. **Handover and follow-up** — written instructions for expected duration, limb protection, red flags, and who to call. For catheters: infusion plan, breakthrough strategy, and daily review.

## High-Yield Tips

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


- See the needle tip or don't move it. If you lose it, stop and find it.
- Angle shallowly — better tip visibility, lower fascicle impalement risk.
- Keep your non-dominant hand still on the probe; move the patient or bed to improve your ergonomics.
- Colour Doppler before every puncture.
- If spread is not what you expect, you are not in the right plane.
- Your safety triad: **ultrasound + low injection pressure + patient feedback**.
- Never let the block delay safe surgery. Convert early rather than late.
