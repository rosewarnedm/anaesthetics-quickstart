## Anatomy of the system: from wall to patient

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Gas travels from **pipelines** (or back-up cylinders) → through the **machine** (regulators, flowmeters, hypoxic guard, vapourisers, common gas outlet) → into the **breathing system** (circle or Mapleson, APL valve, reservoir bag, CO₂ absorbent if circle) → past **monitoring** (oxygen analyser, capnography, agent monitor, airway pressure/volume) → to **scavenging**.

A fault anywhere in this chain can harm the patient. Understand the chain; know where each component sits.

**Four physics principles worth remembering:**
- Small obstructions cause large pressure rises.
- Leaks lose tidal volume and volatile — expect awareness and hypoventilation.
- Rebreathing occurs when CO₂ is not eliminated: exhausted absorbent, incompetent one-way valves, or inadequate fresh gas flow in Mapleson circuits.
- Knob positions and pipeline colours lie. The **oxygen analyser** is the only reliable guardian of inspired oxygen concentration.

## Start-of-day machine check — principles and sequence

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Work through this with your ODP. Speak aloud. If interrupted during a critical step, restart it.

### 1) Power, boot-up, and alarms

- Confirm mains power and adequate battery back-up.
- Run any automated self-test; resolve failures before proceeding.
- Set alarm limits (FiO₂, airway pressure, tidal volume, apnoea) and confirm they are audible.

### 2) Pipelines and cylinders

- Check pipeline connections are secure and correctly labelled; read the gauges.
- Fit an oxygen cylinder, confirm adequate content, then **close it** after testing.
  - Rationale: an open cylinder masks pipeline failure — you will not notice the pipeline has failed until the cylinder runs dry.
- Know your physics: oxygen cylinder pressure falls proportionally with content. Nitrous oxide pressure stays constant until almost empty (liquid–gas equilibrium) — cylinder weight is the only reliable way to gauge content.

### 3) Oxygen monitoring

- Calibrate the oxygen analyser to room air and 100% oxygen at the start of each day.
- It is your last line of defence against a hypoxic mixture. Never bypass or ignore it.

### 4) Vapourisers

- Correct agent, properly mounted, interlock engaged, filler cap closed.
- Use keyed filling only; never overfill.
- If a vapouriser has been tipped, follow the manufacturer's decontamination procedure before use — liquid agent in the delivery pathway causes overdose.
- Only one vapouriser should be deliverable at a time.

### 5) Breathing system integrity

- Assemble the complete circuit — HMEF, connectors, Y-piece, reservoir bag.
- Check the APL valve moves freely; leave it **fully open** (anticlockwise) as your default.
- Leak test: occlude the patient end, pressurise to the recommended level, and confirm the pressure holds.
- Two-bag test (circle): attach a bag at the patient end, compress each bag in turn, and confirm unidirectional flow and valve movement.
- Check the capnography sampling line connects on the patient side of the HMEF; empty the water trap.

### 6) CO₂ absorbent (circle systems)

- Canister seated correctly with gaskets intact.
- Check colour and hardness per manufacturer — replace if exhausted or desiccated.
- Avoid prolonged dry gas flows through an idle machine; desiccated absorbent can produce carbon monoxide, particularly with some volatile agents.

### 7) Ventilator

- Bag/vent selector moves freely; confirm it is in your intended position.
- Run the ventilator self-test with a test lung; verify volumes and pressures are sensible.
- Know your machine's behaviour during oxygen flush — on most circle systems, flushing during mechanical inspiration bypasses the flow-control circuit and can cause volutrauma or dilute volatile delivery. Use the flush with care.

### 8) Scavenging (AGSS)

- Connected, switched on (if active system), reservoir functioning.
- Critical hazard: if scavenging becomes obstructed with the APL closed, circuit pressure will rise dangerously.
- If scavenging fails: minimise theatre pollution with low flows, and escalate for repair.

### 9) Suction and airway rescue

- Suction on, effective, catheter present.
- A **self-inflating bag with an oxygen source** must be immediately available — this is your ultimate rescue device if the machine fails.

### 10) Monitors and gas analysis

- ECG, NIBP, SpO₂, and capnography connected and functioning.
- Confirm capnography produces an expected waveform with a few test breaths before you induce.

## Circuit-specific checks and principles

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


### Circle system (adult)

The circle recycles gas through CO₂ absorbent and unidirectional valves. It supports low-flow anaesthesia — but only when the system is leak-free, absorbent is functional, and gas monitoring is reliable.

**Key checks:**
- Leak test and two-bag test as above.
- Confirm both unidirectional valves move freely — stuck valves cause rebreathing.
- Start with higher fresh gas flows for pre-oxygenation; reduce to low-flow once the system is stable and monitoring confirms safe gas concentrations.

**Common pitfalls:**
- APL left closed during manual ventilation → barotrauma.
- Bag/vent selector left to "vent" during mask induction → no pressure relief.
- Sampling line misconnected → false capnogram, missed hypoventilation.
- Exhausted absorbent or incompetent valves → inspired CO₂ — watch for a rising inspired CO₂ baseline on the capnogram.

### Mapleson systems (A–F) and Bain (coaxial D)

These circuits rely entirely on adequate **fresh gas flow** to wash out CO₂ — there is no absorbent. Flow requirements differ by variant and whether ventilation is spontaneous or controlled. Use your department's recommended flows.

**Key checks:**
- Inspect for kinks, correct assembly, bag integrity, and APL mobility.
- **Bain circuit (coaxial D):** perform the Pethick test (or occlusion test) to confirm the inner fresh-gas limb is patent. A disconnected inner tube massively increases dead space and causes severe rebreathing — perform this test every time.
- **T-piece/Jackson Rees (paediatric):** no APL; gas spills from the open tail. Ensure flows are adequate and the tail is never occluded.

**Hazards:**
- Inadequate flow → rebreathing and hypercapnia. This is an insidious danger; the capnogram is your early warning.
- Adult HMEFs add unacceptable dead space and resistance for small children — use paediatric components.

## Common hazards and how things go wrong

| Hazard | Clue | Root cause |
|---|---|---|
| Hypoxic mixture | Low FiO₂ on analyser, falling SpO₂ | Pipeline crossover, fail-safe limitation |
| Leak | Low volumes, failure to pressurise, light anaesthesia | Loose connections, cracked bag, filler cap |
| Occlusion | High airway pressures, difficult ventilation, rising EtCO₂ | Closed APL, blocked scavenging, kinked circuit, waterlogged HMEF |
| Vapour problem | Awareness or overdose | Wrong agent, tipped vapouriser, back-bar leak |
| Monitoring blind spot | Missed hypoventilation or apnoea | Water trap full, sampling line off, alarms silenced |
| Power/gas loss | Machine failure, hypoxia | Battery flat, mains unplugged, cylinder closed |

## Failure drills you must know

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


### Suspected pipeline oxygen failure or gas crossover

Recognise: falling FiO₂ on analyser, hypoxia despite high oxygen flow, oxygen fail alarm, unexpected odour.

**Immediate actions:**
1. Open the oxygen cylinder.
2. **Disconnect the oxygen pipeline** from the machine — this prevents contaminated gas back-feeding into your circuit.
3. Ventilate with cylinder oxygen via the breathing system or self-inflating bag.
4. Increase FiO₂ to 1.0; confirm on the analyser and SpO₂.
5. Call for help; follow your local major gas failure protocol; inform theatre and engineering.

### Ventilator failure

Switch to manual ventilation with the APL and reservoir bag, or use a self-inflating bag. Maintain oxygen and volatile delivery. If volatile is unavailable, consider TIVA. Get help; do not delay ventilation.

### Scavenging obstruction with rising circuit pressure

Open APL fully. If safe, disconnect the AGSS. Monitor airway pressures; fix the fault promptly.

### Capnography failure

Treat as an equipment problem until proven otherwise. Check the sampling line, water trap, and analyser. If genuinely unavailable, heighten vigilance — auscultation, chest movement, airway pressure, volume, SpO₂, and agent monitoring. Restore capnography as a priority; operating without it is a significant patient safety risk.

## Practical tips and safe defaults

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


- **Start every case:** APL fully open, bag/vent selector in intended position. Say it out loud.
- **Pre-oxygenate** with a well-fitting mask and flows sufficient to prevent rebreathing. Confirm with rising end-tidal oxygen if available.
- **Trust measurements, not markings.** Measured FiO₂ and EtCO₂ tell you what is actually happening.
- **Connect–check–continue.** After any equipment change, re-check before proceeding.
- **Self-inflating bag and oxygen cylinder** must be to hand at all times. Not in the cupboard — to hand.
- **Low-flow anaesthesia** is safe only with a tight system, functional absorbent, and reliable gas monitoring.
- **Oxygen flush** during mechanical inspiration can cause volutrauma and dilute volatile — use it only when you intend to.
- Replace HMEFs and single-use circuits per local infection-control policy.
