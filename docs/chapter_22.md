## Why PACU matters

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Anaesthesia does not end at extubation. The recovery period is when physiology is most labile and complications declare themselves. Your job is a safe handover, vigilant monitoring, early intervention, and a clear plan for ward care.

## Handover: set the team up to succeed

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Use a structured format — SBAR works well. Speak directly to the nurse looking after the patient. Cover:

- **Patient and procedure:** identity, surgeon, allergies, relevant comorbidities (cardiorespiratory disease, OSA, frailty, baseline cognitive function).
- **Airway:** device used, any difficulty encountered (document using DAS terminology), and the plan if the airway fails in PACU.
- **Anaesthetic:** agents, opioids, benzodiazepines, neuromuscular blockers, any intraoperative problems.
- **Regional techniques:** type, dermatomes covered, catheter details, last dose given, ongoing plan.
- **Analgesia and PONV:** what has been given, the postoperative plan, PCA or epidural settings per local protocol.
- **Fluids and physiology:** blood loss, transfusion, lines, drains, urine output.
- **Specific risks:** bleeding sites, airway swelling, tourniquet time, glucose management, positioning injuries.
- **Monitoring and oxygen:** prescribe oxygen with a target range; specify monitoring frequency.
- **Escalation plan:** ward level, outreach triggers, critical care if needed.

Document the handover. Leave written plans and prescriptions before you leave.

## Monitoring standards

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Follow Association of Anaesthetists standards throughout PACU and during transfer:

- Continuous pulse oximetry and ECG; regular non-invasive blood pressure; temperature.
- Capnography for any patient with an airway device in situ, and during transfer.
- Strongly consider capnography for high-risk respiratory depression — opioids, OSA, neuraxial opioids. SpO₂ on supplemental oxygen is a late warning sign; CO₂ rises first.
- Use your hospital's early warning score system.
- Check neuromuscular function objectively wherever possible. Assume residual block risk exists unless documented otherwise.

## Why problems happen: the physiology

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Understanding the mechanism helps you anticipate and recognise problems early.

- **Residual anaesthetics and opioids** reduce airway tone. Supine position and OSA worsen collapse. Residual neuromuscular blockade impairs airway protection and ventilation even in patients who appear awake.
- **Opioids and sedatives** depress ventilatory drive. CO₂ rises before SpO₂ falls — especially on supplemental oxygen.
- **Atelectasis and shunt** reduce oxygenation. Increasing FiO₂ alone may not fix this; adequate analgesia, positioning, and recruitment help restore FRC.
- **Neuraxial blockade** causes sympathectomy — reduced SVR and venous return produce hypotension and bradycardia.
- **Hypothermia** drives shivering, increasing oxygen demand, and impairs coagulation and drug metabolism.
- **Pain, hypoxia, hypercarbia, hypotension, hypoglycaemia, and urinary retention** are all common drivers of agitation and delirium.

## Immediate PACU actions: safe defaults

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


- **Position:** head-up as a starting point. Use lateral/recovery position if the patient is obtunded with an unprotected airway.
- **Oxygen:** start supplemental oxygen for most patients; titrate to a prescribed target. Chronic CO₂ retainers need a lower, clearly prescribed target.
- **Airway adjuncts:** do not hesitate to use oropharyngeal or nasopharyngeal airways. Apply CPAP if trained and indicated.
- **Analgesia:** follow the multimodal plan already in place. Avoid opioid stacking — reassess before redosing.
- **Warmth:** active warming for cold patients; minimise unnecessary exposure.
- **Fluids:** maintain IV access; give fluids according to the clinical picture, not reflexively.

## Common problems and what to do

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


### Airway obstruction

**Clues:** paradoxical see-saw breathing, snoring, rising EtCO₂, falling SpO₂, agitation then somnolence.

**Actions:** airway manoeuvres and adjuncts; head-up positioning; consider CPAP. Treat pain to improve ventilation effort. Check for residual neuromuscular blockade and reverse per objective monitoring and local protocol. Call for help early — do not wait until you are in trouble.

---

### Residual neuromuscular blockade

**Clues:** weak grip, inability to sustain head lift, hypoventilation, airway collapse, diplopia, low TOF ratio.

**Actions:** support the airway and ventilation; give further reversal guided by monitoring and local protocol; consider returning to the theatre environment if the patient is unsafe on the ward.

---

### Hypoventilation and respiratory depression

**Causes:** opioids, benzodiazepines, neuraxial opioids, OSA, residual anaesthetic.

**Actions:** stimulate the patient; support the airway; reduce opioid input (pause PCA); titrate reversal agents per protocol; escalate monitoring to include capnography. If episodes recur, the patient needs a higher level of care.

---

### Laryngospasm, bronchospasm, and post-extubation stridor

**Laryngospasm:** apply continuous positive pressure with 100% oxygen and jaw thrust. If persistent, deepen anaesthesia and follow DAS guidance — a small dose of a fast-acting muscle relaxant may be needed per local protocol. Beware negative-pressure pulmonary oedema as a consequence; treat with PEEP, oxygen, and consider critical care.

**Bronchospasm:** high-flow oxygen, bronchodilators, treat underlying triggers (secretions, aspiration). Consider steroids and magnesium per local policy.

**Post-extubation stridor (airway oedema):** oxygen, nebulised agents per local policy, steroids. Prepare for reintubation; call your senior and ENT early. This is not a situation to manage alone.

---

### Hypoxia after extubation

Think systematically: airway obstruction, atelectasis, aspiration, pulmonary oedema, pneumothorax, PE. Assess airway patency, auscultate, check capnography. Get a CXR and ABG if not resolving. Escalate rapidly if severe or the cause is unclear.

---

### Haemodynamic instability

**Hypotension:**

Common causes — hypovolaemia or bleeding, vasodilation from neuraxial block or GA, cardiogenic impairment, sepsis, anaphylaxis.

Actions: ABC first. Identify the source — look at the surgical site, drains, haemoglobin, and use bedside echo if available. Give fluids judiciously; use vasopressors per protocol if vasodilated. Involve the surgeon early if bleeding is suspected. Follow RCUK guidance if anaphylaxis is possible.

**Hypertension and tachycardia:**

Treat the cause first — pain, agitation, bladder distension, hypoxia, hypercarbia, or withdrawal are common culprits. Antihypertensives are a last resort, not a first response.

---

### Bleeding and transfusion

Look at the patient, dressings, drains, and trend the observations. Correlate with physiology and blood results. Activate your hospital's massive haemorrhage protocol if indicated. Warm the patient and blood products. Monitor for transfusion reactions and follow local and RCUK policy.

---

### Pain management

Apply the multimodal plan: paracetamol, NSAID if appropriate, local anaesthetic techniques, and opioids only as required on top.

- **PCA:** confirm settings match the prescription; educate the patient; only the patient should activate the device.
- **Regional analgesia:** confirm block level and motor function; protect insensate limbs; liaise with the acute pain team for catheter management.
- **Pitfalls:** over-sedation from opioid accumulation; unrecognised neuropathic pain; and crucially — **disproportionate pain or pain on passive stretch should make you think of compartment syndrome. Escalate urgently. Do not give more analgesia and reassess later.**

---

### PONV

Review what prophylaxis has already been given. Use a rescue antiemetic from a different pharmacological class. Consider fluid status and analgesic adjustment. Refractory PONV or suspected ileus: keep nil by mouth, consider a nasogastric tube, escalate.

---

### Temperature and shivering

Measure temperature. Active warming for hypothermia. Shivering markedly increases myocardial oxygen demand — treat with warming and pharmacological measures per local policy. For hyperthermia, consider infection, transfusion reaction, or (rarely but critically) malignant hyperthermia relapse. Escalate promptly.

---

### Delirium and agitation

Exclude and treat hypoxia, hypercarbia, pain, urinary retention, hypoglycaemia, and withdrawal before reaching for sedation. Sedation masks causes and makes reassessment harder — use it only when necessary, in small titrated doses. Older and cognitively impaired patients are high risk; calm, orientating measures early make a real difference.

---

### Glucose and electrolytes

Check glucose in diabetics and the unwell. Treat hypo- and hyperglycaemia per protocol. Correct significant electrolyte derangements — particularly those affecting cardiac rhythm, ventilation, or cognition.

---

### Urinary retention

High risk after neuraxial opioids, pelvic surgery, large fluid volumes, and anticholinergic drugs. Palpate or scan the bladder; have a clear time-bound plan for voiding or catheterisation. Untreated retention drives agitation, hypertension, and PONV.

---

### Regional and neuraxial anaesthesia in PACU

**Spinal and epidural:**
- Monitor block level and haemodynamics actively.
- High block → hypotension, bradycardia, dyspnoea — treat with fluids, vasopressors, airway support, and call for help.
- Do not discharge from PACU until safe motor function criteria are met.
- Neuraxial opioids carry a risk of late respiratory depression. Specify monitoring frequency and escalation plan clearly on the drug chart.

**Peripheral nerve blocks:**
- Communicate expected motor and sensory loss to the ward; protect the insensate limb; document fall risk.
- Interscalene and supraclavicular blocks cause ipsilateral diaphragmatic paresis — use caution in patients with limited respiratory reserve and escalate if they become breathless.
- **LAST (local anaesthetic systemic toxicity)** is rare but potentially fatal. Recognise early CNS features (agitation, perioral tingling, seizures) and cardiac features (arrhythmia, arrest). Start lipid emulsion therapy per your local protocol and call the emergency response immediately.

---

### Chest pain and arrhythmias

Treat chest pain as ACS until proven otherwise: oxygen only if saturations are low, adequate analgesia, 12-lead ECG, senior input promptly.

Manage arrhythmias per RCUK ALS guidance. Correct reversible causes first: potassium, magnesium, hypoxia, pain, and acid-base disturbance.

## Discharge from PACU: principles

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Use your hospital's local criteria aligned with national standards. In general, the patient should have:

- Conscious level at or near baseline with adequate protective reflexes.
- Stable airway and ventilation with acceptable saturations on prescribed oxygen.
- Cardiovascular stability without ongoing active resuscitation.
- Temperature trending towards normal; shivering controlled.
- Pain controlled on the planned regimen; PONV controlled.
- Surgical site satisfactory; drains functioning; no concerns about bleeding.
- For regional/neuraxial: block level appropriate, safe motor function for destination, monitoring plan in place.
- Clear prescriptions: oxygen target, analgesia (including breakthrough), antiemetics, fluids, VTE prophylaxis, glucose management.
- Observation frequency and escalation triggers documented. DNACPR status and ceilings of care clearly communicated.

If criteria are not met or you have concerns, keep the patient in PACU, involve the anaesthetist and surgeon, and consider a higher level of care. Do not discharge to relieve pressure on the PACU.

## Special groups

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


**OSA and obesity:** high risk of obstruction and opioid sensitivity. Multimodal and opioid-sparing analgesia. Semi-upright or lateral positioning. Consider extended PACU stay, capnography, and early CPAP if they use it at home. A low threshold for higher-level care.

**Frail and older patients:** aim for normothermia, normoxia, and adequate analgesia with minimal sedation. Return glasses and hearing aids early. Screen actively for delirium.

**Neuraxial opioids and epidurals:** follow your Trust's monitoring policy for sedation scoring, respiratory rate, and block level. An explicit escalation plan for respiratory depression must be on the chart before the patient leaves PACU.

**Day surgery:** confirm a responsible adult escort, safe transport home, written discharge instructions, a 24-hour contact number, and clear red-flag symptoms. Avoid long-acting sedatives and opioids. Ensure PONV is controlled and voiding has been addressed per local policy.

## Pitfalls to know

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


- **Inadequate reversal of NMB** → airway disaster. Trust objective monitoring over clinical impression.
- **Supplemental oxygen masks hypoventilation.** Capnography reveals it earlier; use it.
- **Opioid stacking** when the real problem is movement or surgical pain. Fix the cause.
- **Missing bleeding** because the patient looks well on oxygen under blankets. Look under dressings; trend the observations.
- **Hypothermia and shivering** dramatically increase myocardial oxygen demand.
- **Urinary retention** drives agitation, hypertension, and PONV — and is easily missed.
- **Failing to prescribe oxygen and monitoring** means the ward will omit them.
- **Poor handover** → missed risks such as a difficult airway, neck surgery, or a high neuraxial block.

## When to escalate

> _This is a project on AI content generation. It is **not a clinical reference** and must not be relied on for clinical decisions._


Call for help — your registrar, consultant on call, or the relevant surgical team — for:

- Actual or threatened airway compromise; persistent hypoxia or hypercapnia.
- Refractory hypotension or hypertension; chest pain; new neurological deficit.
- Ongoing bleeding or rapidly filling drains; expanding neck or wound haematoma.
- Recurrent apnoeas or sedation despite stopping opioids.
- High or rising neuraxial block; suspected LAST; seizures.
- Severe agitation or delirium without a simple explanation.
- Any patient you would not be comfortable discharging to a standard ward bay.

When in doubt, keep the patient, get senior input, and document your reasoning.
